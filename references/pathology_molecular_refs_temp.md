## 1. WHO 2022 (5th Edition) Thyroid Tumor Classification

### 1a. Overview of the 2022 WHO Classification of Thyroid Neoplasms

- **PMID**: 35288841
- **Citation**: Baloch ZW, Asa SL, Barletta JA, Ghossein RA, Juhlin CC, Jung CK, LiVolsi VA, Papotti MG, Sobrinho-Simoes M, Tallini G, Mete O. Endocr Pathol. 2022;33(1):27-63.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s12022-022-09707-3](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12022-022-09707-3)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive summary of the WHO 5th edition changes: follicular cell-derived tumors divided into benign, low-risk (NIFTP, tumors of uncertain malignant potential), and malignant; PTC subtypes stratified as BRAF-like vs RAS-like; new high-grade DTC category introduced alongside PDTC; oncocytic carcinoma recognized as a distinct entity; new grading system for MTC.
- **Supports recommendation**: Pathology classification changes, NIFTP nomenclature, molecular subtype-based classification, high-grade DTC category
- **Full text available**: No (subscription journal, Endocrine Pathology)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 1b. WHO 5th Edition -- Changes in Follicular-Derived Thyroid Carcinoma Diagnosis

- **PMID**: 36964880
- **Citation**: Basolo F, Macerola E, Poma AM, Torregrossa L. Endocrine. 2023;80(3):470-476.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s12020-023-03336-4](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12020-023-03336-4)
- **Key finding**: Reviews the practical effects of WHO 5th edition on diagnostic pathology: tumor types sharply classified by prognostic risk categories (benign, low-risk, malignant), new grading system for DTC implemented, and particular attention paid to molecular profiles of well-differentiated histotypes.
- **Supports recommendation**: Pathology reporting standardization, risk stratification in DTC, integration of molecular classification
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC9199828, open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 1c. NIFTP Reclassification -- Landmark Paper

- **PMID**: 27078145
- **Citation**: Nikiforov YE, Seethala RR, Tallini G, Baloch ZW, Basolo F, Thompson LDR, Barletta JA, Wenig BM, et al. JAMA Oncol. 2016;2(8):1023-9.
- **DOI**: [10.1001/jamaoncol.2016.0386](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2016.0386)
- **Key finding**: International multicenter study of 109 patients with noninvasive EFVPTC followed 10-26 years: ALL were alive with no evidence of disease (0% adverse outcomes). Led to reclassification from "cancer" to "noninvasive follicular thyroid neoplasm with papillary-like nuclear features" (NIFTP), reducing overtreatment of indolent tumors.
- **Supports recommendation**: NIFTP nomenclature adoption, de-escalation of treatment for noninvasive EFVPTC, reduced psychological burden of cancer diagnosis
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC5539411)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 2. BRAF V600E Prognostic Studies

### 2a. BRAF V600E and Mortality in PTC -- Xing et al. 2013 (Landmark)

- **PMID**: 23571588
- **Citation**: Xing M, Alzahrani AS, Carson KA, Viola D, Elisei R, Bendlova B, Yip L, Mian C, et al. JAMA. 2013;309(14):1493-501.
- **DOI**: [10.1001/jama.2013.3190](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2013.3190)
- **Key finding**: In 1849 patients from 13 centers in 7 countries, BRAF V600E was significantly associated with increased cancer-related mortality (5.3% vs 1.1%, P<.001; HR 2.66 after adjustment). However, the association was not independent of tumor features (HR 1.21 when adjusted for LNM, ETE, distant metastasis), suggesting BRAF drives aggressive pathology that mediates mortality risk.
- **Supports recommendation**: BRAF V600E as a prognostic molecular marker, integration of molecular data into risk stratification, potential for BRAF-guided management
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC3791140)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 2b. BRAF V600E and Recurrence of PTC -- Xing et al. 2015

- **PMID**: 25332244
- **Citation**: Xing M, Liu R, Liu X, Murugan AK, Zhu G, Zeiger MA, Pai S, Bishop J. J Clin Oncol. 2015;33(1):42-50.
- **DOI**: [10.1200/JCO.2014.56.8253](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.2014.56.8253)
- **Key finding**: In 2099 patients from a multicenter study, BRAF V600E mutation was independently associated with PTC recurrence (HR 1.82, 95% CI 1.46-2.28) after adjusting for clinicopathologic factors including sex, age, medical center, and conventional pathologic variables. Association was significant even in low-risk stage I/II and micro-PTC.
- **Supports recommendation**: BRAF V600E as independent predictor of recurrence, value in risk stratification for low-risk patients, informs surveillance intensity decisions
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC4268252)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 2c. BRAF V600E Differentiates LNM-Associated Mortality Risk -- Tao/Xing et al. 2021

- **PMID**: 34273152
- **Citation**: Tao Y, Wang F, Shen X, Zhu G, Liu R, Viola D, Elisei R, Puxeddu E, Fugazzola L, et al.; Xing M. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2021;106(11):3228-3238.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/clinem/dgab286](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab286)
- **Key finding**: In 2638 patients from 11 centers in 6 countries, LNM-associated mortality risk was sharply differentiated by BRAF status: in conventional PTC with wild-type BRAF, LNM showed NO increased mortality risk, but with BRAF V600E, LNM conferred robust mortality increase (adjusted HR 5.76). Coexisting LNM + BRAF V600E had adjusted mortality HR of 27.39 vs absence of both.
- **Supports recommendation**: BRAF-guided interpretation of LNM prognostic significance, synergistic risk from BRAF + LNM, molecular-informed surgical/treatment decision-making
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC8530728)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 2d. TCGA Integrated Genomic Characterization of PTC

- **PMID**: 25417114
- **Citation**: Cancer Genome Atlas Research Network. Cell. 2014;159(3):676-90.
- **DOI**: [10.1016/j.cell.2014.09.050](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2014.09.050)
- **Key finding**: Genomic landscape of 496 PTCs: low somatic alteration frequency; expanded known PTC drivers (EIF1AX, PPM1D, CHEK2, diverse fusions) reducing unknown driver cases from 25% to 3.5%. Combined analyses demonstrated BRAF-like vs RAS-like molecular subtypes with distinct signaling, differentiation, and pathology -- proposed molecular reclassification of thyroid cancers into subtypes reflecting underlying biology.
- **Supports recommendation**: BRAF-like vs RAS-like molecular classification framework, molecular subtyping for prognosis, foundation for molecular-guided treatment strategies
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC4243044)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 3. Molecular Testing Validation Studies

### 3a. ThyroSeq v3 Analytical Performance Validation

- **PMID**: 29345728
- **Citation**: Nikiforova MN, Mercurio S, Wald AI, Barbi de Moura M, Callenberg K, Santana-Santos L, Gooding WE, Yip L, Ferris RL, Nikiforov YE. Cancer. 2018;124(8):1682-1690.
- **DOI**: [10.1002/cncr.31245](https://doi.org/10.1002/cncr.31245)
- **Key finding**: ThyroSeq v3 is a DNA- and RNA-based NGS assay analyzing 112 genes for point mutations, indels, fusions, CNAs, and gene expression. In validation: sensitivity 98.0%, specificity 81.8%, accuracy 90.9% in FNA samples. Successfully classified PTC, FTC, Hurthle cell, MTC, and parathyroid lesions.
- **Supports recommendation**: Molecular testing as adjunct to cytology for indeterminate nodules, expanded mutation panel utility, multi-alteration assessment
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC5891361)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 3b. ThyroSeq v3 Clinical Validation -- Multicenter

- **PMID**: 30811896
- **Citation**: Nikiforov YE, Baloch ZW. Cancer Cytopathol. 2019;127(4):225-230.
- **DOI**: [10.1002/cncy.22112](https://doi.org/10.1002/cncy.22112)
- **Key finding**: Review of the prospective, double-blinded, multicenter clinical validation of ThyroSeq v3 in thyroid nodules with indeterminate FNA cytology. Demonstrated clinical utility in resolving indeterminate cytology uncertainty and reducing unnecessary diagnostic surgeries.
- **Supports recommendation**: Clinical adoption of ThyroSeq v3 for Bethesda III/IV nodules, surgery avoidance for molecularly benign nodules
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6519348)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 3c. Afirma GSC Real-World Performance -- Meta-analysis

- **PMID**: 36470585
- **Citation**: Nasr CE, Andrioli M, Endo M, Harrell RM, Livhits MJ, Osakwe I, Polavarapu P, Siperstein A, Wei S, et al.; Angell TE. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2023;108(6):1526-1532.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/clinem/dgac688](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgac688)
- **Key finding**: Meta-analysis of 13 independent real-world post-validation studies of Afirma GSC: real-world sensitivity 97%, observed specificity 88%, observed PPV 65%, NPV 99% -- all significantly better than the original validation study (91%/68%/47%/96%). Higher benign call rate (67% vs 54%) suggests increased avoidance of surgery.
- **Supports recommendation**: Afirma GSC validation for clinical use, rule-out test for indeterminate nodules, demonstrates improved real-world performance over validation data
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10188305)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 3d. Afirma GSC Analytical Verification

- **PMID**: 31333584
- **Citation**: Hao Y, Choi Y, Babiarz JE, Kloos RT, Kennedy GC, Huang J, Walsh PS. Front Endocrinol (Lausanne). 2019;10:438.
- **DOI**: [10.3389/fendo.2019.00438](https://doi.org/10.3389/fendo.2019.00438)
- **Key finding**: Afirma GSC tolerates RNA input variation (5-30 ng), up to 75% dilution with malignant FNA material, up to 75% blood and 30% genomic DNA contamination. Highly reproducible across operators, runs, reagent lots, and laboratories.
- **Supports recommendation**: Analytical robustness of Afirma GSC for routine clinical use, pre-analytical variability tolerance
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6620518, open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 3e. Afirma Xpression Atlas -- Variant and Fusion Detection

- **PMID**: 31572297
- **Citation**: Angell TE, Wirth LJ, Cabanillas ME, Shindo ML, Cibas ES, Babiarz JE, Hao Y, et al.; Waguespack SG. Front Endocrinol (Lausanne). 2019;10:612.
- **DOI**: [10.3389/fendo.2019.00612](https://doi.org/10.3389/fendo.2019.00612)
- **Key finding**: Afirma Xpression Atlas detects gene variants and fusions from whole-transcriptome RNA-seq across 511 genes. Analytical validation showed high reproducibility (89-94% intra-plate, 86-91% inter-plate, 90% inter-lab). Identifies actionable variants including those with approved or investigational targeted therapies.
- **Supports recommendation**: Combined rule-out (GSC) + rule-in (XA) molecular testing approach, precision medicine for thyroid nodules, actionable mutation identification
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6749016, open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 4. Gene Fusion Studies (RET, NTRK) and Targeted Therapy

### 4a. Selpercatinib in RET-Altered Thyroid Cancers (LIBRETTO-001)

- **PMID**: 32846061
- **Citation**: Wirth LJ, Sherman E, Robinson B, Solomon B, Kang H, Lorch J, Worden F, Brose M, et al. N Engl J Med. 2020;383(9):825-835.
- **DOI**: [10.1056/NEJMoa2005651](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2005651)
- **Key finding**: LIBRETTO-001 phase 1/2 trial: selpercatinib demonstrated robust efficacy in RET-altered thyroid cancers. In RET-mutant MTC previously treated with cabozantinib/vandetanib, ORR was 69%; in treatment-naive RET-mutant MTC, ORR was 73%; in RET-fusion positive thyroid cancer, ORR was 79%. Durable responses with manageable safety profile.
- **Supports recommendation**: Selpercatinib as first targeted therapy for RET-altered thyroid cancer, RET testing in thyroid cancer, molecular-guided treatment selection
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10777663)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 4b. Selpercatinib First Approval -- Drug Profile

- **PMID**: 32557397
- **Citation**: Markham A. Drugs. 2020;80(11):1119-1124.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s40265-020-01343-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40265-020-01343-7)
- **Key finding**: FDA approval of selpercatinib (RETEVMO) based on LIBRETTO-001 for RET-mutant MTC requiring systemic therapy, RET fusion-positive thyroid cancer requiring systemic therapy and refractory to RAI, and RET fusion-positive NSCLC. First selectively targeted RET inhibitor approved.
- **Supports recommendation**: Regulatory milestone supporting RET-targeted therapy, molecular testing to identify eligible patients
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC7716849)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 4c. Selpercatinib Tumor-Agnostic Efficacy in RET Fusion-Positive Solid Tumors (LIBRETTO-001 Basket)

- **PMID**: 36108661
- **Citation**: Subbiah V, Wolf J, Konda B, Kang H, Spira A, Weiss J, Takeda M, et al. Lancet Oncol. 2022;23(10):1261-1273.
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S1470-2045(22)00541-1](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(22)00541-1)
- **Key finding**: Tumor-agnostic efficacy of selpercatinib in RET fusion-positive solid tumors beyond lung/thyroid (basket trial component of LIBRETTO-001). Demonstrates broad activity across tumor types harboring RET fusions, supporting tumor-agnostic approval approach.
- **Supports recommendation**: Tumor-agnostic testing for RET fusions, expanding indication for selpercatinib, comprehensive genomic profiling
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC11702314)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 4d. Pralsetinib in RET-Altered Thyroid Cancer (ARROW Trial)

- **PMID**: 34118198
- **Citation**: Subbiah V, Hu MI, Wirth LJ, Schuler M, Mansfield AS, Curigliano G, Brose MS, Zhu VW, Leboulleux S, et al.; Taylor MH. Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol. 2021;9(8):491-501.
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S2213-8587(21)00120-0](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587(21)00120-0)
- **Key finding**: ARROW phase 1/2 trial: pralsetinib 400 mg once daily showed ORR of 71% (95% CI 48-89) in treatment-naive RET-mutant MTC, 60% (95% CI 46-73) in previously treated RET-mutant MTC, and 89% (95% CI 52-100) in RET fusion-positive thyroid cancer. Well tolerated with manageable AE profile (hypertension 17%, neutropenia 13%).
- **Supports recommendation**: Pralsetinib as additional RET-targeted option, confirmation of RET inhibitor class efficacy in thyroid cancer, RET testing recommendations
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 4e. NTRK Fusion Detection Across Multiple Assays (33,997 Cases)

- **PMID**: 31375766
- **Citation**: Solomon JP, Linkov I, Rosado A, Mullaney K, Rosen EY, Frosina D, Jungbluth AA, Zehir A, Benayed R, Drilon A, Hyman DM, Ladanyi M, Sireci AN, Hechtman JF. Mod Pathol. 2020;33(1):38-46.
- **DOI**: [10.1038/s41379-019-0324-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41379-019-0324-7)
- **Key finding**: In the largest NTRK fusion-positive cohort to date (33,997 patients, 87 with oncogenic NTRK1-3 fusions): DNA-based NGS sensitivity 81.1%/specificity 99.9% vs RNA-based sequencing. Pan-Trk IHC sensitivity 87.9%/specificity 81.1%. IHC specificity was 100% for thyroid carcinomas. RNA-based sequencing is the gold standard; assay selection should depend on tumor type.
- **Supports recommendation**: NTRK fusion testing strategies, IHC as screening in thyroid cancer, RNA-based NGS as confirmatory, tiered testing approach
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC7437403)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 4f. Larotrectinib Efficacy in TRK Fusion-Positive Cancers (Landmark)

- **PMID**: 29466156
- **Citation**: Drilon A, Laetsch TW, Kummar S, DuBois SG, Lassen UN, Demetri GD, Nathenson M, Doebele RC, et al. N Engl J Med. 2018;378(8):731-739.
- **DOI**: [10.1056/NEJMoa1714448](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1714448)
- **Key finding**: Landmark basket trial of larotrectinib in TRK fusion-positive cancers across 17 tumor types (adults and children): ORR 75% (95% CI 61-85), with responses in 13/17 tumor types including thyroid cancer. Led to first tumor-agnostic approval based on a specific molecular alteration. Established paradigm for biomarker-driven cancer therapy.
- **Supports recommendation**: NTRK fusion testing in advanced thyroid cancer, tumor-agnostic treatment paradigm, larotrectinib as standard option for NTRK-fusion positive cancers
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC5857389)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

### 4g. Larotrectinib Redifferentiation Effect in NTRK-Rearranged Thyroid Cancer

- **PMID**: 35171708
- **Citation**: Groussin L, Theodon H, Bessiene L, Bricaire L, Bonnet-Serrano F, Cochand-Priollet B, et al.; Huillard O. Thyroid. 2022;32(5):594-598.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2021.0524](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2021.0524)
- **Key finding**: In 3 consecutive patients with metastatic RAI-resistant NTRK-rearranged thyroid cancer treated with larotrectinib, 2/3 showed RAI reuptake (redifferentiation), demonstrating that TRK inhibition can reverse dedifferentiation in NTRK-fusion thyroid cancers, potentially enabling combination with RAI therapy.
- **Supports recommendation**: Redifferentiation strategy using targeted inhibitors, combined larotrectinib + RAI approach, expanding treatment paradigm for NTRK-fusion RAI-refractory thyroid cancer
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, Thyroid journal)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 4h. TRK Inhibitors in TRK Fusion-Positive Cancers -- Review (Drilon)

- **PMID**: 31738426
- **Citation**: Drilon A. Ann Oncol. 2019;30(Suppl_8):viii23-viii30.
- **DOI**: [10.1093/annonc/mdz282](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mdz282)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive review of first-generation TRK inhibitors (larotrectinib, entrectinib): brisk and durable responses in TRK fusion-positive cancers including thyroid cancer, intracranial activity, and next-generation agents (selitrectinib, repotrectinib) for acquired resistance. Parallels ALK fusion-positive lung cancer treatment paradigm with sequential TRK inhibitor use.
- **Supports recommendation**: Sequential TRK inhibitor strategy, monitoring for resistance, next-generation TRK inhibitors in development
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6859818)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 5. Extrathyroidal Extension (ETE) Prognostic Studies

### 5a. Extrathyroidal Extension -- What Does It Mean? (Shaha, MSKCC)

- **PMID**: 28438293
- **Citation**: Shaha AR. Oral Oncol. 2017;68:50-52.
- **DOI**: [10.1016/j.oraloncology.2017.03.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.oraloncology.2017.03.008)
- **Key finding**: Critical review demonstrating that microscopic/minimal ETE has NO prognostic implication in well-differentiated thyroid cancer, despite previous staging systems upstaging such tumors. Supports the rationale for removing microscopic ETE from T-staging.
- **Supports recommendation**: Removal of microscopic ETE from T3 classification, AJCC 8th edition T-staging changes, de-escalation of treatment for microscopic ETE
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, Oral Oncology)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 5b. Gross ETE Invading Only Strap Muscles -- Prognostic Significance (Park et al.)

- **PMID**: 29663333
- **Citation**: Park SY, Kim HI, Kim J-H, Kim JS, Oh YL, Kim SW, Chung JH, Jang HW, Kim TH. Br J Surg. 2018;105(9):1155-1162.
- **DOI**: [10.1002/bjs.10830](https://doi.org/10.1002/bjs.10830)
- **Key finding**: In 3174 DTC patients: 10-year CSS was NOT significantly different among no ETE (98.6%), microscopic ETE (98.3%), and gross ETE invading only strap muscles (98.9%). On multivariate analysis, gross ETE invading only strap muscles was NOT an independent predictor of recurrence (HR 1.09, P=0.685). Supports the AJCC 8th edition's new T3b designation.
- **Supports recommendation**: AJCC 8th edition T3b category for strap muscle-only gross ETE, distinction from gross ETE into major structures, ETE subclassification in staging
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, British Journal of Surgery)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 5c. AJCC 8th Edition T Staging Analysis for PTC -- Microscopic ETE Impact (Tran et al.)

- **PMID**: 29672723
- **Citation**: Tran B, Roshan D, Abraham E, Wang L, Garibotto N, Wykes J, Campbell P, Ebrahimi A. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2018;103(6):2199-2206.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/jc.2017-02551](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2017-02551)
- **Key finding**: Compared AJCC 7th vs 8th edition T staging in 577 PTC patients. In patients >= 55 years without macroscopic ETE or distant metastases, pT8 was actually INFERIOR to pT7 (lower C-index 0.67 vs 0.76). Microscopic ETE was strongly associated with other adverse prognostic factors and reduced DFS (HR 2.8, P=0.002) irrespective of tumor size -- suggesting microscopic ETE may be an effective surrogate for aggressive disease biology even if not independent.
- **Supports recommendation**: Nuanced interpretation of microscopic ETE removal from staging, microscopic ETE as a marker of aggressive biology, potential need for refinement in future staging editions
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, JCEM)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 5d. Microscopic Positive Margins and ETE in DTC (Wang/Ganly, MSKCC)

- **PMID**: 26122000
- **Citation**: Wang LY, Ghossein R, Palmer FL, Nixon IJ, Tuttle RM, Shaha AR, Shah JP, Patel SG, Ganly I. Thyroid. 2015;25(9):993-8.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2015.0141](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2015.0141)
- **Key finding**: In 2616 DTC patients with total thyroidectomy, 5-year LRFS was excellent (99.4%). Microscopic positive margin was NOT an independent predictor of local failure after adjusting for gross ETE (P=0.193). Gross ETE was the dominant prognostic factor, further supporting the distinction between microscopic and gross ETE.
- **Supports recommendation**: Gross ETE as the critical prognostic factor over microscopic margins/ETE, conservative management of microscopic positive margins, supports ETE reclassification
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC4968280)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 6. Vascular Invasion Extent Studies

### 6a. Prognostic Impact of Extent of Vascular Invasion in FTC (Leong et al.)

- **PMID**: 36031639
- **Citation**: Leong D, Gill AJ, Turchini J, Waller M, Clifton-Bligh R, Glover A, Sywak M, Sidhu S. World J Surg. 2022;47(2):412-420.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00268-022-06696-6](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00268-022-06696-6)
- **Key finding**: In 292 FTC cases (139 MIFTC, 141 EAFTC, 12 WIFTC): risk of metastasis in EAFTC with >= 4 foci of angioinvasion was 31.7% (HR 5.89, P=0.004) vs 6.3% for <4 foci (HR 1.74, P=0.47) vs 3.6% for MIFTC. DSS was 96.8% for <4 foci and 82.6% for >= 4 foci (P=0.003). The 4-foci threshold is a critical prognostic cutpoint.
- **Supports recommendation**: >= 4 foci of vascular invasion as threshold for aggressive behavior in FTC, total thyroidectomy consideration for >= 4 foci, risk-stratified management of EAFTC
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, World J Surg)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 6b. Vascular Invasion in Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma -- Kuma Hospital (Ito et al.)

- **PMID**: 35491160
- **Citation**: Ito Y, Hirokawa M, Masuoka H, Higashiyama T, Kihara M, Onoda N, Miya A, Miyauchi A. Endocr J. 2022;69(9):1149-1156.
- **DOI**: [10.1507/endocrj.EJ22-0077](https://doi.org/10.1507/endocrj.EJ22-0077)
- **Key finding**: In 523 FTC patients, DR-FS rates significantly decreased step-by-step from VI(-) to VI(1+) to VI(2+), including VI(-) vs VI(1+) (P=0.011) and VI(1+) vs VI(2+) (P=0.014). Multivariate analysis confirmed VI degree as an independent predictor of distant recurrence. Authors suggest subclassifying EAFTC by degree of VI and restricting WIFTC definition to VI-positive cases.
- **Supports recommendation**: Graded VI assessment in FTC reporting, subclassification of EAFTC by VI foci count, refined WHO classification of FTC subtypes
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, Endocrine Journal; though likely open access from Japan)
- **Data source**: Abstract

### 6c. Vascular Invasion in Encapsulated Hurthle Cell Carcinoma -- >= 4 Foci (Ghossein, MSKCC, Landmark)

- **PMID**: 16534796
- **Citation**: Ghossein RA, Hiltzik DH, Carlson DL, Patel S, Shaha A, Shah JP, Tuttle RM, Singh B. Cancer. 2006;106(8):1669-76.
- **DOI**: [10.1002/cncr.21825](https://doi.org/10.1002/cncr.21825)
- **Key finding**: In 50 encapsulated Hurthle cell carcinomas: ALL 7 patients who developed recurrence had >= 4 foci of vascular invasion (P<.0001). This is the seminal study establishing the >= 4 foci threshold. Solid/trabecular growth and mitosis correlated with numerous vascular invasion foci. Diligent search for vascular invasion recommended.
- **Supports recommendation**: Origin of the >= 4 vascular invasion foci threshold, comprehensive pathologic assessment of vascular invasion, risk stratification in encapsulated oncocytic carcinoma
- **Full text available**: No (subscription, Cancer journal)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## Notes for Guideline Comparison Project

### Key Themes for ATA 2015 vs 2025 Changes:

1. **Pathology classification**: WHO 5th edition fundamentally reorganizes thyroid tumors with molecular-based classification (BRAF-like vs RAS-like), introduces low-risk neoplasm category (NIFTP), and adds high-grade DTC -- all developed after ATA 2015 publication.

2. **Molecular markers for prognosis**: The Xing et al. series (2013-2021) establishes BRAF V600E as a robust prognostic marker, while TCGA data provides the framework for molecular subtyping that guides risk assessment.

3. **Molecular testing evolution**: Both ThyroSeq v3 and Afirma GSC represent major advances since ATA 2015, which referenced earlier versions (ThyroSeq v2, Afirma GEC). Performance has improved substantially, with higher specificity and benign call rates.

4. **Targeted therapy revolution**: Selpercatinib (LIBRETTO-001), pralsetinib (ARROW), and larotrectinib for RET/NTRK fusion-positive thyroid cancers were all developed post-2015 -- creating entirely new treatment paradigms not addressed in ATA 2015.

5. **ETE reclassification**: Multiple studies confirm microscopic ETE has limited independent prognostic impact, supporting the AJCC 8th edition change (2018) that removed it from T3 staging -- a post-2015 development.

6. **Vascular invasion grading**: The >= 4 foci threshold, established by Ghossein 2006 and validated by subsequent studies, is now integrated into WHO 2022 FTC classification -- relevant for updated surgical/RAI recommendations.
