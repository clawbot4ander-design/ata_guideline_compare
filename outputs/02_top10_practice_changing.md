# Top 10 Practice-Changing Differences: ATA 2015 vs 2025 DTC Guidelines

**Date:** 2026-03-10 | **Version:** 1.1 (with cross-validation corrections)

---

## How to Read This Document

Each of the 10 changes includes:
- **What changed** -- exact recommendation text with citations
- **Why it changed** -- evidence and rationale from the guideline
- **Who is affected** -- specific patient groups
- **What to do differently** -- concrete Monday morning actions
- **Cross-validation notes** -- corrections and nuances identified through independent verification

Changes are ranked by combined clinical impact (volume of patients affected x magnitude of practice change).

## 1. Lobectomy Expansion to <=2 cm as Strong Default

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R35C: Lobectomy for tumors <1 cm, cN0, no ETE | R15A: Lobectomy for tumors <=2 cm (cT1N0M0) |
| **Strength** | Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence | Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence |
| **Key wording** | "should be a thyroid lobectomy unless there are clear indications to remove the contralateral lobe" | "should be a thyroid lobectomy unless there are bilateral cancers or other indications to remove the contralateral lobe" |
| **Size threshold** | <1 cm | <=2 cm |
| **Source** | Section B7, p. 32 | p. 862 |

The strong lobectomy default has doubled in size range. Tumors 1-2 cm, which were previously in an equipoise zone (2015 R35B: "can be either"), are now under a strong lobectomy recommendation.

### Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline provides explicit rationale (p. 862, R15 discussion):

1. **No survival difference for 1-4 cm tumors.** The Adam et al. updated NCDB analysis (61,775 patients) demonstrated that the survival advantage for total thyroidectomy in 1-4 cm PTC "disappeared when further adjustment was made for additional variables related to complexity and severity of illness." Multiple SEER analyses (Barney et al., Mendelsohn et al.) confirmed no survival difference. A single-center study (Matsuzu et al.) showed 97% cause-specific survival at median 17 years after lobectomy.

2. **Halved complication rate.** Even for high-volume surgeons, complication rates are 7.6% for lobectomy vs 14.5% for total thyroidectomy. This morbidity differential, combined with survival equivalence, tips the balance.

3. **Reduced need for RAI.** The ESTIMABL2 RCT eliminated the primary historical justification for total thyroidectomy (enabling RAI) in low-risk patients.

4. **AJCC 8th edition changes.** Removal of microscopic ETE from T3 staging and age cutoff change from 45 to 55 years downstaged 20-30% of patients.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (post-2015 database reanalyses) + Reinterpretation (same data, survival equivalence now prioritized over recurrence reduction) + De-escalation (morbidity avoidance).

### Who Is Affected

Patients with unilateral, intrathyroidal DTC between 1 and 2 cm (cT1bN0M0). This is a large proportion of newly diagnosed DTC, as the majority present with T1 disease. This single change affects the largest number of patients of any recommendation in the guideline.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Surgeons:** Default to lobectomy for all cT1N0M0 tumors (up to 2 cm). Reserve total thyroidectomy for bilateral disease, prior radiation, familial DTC, or patient preference.
2. **Tumor board:** Advocate for lobectomy in 1-2 cm cN0 tumors. Total thyroidectomy in this size range now requires specific justification.
3. **Patient counseling:** Explain that lobectomy has half the complication rate with equivalent survival. Discuss the ~20% possibility of needing completion thyroidectomy if final pathology reveals unexpected high-risk features.
4. **Downstream implications:** More lobectomy patients means fewer patients needing lifelong thyroid hormone, fewer candidates for RAI, and a growing population where Tg monitoring is unreliable.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Verified accurate.** The threshold expansion from <1 cm to <=2 cm is unambiguous in the guideline text. Impact rating: HIGH (confirmed).
- **Multifocality caveat.** The 2025 guideline explicitly notes that bilateral multifocality with clinically significant contralateral nodularity may make total thyroidectomy preferred, even for T1 tumors. Preoperative contralateral nodularity is present in 30-50% of patients, modifying the practical impact.
- **FTC-specific data.** The 2025 guideline separately cites a SEER study for FTC (without ETE/metastasis) showing no difference in 15-year disease-specific survival between lobectomy and total thyroidectomy (98% vs 97%).
- **Completion thyroidectomy rates.** One study found that doing more lobectomies after the 2015 guideline did NOT increase completion thyroidectomy rates (fell from 74% to 20%), countering the fear that more lobectomies leads to more second surgeries.

## 2. RAI for Low-Risk DTC: Strong/High Evidence AGAINST

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R51A: RAI not routinely recommended for ATA low-risk | R32A: RAI not recommended routinely for low-risk |
| **Strength** | Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence | Strong recommendation, High certainty evidence |
| **Sub-category** | R51B: Not recommended for unifocal microPTC (Strong/Moderate) | Subsumed into R32A |
| **Source** | p. 55, Table 14 | p. 884 |

This is the largest evidence-quality jump in the entire guideline: from Weak/Low to Strong/High. The upgrade from Low to High quality reflects the first Phase III RCT data in thyroid cancer RAI decision-making.

### Why It Changed

1. **ESTIMABL2 Phase III RCT (776 patients).** Low-risk DTC patients randomized to follow-up without RAI vs ablation. Event-free rates: 95.6% (no RAI) vs 95.9% (RAI), meeting non-inferiority criteria. The 2025 guideline calls this a demonstration that "a follow-up strategy" is non-inferior to "systematic adjuvant postoperative administration of RAI in patients with low-risk DTC" (p. 885).

2. **HiLo RCT (438 patients, 6.5-year follow-up).** Low-activity RAI (1.1 GBq) was non-inferior to high-activity (3.7 GBq). Cumulative recurrence at 7 years: 5.9% vs 7.3% (HR 1.10, CI 0.47-2.59).

3. **NTCTCSG registry data.** RAI treatment for Stage I and II DTC did not influence disease-specific or disease-free survival.

4. **Multi-institutional retrospective study (1,298 patients).** No benefit of RAI with respect to overall or disease-free survival.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (Phase III RCT -- the single clearest pivotal study driving any recommendation change in the guideline).

### Who Is Affected

All low-risk DTC patients after total thyroidectomy. This effectively ends the debate about RAI in this group. Any remaining use requires specific justification.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Nuclear medicine/endocrinology:** Stop routine RAI for low-risk DTC. The evidence is RCT-based. Document specific justification if RAI is still used.
2. **If RAI is used in select low-risk cases:** Use 30-50 mCi (1.1-1.85 GBq). The door is not completely closed -- "select cases" are still permitted.
3. **RAI preparation:** Default to rhTSH (now "preferred" per R34A, not merely an "alternative").
4. **Accept loss of post-therapy WBS.** Forgoing RAI also means forgoing the staging information from the post-therapy whole-body scan (which detects unexpected metastases in 6-13% of cases). Clinicians must accept this tradeoff for low-risk patients.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Correction: 2015 R51B was already Strong/Moderate against RAI for unifocal papillary microcarcinoma specifically.** The dramatic upgrade from Weak/Low to Strong/High applies to the broader low-risk category (R51A), not to microPTC alone. The diff should distinguish these sub-parts.
- **Follow-up limitation acknowledged.** The guideline states: "One potential limitation of these studies is the relatively limited duration of follow-up." Late recurrences (>10 years) could theoretically modify the risk-benefit calculation.
- **Molecular stratification of RAI response is new.** RAS mutations and RET fusions predict favorable RAI response; BRAF V600E + TERT promoter and TP53 mutations predict resistance. This may refine select cases where RAI is still considered.
- **Compounding effect with lobectomy expansion.** Patients who undergo lobectomy are ineligible for RAI. The combined effect of more lobectomies + strong recommendation against RAI in low-risk = potential 40-60% decrease in total RAI volume.

## 3. Prophylactic CND: "Should Not Be Performed"

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R36C: Thyroidectomy without pCND "is appropriate" for T1-T2 cN0 PTC | R19A: pCND "should not be performed" for most T1-T2 cN0 PTC and most FTCs |
| **Strength** | Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence | Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence |
| **Rhetorical force** | Permissive (appropriate to omit) | Directive (should not be performed) |
| **Source** | p. 34 | p. 866 |

The change from "is appropriate [to omit]" to "should not be performed" is a fundamental shift in rhetorical force -- from permission to omit to a directive prohibition.

### Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline cites multiple meta-analyses (p. 867):

1. **Chen et al. (23 studies, 18,376 patients).** pCLND reduced locoregional recurrence (OR 0.65, CI 0.48-0.88) but increased transient RLN injury (OR 2.03), transient hypocalcemia (OR 2.23), and permanent hypocalcemia (OR 2.22).

2. **Wang et al. (NNT analysis).** NNT of 31 prophylactic CLNDs to prevent 1 recurrence. Overall recurrence: 7.9% without vs 4.7% with pCND. RR 0.59, **CI 0.33-1.07 -- crossing 1.0, not statistically significant.**

3. **Hughes et al. (14 studies).** "Observation of the central neck is safe."

4. **Lee et al.** "No association between occult lymph node metastasis and regional recurrence."

**Rationale category:** New evidence (meta-analyses with quantified benefit-harm tradeoff) + Outcome re-weighting (NNT of 31 with doubled complications = net harm).

### Who Is Affected

All patients undergoing thyroidectomy for cT1-T2 cN0 PTC -- the majority of PTC cases. Also applies to most FTCs (newly specified). This is one of the most significant surgical practice changes in the guideline.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Surgeons:** Stop routine pCND for T1-T2 cN0 PTC. Perform careful intraoperative inspection; proceed to dissection only if suspicious nodes are found.
2. **For T3-T4 tumors:** pCND "may be considered" (R19B, Conditional/Low) but should be weighed against intraoperative risks. This is weaker than the 2015 R36B "should be considered."
3. **Pathology:** When pCND is performed, report lymph node yield (>=5 from central neck) and lymph node ratio.
4. **Tumor board:** Do not use incidental microscopic pN1a disease from pCND as the sole justification for RAI.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Statistical non-significance of recurrence reduction.** The Wang meta-analysis CI crosses 1.0 (RR 0.59, CI 0.33-1.07), meaning the recurrence reduction may not be real. This further strengthens the recommendation against pCND beyond what the original diff analysis stated.
- **The "most" qualifier matters.** R19A says "for MOST small, noninvasive, clinically node-negative PTC." The guideline does not specify which patients are exceptions. Clinicians must decide case-by-case (e.g., young males with aggressive cytologic features, BRAF V600E on FNA).
- **Training/learning curve paradox.** If pCND is no longer routinely performed, surgeons will have fewer opportunities to learn central neck dissection, potentially affecting competency when therapeutic CND is needed. This is a long-term downstream consequence.
- **Staging cascade effect.** Not performing pCND means accepting a degree of understaging. This is appropriate for the population (occult disease is clinically insignificant in most cases) but means some individuals with occult macroscopic N1a disease will be missed.
- **cN1b provision quietly retired.** The 2015 R36B listed lateral neck disease (cN1b) as a standalone indication for prophylactic central neck dissection. The 2025 R19B replaces this with broader language, subsuming it under therapeutic dissection (R20B-C).

## 4. Risk Stratification: 3-Tier to 4-Tier System

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R48: 3-tier system (low, intermediate, high) | R28: 4-tier system (low, low-intermediate, intermediate-high, high) |
| **Strength** | Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence | Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence |
| **Intermediate category** | Single tier, 5-20% recurrence | Split into low-intermediate (5-10%) and intermediate-high (10-25%) |
| **Histotype-specific** | Uniform criteria | Separate criteria for PTC, FTC/IEFVPTC, and OTC |
| **Source** | p. 41, Table 11 | p. 874, Fig. 2, Tables 7-8 |

### Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline extensively documents heterogeneity within the 2015 intermediate category (p. 876):

1. **5-fold recurrence range within intermediate.** Patients with microscopic N1a had 4-8% recurrence; those with macroscopic N1 disease had >20%. The 2015 system forced the same management for both.

2. **New prognostic features validated.** Post-2015 studies confirmed lymph node ratio (LNR >0.25-0.5), extranodal extension, and extensive vascular invasion (>=4 foci) as independent predictors, enabling a principled split.

3. **AJCC 8th edition reclassifications.** Removal of microscopic ETE from T3 required re-mapping risk categories.

4. **Histotype-specific risk.** OTC is now recognized as a distinct entity (WHO 5th edition), with different risk features from PTC and FTC.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (multiple retrospective multivariable analyses) + Reinterpretation (documented within-category heterogeneity) + Methodology change (AJCC 8th, WHO 5th).

### Who Is Affected

All patients previously classified as "intermediate risk" -- the largest and most clinically heterogeneous group. Treatment decisions for RAI, TSH suppression, and surveillance intensity all flow from risk tier assignment. The split enables more precise treatment: low-intermediate patients may avoid RAI and aggressive suppression; intermediate-high patients receive clearer guidance that these interventions should be strongly considered.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Pathology reporting:** Ensure synoptic reports include number of lymph nodes examined, LNR, extranodal extension, specific vascular invasion count, and OTC status.
2. **EMR templates:** Update from 3-tier to 4-tier risk stratification.
3. **Tumor board:** Explicitly classify formerly "intermediate" patients into low-intermediate or intermediate-high. This classification drives the RAI decision.
4. **Molecular testing:** R28B states postoperative molecular profiling is not routinely recommended but "if such data have been obtained, they can be used to further estimate risks." BRAF+TERT co-mutation formally places patients in high-risk regardless of other features.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Boundary uncertainty acknowledged by the guideline itself.** Figure 2 footnote: "No clear cutoffs for LNs between low-intermediate and high-intermediate risk groups." The 4-tier system is an improvement over 3 tiers but contains acknowledged arbitrariness at the boundary.
- **Implementation difficulty underestimated.** Requires EMR updates, tumor board retraining, pathology synoptic report revisions, and re-stratification of existing patients. This will take time to propagate.
- **The old system was not wrong.** The 2025 guideline validates the 2015 3-tier system as a good recurrence predictor. The change is for added precision, not because the prior system failed.
- **Molecular profiling gap.** R28B creates an inequality: patients whose tumors happen to have been molecularly profiled get more precise risk estimates than those who were not.

## 5. TSH Suppression Weakened + Outcome Re-weighting

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **High-risk initial TSH** | R59A: <0.1 mU/L (Strong/Moderate) | R45: Individualize; subnormal may benefit (Conditional/Low) |
| **Low-risk TSH** | R59C/E: 0.5-2 mU/L (Weak/Low) | R46A: Long-term suppression "not suggested" (Conditional/Low) |
| **Post-lobectomy** | 0.5-2 mU/L; hormone may not be needed | Normal range; 70-80% can avoid L-T4 entirely |
| **Source** | p. 61-63 | pp. 895-897 |

### Why It Changed

1. **NTCTCSG study (3,238 patients).** Improved overall and disease-free survival with moderate suppression (subnormal to normal TSH), but "no further improvement for any stage with TSH levels averaging in the undetectable subnormal range" (p. 896).

2. **Cardiovascular and bone harm documented.** TSH <=0.4 mIU/L associated with increased atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis (ref 962). One study showed lower survival when TSH <0.02 mIU/L (ref 963).

3. **No recurrence benefit in low-risk.** Multiple studies showed no difference in recurrence across TSH strata (0.5 to >4.5 mIU/L) in low-risk patients.

4. **Practical difficulty of targets.** Real-world data showed only 29% of patients achieved target TSH despite good adherence; approximately 50% were overtreated.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (NTCTCSG) + Outcome re-weighting (harms of suppression now better documented than benefits) + Implementation feedback (difficulty achieving precise targets).

### Who Is Affected

- **Low-risk patients after lobectomy:** 70-80% can avoid thyroid hormone entirely. This is transformative when combined with lobectomy expansion.
- **High-risk patients:** No longer targeted to undetectable TSH. Subnormal but detectable is now the goal, reducing cardiovascular/bone morbidity.
- **Older adults, patients with atrial fibrillation, postmenopausal women:** Explicit recognition that suppression risks may outweigh benefits.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Post-lobectomy:** Check TSH 6-8 weeks after surgery. If normal, do not start thyroid hormone. Only initiate L-T4 if TSH rises above reference range.
2. **Low-risk with excellent response:** Target TSH within normal reference range. Review existing patients on suppression and consider dose reduction.
3. **High-risk:** Individualize. Subnormal (0.1-0.5) may be reasonable, but avoid undetectable (<0.1). Reassess every 1-2 years.
4. **Cardiovascular risk assessment:** Before initiating or continuing suppression, assess for AF risk, osteoporosis, and ischemic heart disease.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Low-risk TSH change is under-rated at "Moderate" in original analysis.** Combined with lobectomy expansion and RAI de-escalation, this creates a transformative patient experience: a patient with 1.5 cm PTC may receive lobectomy, no RAI, no thyroid hormone, and limited surveillance. This compounding effect warrants a HIGH impact rating.
- **"Individualize" replaces specific numbers.** While more accurate, removing specific TSH targets creates operational difficulty. The 2015 targets (though imprecise) were actionable. Clinicians will need local protocols to fill this gap.
- **TSH during active surveillance is unresolved.** Conflicting data exist on whether TSH levels affect tumor growth in AS patients. The 2025 guideline leaves this unanswered.

## 6. Surveillance Endpoints Defined: 5-8 Year US, 10-15 Year Tg, "Complete Remission"

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **US endpoint** | None defined; "periodically" (R65A) | Can stop at 5-8 years of sustained excellent response (R48, Conditional/Low) |
| **Tg endpoint** | None defined; ongoing q6-12mo (R62-63) | Can stop at 10-15 years ("complete remission") (R48, Conditional/Low) |
| **Tg after lobectomy** | Periodic, routine (R64, Strong/Low) | Not recommended routinely (R47D, Conditional/Very Low) |
| **"Remission" concept** | Not defined | Formally introduced: sustained excellent response for 10-15 years |
| **Source** | pp. 62-63 | pp. 899-901 |

### Why It Changed

1. **Ultra-low late recurrence rates.** Multiple cohorts (>1,000 patients, >10-year follow-up) showed <1.4% recurrence after 5-8 years of sustained excellent response.

2. **False-positive US exceeds true recurrence.** In low-risk patients with excellent response, continued US yields more false positives than true recurrences, generating unnecessary biopsies and patient anxiety.

3. **Tg unreliable after lobectomy.** A commissioned systematic review found "serum Tg measurement following partial thyroidectomy was not accurate for diagnosing DTC recurrence or metastasis." Tg rose in only 3 of 18 patients who recurred after lobectomy.

4. **Financial and psychological burden.** "Lifelong biochemical assessments and neck ultrasounds are associated with financial and psychological burden that may not be justified" (p. 899).

**Rationale category:** New evidence (long-term cohort data) + Patient-centered (reducing monitoring burden) + De-escalation philosophy.

### Who Is Affected

Hundreds of thousands of DTC survivors currently in indefinite surveillance. This is the largest affected population of any recommendation change in the guideline. The growing lobectomy population is also directly affected by the Tg monitoring reversal.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Review existing patients.** Identify low-risk patients with excellent response followed >5 years. Discuss discontinuing routine US.
2. **10-15 year milestone.** For patients with sustained excellent response, introduce the "complete remission" concept and discuss stopping biochemical monitoring.
3. **Post-lobectomy:** Obtain a single Tg at ~12 weeks postop. If not markedly elevated, do not plan routine serial Tg.
4. **Do NOT stop monitoring for:** Patients with indeterminate or incomplete response, history of structural recurrence, intermediate-high or high-risk categories, or rising TgAb.
5. **Documentation:** At each visit, formally record the response category (excellent, indeterminate, biochemically incomplete, structurally incomplete). This drives the de-escalation timeline.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Implementation resistance likely.** Endocrinologists have been trained for decades that DTC surveillance is lifelong. Changing this requires cultural shift, not just guideline awareness. Patient expectations and practice economics also act as barriers.
- **"Complete remission" is hedged.** The guideline qualifies: "This does not always mean that cancer has been cured or will not return." This cautious language may not provide the psychological closure intended.
- **Tg after lobectomy reversal is under-rated.** As the lobectomy population grows, the absolute number of patients affected by this reversal grows proportionally. Should be rated HIGH for forward-looking impact.
- **No RCT comparing monitoring strategies.** The evidence is strong for low recurrence rates but weak for formally comparing monitoring vs no monitoring.

## 7. Active Surveillance Formally Endorsed (Elevated from 2015 R12)

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Location** | R12 (nodule section): AS "can be considered" for very low-risk papillary microcarcinomas | R11-R14 (DTC section): Formal recommendation with protocols |
| **Eligibility** | Primarily high-surgical-risk patients, limited life span, concurrent medical issues | Broadened: "some patients with cT1aN0M0 PTCs" via shared decision-making |
| **Monitoring protocol** | Not specified | R12: US q6mo x 1-2 years, then annually; R13: No routine Tg; R14: Surgery triggers defined (growth >=3 mm, new nodes) |
| **Percutaneous ablation** | Not addressed | R11B: May be considered as alternative (Conditional/Low) |
| **Source** | R12, p. 16-17 (nodule section) | R11-R14, pp. 859-861 |

### Why It Changed

1. **Systematic review by task force subgroup.** Found "low-certainty evidence that in adults with small, low-risk DTC, active surveillance and immediate resection are associated with similar, low risk of all-cause or cancer-specific mortality, distant metastasis, and recurrence after thyroidectomy" (p. 859).

2. **Japanese cohort data (N=1,235, 75-month follow-up).** Tumor growth rates were low; older patients (>60 years) were significantly less likely to experience progression than younger adults (<40 years).

3. **Korean and US AS programs.** Additional prospective data supporting safety.

4. **Percutaneous ablation matured.** Meta-analyses showed 57.6-91% complete tumor disappearance with 0.7% major complications.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (accumulated AS program data) + De-escalation (avoiding surgery for indolent cancers) + Patient-centered (shared decision-making required).

### Who Is Affected

Patients with papillary thyroid microcarcinoma (cT1aN0M0), especially older patients (>60 years). This is a large and growing population given incidental detection rates.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Establish institutional AS protocols.** Requires experienced team with high-quality neck ultrasound. Define monitoring schedule (US q6mo x 1-2 years, then annually) and intervention triggers.
2. **Broaden candidate selection.** The 2015 framing emphasized high-surgical-risk patients. The 2025 framing opens AS to the general population with appropriate shared decision-making.
3. **Document exclusion criteria.** Not candidates: aggressive histology on cytopathology, imaging evidence of invasion (RLN/trachea/esophagus), visible ETE, regional/distant metastases, posteriorly located tumors abutting trachea.
4. **Accept patient anxiety attrition.** Studies show 10-30% of AS patients elect surgery due to anxiety. This is acknowledged as a legitimate trigger.
5. **Percutaneous ablation.** Available predominantly in specialized centers. In the US as of 2026, not yet widely available or covered by many payers.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **CORRECTION: Active surveillance was NOT absent from 2015.** It was present in 2015 R12 (nodule section) with specific criteria and discussion of Japanese data. The correct characterization is "elevated and expanded from the nodule section to a formal DTC recommendation" -- not "newly added." The genuine changes are: (1) broadened eligibility beyond high-surgical-risk patients, (2) standardized protocols, (3) addition of percutaneous ablation, (4) removal of the implicit framing that surgery is still the default.
- **Age-dependent benefit.** Patients >60 years are better AS candidates than those <40 years, based on Japanese cohort data showing significantly less progression in older patients.
- **Long-term data gap.** Most AS studies have 5-10 year follow-up, but papillary microcarcinoma natural history extends over decades. Follow-up duration remains unknown.
- **Implementation barriers.** Requires institutional expertise in serial high-quality neck ultrasound. Patient anxiety, liability concerns, and long-term compliance are practical challenges that limit near-term adoption in community settings.
- **Percutaneous ablation deserves separate assessment.** This is an active treatment, not observation. Evidence comes predominantly from Asian centers; generalizability and US availability are limited. Impact is LOW-MODERATE in the US, MODERATE-HIGH in Asia.

## 8. Lobectomy for cT2 (2-4 cm): Conditional Preference (NOTE: Mixed Evidence, Lower Certainty)

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R35B: "can be either a bilateral procedure... or a unilateral procedure" | R15B: Lobectomy "may be the preferred initial surgical treatment due to significantly lower risk and side effects" |
| **Strength** | Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence | Conditional recommendation, Low-moderate certainty evidence |
| **Framing** | True clinical equipoise | Directional preference for lobectomy |
| **Source** | pp. 31-32 | p. 862 |

**This item is deliberately ranked #8 rather than higher because the cross-validation identified significant uncertainty.** The 2025 guideline expresses a directional preference for lobectomy but does so with LESS certainty than the 2015 guideline had about equipoise. The recommendation strength decreased from Strong to Conditional, and the evidence certainty from Moderate to Low-moderate.

### Why It Changed

1. **Same survival data, different interpretation.** The same NCDB/SEER analyses that 2015 cited as supporting "either" are now interpreted as favoring lobectomy because of the morbidity differential.

2. **De-escalation philosophy.** Lower complication rates (7.6% vs 14.5%) tip the balance when survival is equivalent.

3. **Shared decision-making.** Mandatory counseling about possible conversion to total thyroidectomy or need for completion thyroidectomy.

**Rationale category:** Reinterpretation of existing data + De-escalation + Patient-centered.

### Who Is Affected

Patients with low-risk, unilateral DTC 2-4 cm, cN0M0. Practice change will vary by setting: academic centers already performing lobectomy for select 2-4 cm tumors may see little change; community surgeons who defaulted to total thyroidectomy for anything >1 cm face a larger shift.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Present lobectomy as the suggested approach** for low-risk cT2 patients, but maintain total thyroidectomy as a legitimate alternative.
2. **Mandatory counseling.** Discuss the possibility of conversion/completion thyroidectomy if higher-risk factors emerge.
3. **Histotype matters.** The NCDB data show the survival difference for total thyroidectomy was only for classical PTC (not FVPTC) in the T2 range. Lobectomy preference is most confidently applied to FVPTC and least confidently to classical PTC with concerning features.
4. **Document the discussion.** Shared decision-making should be documented, including discussion of the conflicting evidence.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **CORRECTION: This is NOT a "stronger recommendation" for lobectomy.** The recommendation strength decreased from Strong/Moderate (2015, for either option) to Conditional/Low-moderate (2025, for lobectomy preference). The change is directional (from equipoise to preference), not a strength upgrade. Accurate characterization: "Directional shift toward lobectomy at lower certainty."
- **Approximately half of meta-analyses show lower recurrence with total thyroidectomy for T2.** The 2025 guideline explicitly states this (p. 862): "approximately half showing no difference in recurrence or survival... The other approximate half demonstrate statistically significant, lower recurrence rates with total thyroidectomy." One meta-analysis found improved overall survival confined to T2 tumors specifically.
- **Total thyroidectomy explicitly preserved as legitimate.** The guideline states patients "may prefer to undergo total thyroidectomy and RAI postoperatively to possibly reduce their risk of recurrence and improve survival." This is not an unambiguous de-escalation.
- **Classical PTC vs FVPTC distinction.** An NCDB study showed significantly better survival with total thyroidectomy for classical PTC but not FVPTC in the T2 size range.
- **Confidence rating should be Moderate (not High)** given the conflicting evidence explicitly acknowledged in the guideline.

## 9. Precision Oncology for RAI-Refractory DTC (Mutation-Specific Therapy)

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R96: Sorafenib or lenvatinib for progressive RAIR DTC (one general recommendation) | R61-R74: Mutation-specific therapy algorithms with 14 recommendations |
| **Agents** | Sorafenib (2013 approval), Lenvatinib (2015 approval) | + Larotrectinib, Entrectinib (NTRK), Selpercatinib, Pralsetinib (RET), Dabrafenib+Trametinib (BRAF V600E), ALK inhibitors, immunotherapy, redifferentiation therapy |
| **Framework** | Empiric multi-kinase inhibitor (MKI) | Molecular testing first; gene-specific targeted therapy preferred over empiric MKI |
| **Redifferentiation** | Not addressed | R74: KI-mediated redifferentiation to restore RAI avidity |
| **Source** | pp. 80-82 | pp. 907-925 |

### Why It Changed

1. **FDA approvals of selective inhibitors (post-2015).** Larotrectinib (2018), entrectinib (2019), selpercatinib (2020), pralsetinib (2020) were all approved after the 2015 guideline, with higher response rates and better tolerability than empiric MKIs.

2. **Phase I/II trial data.** Mutation-specific trials showed superior response rates and fewer side effects than broad-spectrum MKIs.

3. **Redifferentiation concept.** Selumetinib and other MEK/BRAF inhibitors showed ability to restore RAI avidity in RAIR tumors, creating a new therapeutic category.

**Rationale category:** New evidence (Phase I/II trials) + New technology (FDA-approved selective inhibitors).

### Who Is Affected

Patients with progressive RAI-refractory DTC -- a smaller but high-acuity population. This is the area where the guideline has expanded most dramatically (from 1 recommendation to 14).

### What to Do Differently

1. **Molecular testing is now essential** for all RAIR DTC patients before systemic therapy. Test for NTRK fusions, RET fusions/mutations, BRAF V600E, ALK fusions.
2. **Use gene-specific therapy when a targetable mutation is found.** Empiric MKI (sorafenib, lenvatinib) is now a fallback, not the default.
3. **Consider redifferentiation.** For BRAF-mutated RAIR DTC, dabrafenib+trametinib may restore RAI avidity, enabling a return to RAI therapy.
4. **Lenvatinib starting dose.** R64 specifically recommends considering reduced starting doses based on post-marketing experience with dose-limiting toxicities.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **This change is confirmed accurate.** The expansion from 1 to 14 recommendations reflects a genuine paradigm shift from empiric to precision oncology.
- **Impact is HIGH for the RAIR subgroup** but affects a smaller absolute number of patients compared to surgery and RAI changes. Most newly diagnosed DTC patients will never need systemic therapy.
- **Rapid evidence evolution.** This is the most rapidly evolving area; recommendations may need updating sooner than other sections.

## 10. Completion Thyroidectomy Downgraded

### What Changed

| | 2015 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| **Recommendation** | R38A: "Should be offered" to patients for whom total thyroidectomy would have been recommended | R16A: "May be considered" based on postoperative risk, RLN status, patient preference |
| **Strength** | Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence | Conditional recommendation, Low-moderate certainty evidence |
| **Decision framework** | Hypothetical: "Would TT have been recommended?" | Risk-based: "Does this patient's risk profile benefit from completion?" |
| **RLN consideration** | Not explicitly mentioned | Explicitly required: "accounting for recurrent laryngeal nerve function" |
| **Source** | p. 35 | p. 864 |

### Why It Changed

1. **Most incidental cancers are low-risk.** The majority of cancers found after lobectomy for indeterminate cytology are low-risk and do not require total thyroidectomy.

2. **AJCC 8th edition.** Microscopic ETE removed from T3 -- eliminating a major trigger for completion thyroidectomy.

3. **More nuanced risk features.** Completion thyroidectomy decisions now consider LNR (>0.3), number of positive nodes (>3-5), extranodal extension, and extensive vascular invasion rather than tumor size alone.

4. **RLN safety.** Explicit requirement to assess RLN function before proceeding. If ipsilateral nerve was injured or contralateral nerve is at risk, completion thyroidectomy carries substantially higher risk.

5. **Patient preference.** Shared decision-making formally incorporated.

**Rationale category:** De-escalation (balancing recurrence risk against surgical morbidity) + Reinterpretation (most incidentals are low-risk) + Methodology change (AJCC 8th).

### Who Is Affected

Patients diagnosed with DTC after lobectomy for indeterminate cytology -- a common clinical scenario. Previously, most would have been offered completion thyroidectomy. Now, many (especially those with low-risk features) can be observed.

### What to Do Differently

1. **Post-lobectomy risk assessment.** Classify using the 4-tier system. If low risk, counsel that lobectomy alone is likely sufficient.
2. **Reframe the question.** Instead of "Would we have done total thyroidectomy if we had known?" ask "Does this patient's risk profile benefit from completion?"
3. **Document specific features.** Record LNR, extranodal extension, vascular invasion count, molecular features, RLN status, and patient preference.
4. **Specific high-risk triggers for completion:** LNR >0.3, >3-5 positive nodes, extranodal extension, extensive vascular invasion, aggressive histology with additional risk factors.

### Cross-Validation Notes

- **Cascading effect from other changes.** With lobectomy becoming the default for T1-T2 and RAI being de-escalated for low-risk, the completion thyroidectomy decision becomes less consequential for low-risk patients (even if completion is not performed, they would not have received RAI anyway).
- **Completion rates did not increase after 2015 lobectomy expansion.** One study found rates fell from 74% to 20% after 2015 guideline implementation, countering fears of more second surgeries.
- **RLN status as explicit criterion is new and important.** This validates clinical practice of avoiding re-operation when contralateral nerve status is uncertain.

## Compounding Effects: The Transformative Patient Journey

The most important insight from this analysis is not any single change but the compounding effect of changes #1, #2, #5, #6, and #10 acting together. Consider a patient diagnosed with a 1.5 cm papillary thyroid carcinoma in 2015 vs 2025:

| Decision Point | 2015 Pathway | 2025 Pathway |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Surgery | Equipoise -- many received total thyroidectomy | Lobectomy (Strong recommendation) |
| Prophylactic CND | Often performed | Should not be performed |
| Completion thyroidectomy | Frequently offered if risk features | Rarely needed (most are low-risk) |
| RAI | Considered for many | Not recommended (Strong/High) |
| Thyroid hormone | Lifelong L-T4 with TSH target 0.5-2 | 70-80% avoid L-T4 entirely |
| Surveillance | Indefinite Tg + US | US for 5-8 years; single postop Tg; can achieve "complete remission" |
| Total interventions | TT + RAI + lifelong L-T4 + indefinite monitoring | Lobectomy only + limited monitoring with defined endpoint |

This aggregated shift is transformative. The proportion of DTC patients receiving RAI could decrease 40-60%. The number needing lifelong thyroid hormone could decrease substantially. Healthcare utilization for the growing DTC survivor population is fundamentally reduced.

## Implementation Barriers

1. **4-tier risk system:** Requires EMR template updates, pathology synoptic report revisions, tumor board retraining, and re-stratification of existing patients.
2. **Active surveillance:** Requires institutional expertise in serial high-quality neck ultrasound, patient anxiety management protocols, and long-term compliance infrastructure.
3. **Surveillance de-escalation:** Faces physician inertia (decades of training in lifelong monitoring), patient expectations (monitoring provides reassurance), and practice economics (surveillance is a revenue stream).
4. **"Individualize" TSH targets:** Replaces specific numbers that were operationally useful, requiring local protocols.
5. **Precision oncology for RAIR:** Requires molecular testing infrastructure, tumor boards with molecular expertise, and access to gene-specific therapies.
6. **pCND training paradox:** Fewer routine pCNDs means fewer training opportunities, potentially affecting surgical competency for therapeutic CND.
