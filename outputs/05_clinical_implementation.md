## Workflow 1: Initial Surgery Selection (Lobectomy vs Total Thyroidectomy)

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 Workflow (R35A-C)

The 2015 guideline created a three-tier surgical decision framework based on tumor size:

- **<1 cm, cN0, no ETE:** Lobectomy is the default. Total thyroidectomy only if clear indications exist (prior radiation, familial DTC, clinical nodal metastases). (R35C, Strong/Moderate)
- **1-4 cm, cN0, no ETE:** Either lobectomy or total thyroidectomy is acceptable. The treatment team "may choose total thyroidectomy to enable RAI therapy or to enhance follow-up." The framing is one of clinical equipoise. (R35B, Strong/Moderate)
- **>4 cm, gross ETE, cN1, or M1:** Total thyroidectomy with gross tumor removal. (R35A, Strong/Moderate)

In practice, many surgeons defaulted to total thyroidectomy for any tumor >1 cm because (a) the equipoise framing left room for it, (b) institutional culture favored it, and (c) the rationale for RAI and Tg-based surveillance was widely accepted.

#### 2025 Workflow (R15A-C)

The 2025 guideline restructured the surgical decision around a different size threshold and shifted the default:

- **Up to 2 cm (cT1), cN0M0:** Lobectomy is the recommended procedure, unless bilateral cancers or other indications exist. (R15A, Strong/Moderate certainty)
- **>2 cm to 4 cm (cT2), cN0M0, low risk:** Lobectomy is "preferred" because of "significantly lower risk and side effects." Total thyroidectomy remains an option for RAI eligibility or enhanced follow-up. Counsel about possible conversion or completion thyroidectomy. (R15B, Conditional/Low-Moderate certainty)
- **>4 cm (cT3a), gross ETE (cT3b/T4), cN1, or M1:** Total thyroidectomy with gross tumor removal. (R15C, Strong/Moderate certainty)

The 2025 guideline also adds, for the first time, active surveillance (R11) and percutaneous ablation (R11B) as alternatives to surgery for cT1aN0M0 PTC.

#### Summary of the Shift

| Feature | 2015 Default | 2025 Default |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Tumor <1 cm, cN0 | Lobectomy | Lobectomy (or active surveillance for cT1a) |
| Tumor 1-2 cm, cN0 | Equipoise (either acceptable) | Lobectomy recommended |
| Tumor 2-4 cm, cN0, low risk | Equipoise (either acceptable) | Lobectomy preferred |
| Tumor >4 cm or cN1/M1/ETE | Total thyroidectomy | Total thyroidectomy |

The effective lobectomy ceiling moved from <1 cm to up to 4 cm (with lobectomy being the clear default up to 2 cm and the preferred option up to 4 cm in low-risk patients).

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline explicitly cites multiple lines of evidence for this shift:

1. **No survival difference for 1-4 cm tumors.** The 2025 guideline references the Adam et al. updated NCDB analysis (61,775 patients, 1998-2006), which showed that the overall survival advantage seen in the earlier Bilimoria et al. study for 1-4 cm PTC "disappeared when further adjustment was made for additional variables related to complexity and severity of illness." This directly contradicted the main survival argument for total thyroidectomy in 1-4 cm tumors. The 2015 guideline already cited this evidence but still maintained equipoise; the 2025 guideline tips the balance.

2. **Complication rate differential.** The 2025 guideline emphasizes the halved complication rate with lobectomy. It cites data showing that even high-volume surgeons have complication rates of 7.6% after lobectomy vs 14.5% after total thyroidectomy. This outcome reweighting -- giving more weight to surgical morbidity vs theoretical survival benefit -- is a philosophical shift.

3. **Reduced need for RAI.** The 2015 guideline acknowledged that "our current more selective approach to RAI ablation in these patients requires a critical reassessment" of the rationale for total thyroidectomy. By 2025, with the ESTIMABL2 RCT demonstrating non-inferiority of no-RAI in low-risk DTC, the primary historical justification for total thyroidectomy (enabling RAI) has weakened substantially.

4. **Surveillance no longer requires total thyroidectomy.** The shift toward reliance on neck ultrasound rather than RAI scanning and stimulated Tg for surveillance means that the follow-up rationale for total thyroidectomy is diminished.

5. **AJCC 8th edition staging changes.** Microscopic ETE was removed from T3 staging. The age cutoff changed from 45 to 55 years. Both changes result in 20-30% of patients being downstaged, removing some of the prior justification for aggressive surgery.

**Rationale type:** New evidence (NCDB/SEER reanalyses, complication data) + De-escalation philosophy (morbidity avoidance) + Outcome re-weighting (equal emphasis on complication avoidance as on recurrence prevention).

**Confidence in rationale:** High. The guideline text explicitly lays out the reasoning.

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **Low-risk PTC 1-2 cm:** These patients shift from a genuine clinical decision (equipoise) to a strong recommendation for lobectomy. This is the largest affected group, representing a substantial proportion of new DTC diagnoses. Impact: HIGH.
- **Low-risk PTC 2-4 cm, cN0:** These patients shift from equipoise to a preference for lobectomy. Surgeons who previously defaulted to total thyroidectomy for all tumors >1 cm will need to change practice. Impact: HIGH.
- **Patients with cT1a PTC:** Now have the option of active surveillance or percutaneous ablation, avoiding surgery entirely. Impact: HIGH for this subgroup.
- **Multifocal disease:** Not a contraindication to lobectomy if all foci are ipsilateral, but bilateral cancers remain an indication for total thyroidectomy.
- **Node-positive patients (cN1):** No change. Total thyroidectomy remains standard.

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Surgeons:** Default to lobectomy for all cT1-T2, cN0M0 DTC. Reserve total thyroidectomy for tumors >4 cm, cN1, gross ETE, bilateral disease, or patient preference for RAI-based follow-up.
2. **Endocrinologists:** In tumor board, advocate for lobectomy in 1-4 cm cN0 tumors unless specific high-risk features (aggressive histology, BRAF+TERT co-mutation, extensive vascular invasion) are present.
3. **Patient counseling:** Explain that lobectomy has half the complication rate with equivalent survival outcomes. Discuss the possibility of completion thyroidectomy if final pathology reveals unexpected high-risk features.
4. **Active surveillance programs:** Establish institutional protocols for monitoring cT1aN0M0 PTC with ultrasound every 6 months for 1-2 years, then annually. Identify an experienced team and set criteria for intervention (growth >=3 mm, new nodes, posterior extension).

**Net effect:** Reduces overtreatment, surgical morbidity (RLN injury, hypoparathyroidism), need for lifelong thyroid hormone replacement in many patients.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 2: Completion Thyroidectomy

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 Workflow (R38)

- **R38A:** "Completion thyroidectomy should be offered to patients for whom a bilateral thyroidectomy would have been recommended had the diagnosis been available before the initial surgery." (Strong/Moderate)
- Practical interpretation: If post-lobectomy pathology revealed a tumor 1-4 cm (or any feature that would have prompted total thyroidectomy), completion thyroidectomy was the expected step.
- Since the 2015 threshold for total thyroidectomy was lower (any >1 cm tumor was in equipoise), many patients who had lobectomy for indeterminate cytology and were found to have cancer >1 cm were offered completion thyroidectomy.

#### 2025 Workflow (R16)

- **R16A:** "Completion thyroidectomy for cancer following initial lobectomy may be considered to address persistent or additional disease, enable RAI, or enhance monitoring based on postoperative risk assessment. Factors influencing this decision include tumor size, histologic subtype and grade, extent of invasion, lymph node status, molecular features, recurrent laryngeal nerve status, patient preference, and the presence of suspicious contralateral nodularity." (Conditional/Low-Moderate)
- The shift from "should be offered" (Strong) to "may be considered" (Conditional) is a major downgrade in recommendation strength.
- The 2025 framing explicitly ties completion thyroidectomy to postoperative risk, not merely to the hypothetical "would total thyroidectomy have been recommended."

#### Practical Difference

| Scenario | 2015 Approach | 2025 Approach |
|----------|--------------|--------------|
| Incidental 1.5 cm PTC found on lobectomy path | Completion thyroidectomy typically offered | Lobectomy alone likely sufficient (low risk cT1) |
| 3 cm intrathyroidal PTC, cN0, low risk | Completion thyroidectomy typically offered | Consider lobectomy alone; completion only if high-risk features |
| Vascular invasion on path (PTC) | Completion thyroidectomy | Consider, weighing against risks; especially if limited vascular invasion |
| Extensive vascular invasion (FTC/OTC) | Completion thyroidectomy | Completion thyroidectomy warranted (high-risk feature) |
| pN1 with >5 nodes or extranodal extension | Completion thyroidectomy | Completion thyroidectomy warranted |
| pN1 with 1-2 micrometastatic nodes | Completion thyroidectomy often performed | Lobectomy alone likely sufficient |

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline text provides several explicit reasons:

1. **Most incidental cancers are low-risk.** The guideline notes that the majority of cancers discovered after lobectomy for indeterminate nodules are low-risk and do not require total thyroidectomy.

2. **AJCC 8th edition removed microscopic ETE from T3.** This removed a substantial trigger for completion thyroidectomy that existed under the 7th edition staging.

3. **More nuanced risk features.** The 2025 guideline emphasizes that completion thyroidectomy decisions should consider LNR, extranodal extension, extensive vascular invasion, and molecular features rather than using a blanket approach based on tumor size.

4. **RLN status.** The 2025 guideline explicitly states that RLN function after initial lobectomy should inform the decision. If the contralateral nerve is at risk or the ipsilateral nerve was injured, completion thyroidectomy carries substantially higher risk.

5. **Patient preference.** The 2025 guideline formally incorporates shared decision-making into the completion thyroidectomy discussion.

**Rationale type:** De-escalation philosophy + Reinterpretation (same data, higher threshold for re-operation) + Patient-centered (shared decision-making).

**Confidence in rationale:** High. The guideline explicitly discusses these factors.

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **Patients with incidental 1-4 cm PTC found on lobectomy for indeterminate nodules:** Previously, most would have been offered completion thyroidectomy. Now, many can be observed. Impact: HIGH.
- **Patients with pN1 micrometastatic disease (few small nodes):** No longer an automatic trigger for completion thyroidectomy.
- **Patients with contralateral RLN concerns:** The explicit mention of RLN status validates clinical reluctance to re-operate.

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **After lobectomy shows cancer:** Assess using the 2025 4-tier risk stratification. If low risk (intrathyroidal, no aggressive features, low-volume or no nodal disease), counsel patient that lobectomy may be sufficient.
2. **Tumor board discussions:** Frame the question as "Does this patient's risk profile benefit from completion thyroidectomy?" rather than "Would we have done total thyroidectomy if we had known?"
3. **Documentation:** Document the specific risk features considered, RLN status, and patient preference.

**Net effect:** Fewer completion thyroidectomies, reduced re-operative complications, reduced need for lifelong thyroid hormone, maintained oncologic safety.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 3: Lymph Node Surgery

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### Prophylactic Central Neck Dissection (pCND)

**2015 Position:**

- **R36C:** "Thyroidectomy without prophylactic central neck dissection is appropriate for small (T1 or T2), noninvasive, clinically node-negative papillary thyroid carcinoma." (Weak/Low)
- **R36B:** "Prophylactic central-compartment neck dissection (ipsilateral or bilateral) should be considered in patients with PTC with clinically uninvolved central neck nodes (cN0) who have advanced primary tumors (T3 or T4) or clinically involved lateral neck nodes (cN1b)." (Weak/Low)

The 2015 position was permissive: pCND was "appropriate" to omit for small tumors but "should be considered" for advanced tumors. Many surgeons performed routine pCND because the language did not strongly discourage it and because staging information was considered potentially valuable.

**2025 Position:**

- **R19A:** "Prophylactic central-compartment lymph node dissection should not be performed for most small, noninvasive, clinically node-negative PTC (cT1-T2, cN0) and for most FTCs." (Strong/Moderate certainty)
- **R19B:** "Prophylactic central-compartment neck dissection may be considered in patients with PTC and clinically uninvolved lymph nodes (cN0) who have advanced primary tumors (T3 or T4) or for whom the information will be used to plan further steps in therapy, but this approach should be weighed against the risks as they evolve during thyroidectomy." (Conditional/Low)

The critical change: pCND went from "appropriate to omit" (permissive) to "should not be performed" (directive against). The recommendation strength upgraded from Weak to Strong, and the evidence quality from Low to Moderate.

#### Therapeutic Node Dissection

Both guidelines agree: therapeutic dissection for cN1 disease is mandatory (Strong recommendation in both). No substantive change.

The 2025 guideline adds new detail on lymph node yield (LNY) expectations: >=5 nodes from central neck, >=20 from lateral neck, and introduces LNR as a prognostic tool.

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline provides extensive data from multiple meta-analyses:

1. **Number needed to treat (NNT) = 31.** The Wang et al. meta-analysis found that 31 patients must undergo pCND to prevent a single recurrence. The 2025 guideline explicitly cites this.

2. **pCND doubles complication rates.** The Chen et al. meta-analysis (23 studies, 18,376 patients) showed:
   - Transient RLN injury: OR 2.03 (CI 1.32-3.13)
   - Transient hypocalcemia: OR 2.23 (CI 1.84-2.70)
   - Permanent hypocalcemia: OR 2.22 (CI 1.58-3.13)

3. **No survival benefit demonstrated.** Multiple studies including Hughes et al. (14 studies) and Zhao et al. (17 studies, 4,437 patients) found no overall survival benefit from pCND.

4. **Upstaging without clinical consequence.** pCND frequently converts cN0 to pN1a, leading to AJCC stage migration and increased RAI utilization without demonstrated outcome benefit. The 2015 guideline already recognized this problem ("microscopic nodal positivity does not carry the recurrence risk of macroscopic clinically detectable disease"), but the 2025 guideline draws a firmer conclusion.

5. **Equipoise analysis favors omission.** The Zhao meta-analysis showed that while pCND reduced locoregional recurrence (risk ratio 0.66), the pCND group received higher RAI doses (74.6% vs 59.9%) and had significantly more morbidity (OR 2.56).

**Rationale type:** New evidence (multiple meta-analyses post-2015) + De-escalation philosophy + Outcome re-weighting (morbidity avoidance vs marginal recurrence reduction).

**Confidence in rationale:** High. The meta-analyses are directly cited with specific numbers.

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **T1-T2, cN0 PTC patients:** The largest affected group. Routine pCND should stop. Impact: HIGH.
- **High-volume thyroid surgeons who routinely perform pCND:** Practice change required.
- **Low-volume surgeons:** The morbidity argument is even stronger, as complication rates from pCND are higher in lower-volume hands.
- **T3-T4 patients:** Modest impact. pCND remains a conditional option but now requires explicit risk-benefit discussion.

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Surgeons:** Stop routine pCND for T1-T2 cN0 PTC. Perform careful intraoperative inspection of the central compartment; proceed to dissection only if suspicious nodes are identified.
2. **When performing pCND for T3-T4:** Document the specific rationale. Weigh intraoperative findings including parathyroid and RLN status.
3. **Pathology:** When pCND is performed, report LNY and LNR. A central neck specimen should contain >=5 lymph nodes; a lateral specimen >=20.
4. **Tumor board:** Do not use microscopic pN1a disease from pCND as the sole justification for RAI.

**Net effect:** Reduces surgical morbidity (hypocalcemia, RLN injury), reduces inappropriate upstaging, reduces inappropriate RAI use.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 4: Postoperative Risk Stratification

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 System: 3-Tier (R48)

The 2015 ATA Risk Stratification System used three tiers:

| Category | Key Features | Estimated Recurrence |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Low risk** | Intrathyroidal DTC, <=5 pN1 micrometastases (<0.2 cm), no vascular invasion, no aggressive histology, BRAF-mutated PTMC included | 1-5% |
| **Intermediate risk** | Microscopic ETE, aggressive histology (tall cell, columnar cell, hobnail), vascular invasion (PTC), cN1 or >5 pN1 with all nodes <3 cm, RAI-avid uptake outside thyroid bed | 5-20% |
| **High risk** | Gross ETE, incomplete resection, distant metastases, pN1 >=3 cm, FTC with extensive vascular invasion (>4 foci) | 20-50%+ |

The intermediate category was acknowledged as heterogeneous. The 2015 text explicitly noted that within intermediate risk, recurrence estimates ranged from 4% (few micrometastatic nodes) to 22% (clinical N1 disease) -- a 5-fold range.

#### 2025 System: 4-Tier (R28)

The 2025 guideline splits the intermediate category into two:

| Category | Key Features | Estimated Recurrence |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Low risk** | Intrathyroidal DTC, no vascular invasion, no aggressive histology, unifocal or multifocal PTMC (no ETE), minimally invasive FTC (capsular invasion only or <4 vascular foci) | 1-5% |
| **Low-intermediate risk** | Microscopic ETE, limited vascular invasion, aggressive histology without other risk factors, small-volume lymph node disease (few micrometastatic nodes, smaller size) | 5-10% |
| **Intermediate-high risk** | Clinical N1 disease, multiple lymph node metastases, LNR >0.25-0.5, extranodal extension, macroscopic lymph node disease, aggressive histology with additional risk factors | 10-25% |
| **High risk** | Gross ETE, incomplete resection, distant metastases, large lymph node metastases (>2-3 cm), FTC with extensive vascular invasion (>=4 foci), BRAF+TERT co-mutation | 25-50%+ |

Additional 2025 refinements:

- OTC is now classified as a distinct entity (not a subset of FTC)
- Microscopically positive margins are discussed but not yet incorporated as a formal criterion (data mixed)
- Age is discussed but not included in recurrence prediction (conflicting data)
- BRAF+TERT co-mutation is formally recognized as high-risk

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

The 2025 guideline explicitly states:

1. **Heterogeneity within intermediate category.** The 2015 intermediate category included patients with 4% recurrence (few microscopic nodes) and patients with 22% recurrence (clinical N1 with macroscopic disease). This 5-fold range made the category clinically unhelpful for treatment decisions, particularly for RAI.

2. **Different treatment implications.** A patient with low-intermediate risk (e.g., microscopic ETE only) and a patient with intermediate-high risk (e.g., cN1 with extranodal extension) should receive different intensity of follow-up and different consideration for RAI. The 2015 system forced the same recommendation for both.

3. **LNR and extranodal extension data.** Multiple post-2015 studies validated LNR >0.25-0.5 and extranodal extension as independent predictors of recurrence, justifying a split within the intermediate category.

4. **AJCC 8th edition changes.** Removing microscopic ETE from T3 staging necessitated re-evaluation of where microscopic ETE falls in risk stratification (it is now low-intermediate, not firmly intermediate).

5. **Dynamic risk stratification validation.** Studies showed that response-to-therapy assessment has a PVE of 62-84%, far exceeding the 19-34% of the initial 3-tier system. This argues for a more nuanced initial classification that better aligns with the dynamic system.

**Rationale type:** Reinterpretation (documented heterogeneity within intermediate group) + New evidence (LNR, extranodal extension data, co-mutation data) + Methodology change (AJCC 8th edition).

**Confidence in rationale:** High.

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **Previously "intermediate-risk" patients with small-volume nodal disease:** Now classified as low-intermediate, they may not need RAI or aggressive TSH suppression. Impact: HIGH.
- **Previously "intermediate-risk" patients with macroscopic nodal disease/extranodal extension:** Now classified as intermediate-high, they get clearer guidance that RAI should be strongly considered. Impact: MODERATE.
- **Patients with BRAF+TERT co-mutation:** Formally placed in high-risk category regardless of other features. Impact: MODERATE (this was informally recognized in 2015 but now codified).

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Pathology reporting:** Ensure synoptic reports include number of lymph nodes examined, LNR, extranodal extension, and specific vascular invasion count (not just "present/absent"). These features drive the low-intermediate vs intermediate-high split.
2. **Tumor board:** When classifying risk, explicitly state whether a patient falls in low-intermediate or intermediate-high within the old intermediate category. This classification drives the RAI decision (see Workflow 5).
3. **Consider molecular testing:** If BRAF+TERT co-mutation status is available, incorporate it into the risk assessment. If not routinely obtained, consider obtaining when histology shows aggressive features.
4. **Electronic medical records:** Update risk stratification templates from 3-tier to 4-tier.

**Net effect:** Reduces uncertainty in the largest and most heterogeneous risk group, enables more precise treatment selection.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 5: RAI Decision-Making

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 Workflow (R51, Table 14)

- **Low-risk:** RAI not routinely recommended. (Weak/Low)
- **Intermediate-risk:** RAI "should be considered." Generally favored for many intermediate subgroups. (Weak/Low)
- **High-risk:** RAI routinely recommended. (Strong/Moderate)

The 2015 guideline had a detailed Table 14 with specific clinical scenarios (T1a, T1b-T2, T3 by size/ETE, N1a, N1b, T4, M1) and whether evidence supported RAI for survival or disease-free survival. Most intermediate-risk scenarios received "Consider -- generally favored."

#### 2025 Workflow (R32, Table 10)

- **Low-risk:** RAI "not recommended routinely." (Strong/High certainty) -- note the upgrade from Weak to Strong and from Low to High quality evidence
- **Low-intermediate and intermediate-high risk:** RAI "may be considered." (Conditional/Low)
- **High-risk:** RAI "routinely recommended." (Strong/Moderate)
- **Distant metastases:** RAI "routinely recommended." (Strong/Moderate)

#### Key Differences

| Feature | 2015 | 2025 |
|---------|------|------|
| Low-risk RAI recommendation strength | Weak/Low | Strong/High |
| Evidence base for low-risk no-RAI | Retrospective observational studies | Phase III RCT (ESTIMABL2) + HiLo RCT |
| Intermediate-risk framing | "Should be considered" | "May be considered" |
| Intermediate-risk granularity | Single category | Split into low-intermediate and intermediate-high |
| RAI preparation preference | rhTSH "can be used as alternative" | rhTSH "is preferred" (R34A) |
| Dosing for low-risk (if RAI used) | 30 mCi favored (R55) | 30-50 mCi (1.1-1.85 GBq) |
| Dosing for intermediate | Remnant ablation to 150 mCi | 30-100 mCi (1.1-3.7 GBq) |
| Dosing for high-risk | 100-200 mCi | 100-150 mCi (3.7-5.55 GBq); dosimetry recommended for >200 mCi |

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

1. **ESTIMABL2 Phase III RCT (776 patients).** This is the single most impactful new study. Low-risk DTC patients (pT1a N0 with sum of diameters >=10 mm, pT1b N0) were randomized to follow-up without RAI vs ablation with 1.1 GBq following rhTSH. The percentage without an event was 95.6% (no RAI) vs 95.9% (RAI), meeting non-inferiority criteria. The 2025 guideline states: "This Phase III clinical trial demonstrated noninferiority of a follow-up strategy when compared with systematic adjuvant postoperative administration of RAI in patients with low-risk DTC."

2. **HiLo RCT (438 patients, 6.5-year follow-up).** Low-activity RAI (1.1 GBq) was non-inferior to high-activity RAI (3.7 GBq): cumulative recurrence at 7 years was 5.9% vs 7.3% (HR 1.10, CI 0.47-2.59, p=0.83).

3. **NCDB intermediate-risk analysis (21,870 patients).** For intermediate-risk patients, RAI was associated with a 29% reduction in risk of death (HR 0.71, CI 0.62-0.82, p<0.001), supporting continued RAI consideration in this group.

4. **Splitting intermediate risk enables nuanced RAI decisions.** Low-intermediate patients (e.g., microscopic ETE only) have a recurrence risk of 5-10%, similar to the upper range of low-risk. RAI benefit for this subgroup is uncertain. Intermediate-high patients (e.g., cN1 with macroscopic disease) have a recurrence risk of 10-25%, where retrospective data suggest more benefit.

5. **Molecular predictors of RAI response.** The 2025 guideline notes that RAS mutations and RET fusions predict favorable RAI response, while BRAF+TERT and TP53 mutations predict RAI resistance. This information can be used to refine RAI decisions in the intermediate group.

**Rationale type:** New evidence (RCTs) for low-risk; new evidence (NCDB) + framework change (4-tier split) for intermediate-risk.

**Confidence in rationale:** High for low-risk (based on Phase III RCT). Moderate for intermediate (based on retrospective data and framework change).

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **Low-risk PTC after total thyroidectomy:** The shift from Weak to Strong recommendation against RAI, backed by an RCT, effectively ends the debate for this group. Any remaining use of RAI in low-risk patients now requires specific justification. Impact: HIGH.
- **Low-intermediate risk patients (formerly intermediate):** Some of these patients will no longer be offered RAI, as their risk profile is closer to low-risk. Impact: HIGH.
- **Intermediate-high risk patients:** RAI remains "may be considered" but with clearer recognition that this subgroup likely benefits. Impact: MODERATE.
- **All patients receiving RAI:** rhTSH preparation is now preferred over thyroid hormone withdrawal, improving quality of life during treatment. Impact: MODERATE.

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Nuclear medicine/endocrinology:** Stop routine RAI for low-risk DTC. The evidence level is now RCT-based. Document if RAI is being used in low-risk patients, and include the specific justification.
2. **RAI preparation:** Default to rhTSH preparation. Reserve thyroid hormone withdrawal for patients with known distant metastases or when rhTSH is unavailable.
3. **For intermediate-risk patients:** Use the 4-tier system. Low-intermediate patients can be observed; intermediate-high patients should be more strongly considered for RAI.
4. **Dosing:** For any RAI in low-risk (if used), use 30-50 mCi. For intermediate, use 30-100 mCi. Reserve doses >150 mCi for high-risk patients with known disease. Recommend dosimetry for doses >200 mCi.
5. **Post-therapy WBS:** Continue performing after RAI (R37, Strong/Moderate). Add SPECT/CT when available (R38, new recommendation).

**Net effect:** Reduces radiation exposure and side effects in low-risk patients; provides RCT-level evidence to support practice change; enables more selective RAI use in intermediate-risk patients.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 6: TSH Suppression

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 Workflow (R59, R70)

Initial TSH targets by risk category:

| Risk Category | Initial TSH Target | Duration |
|---------------|-------------------|----------|
| High risk | <0.1 mU/L (Strong/Moderate) | Indefinite if incomplete response; 5+ years then 0.1-0.5 for excellent response |
| Intermediate risk | 0.1-0.5 mU/L (Weak/Low) | Until excellent response achieved |
| Low risk, undetectable Tg | 0.5-2 mU/L (Weak/Low) | Ongoing |
| Low risk, detectable Tg | 0.1-0.5 mU/L (Weak/Low) | Until Tg undetectable |
| Low risk, post-lobectomy | 0.5-2 mU/L (Weak/Low) | Ongoing; thyroid hormone may not be needed |

Long-term TSH suppression (R70) was recommended stratified by response to therapy, with ongoing suppression to 0.1-0.5 for incomplete or indeterminate response.

#### 2025 Workflow (R45, R46)

- **R45:** "Individualization of decisions to initiate TSH suppression to below the reference range is recommended based on potential benefits and risks; recognizing that patients with high-risk disease may be more likely to benefit from a TSH in the subnormal range than those with low-risk disease." (Conditional/Low)
- **R46A:** "Long-term TSH suppression is not suggested for patients with low- or intermediate-risk disease who have no evidence of biochemical or structural recurrence." (Conditional/Low)
- **R46B:** "Risks versus benefits of TSH suppression and TSH goals should be re-evaluated over time." (GPS)

The 2025 TSH targets by response to therapy (Table 9):

| Response | TSH Goal |
|----------|---------|
| Excellent response | Normal reference range |
| Indeterminate response | Normal reference range |
| Biochemical incomplete | Below normal reference range |
| Structural incomplete | Below normal reference range |

#### Key Differences

| Feature | 2015 | 2025 |
|---------|------|------|
| High-risk initial TSH | <0.1 mU/L (Strong) | Individualized; subnormal may benefit (Conditional) |
| Low-risk TSH target | 0.5-2.0 mU/L | Normal reference range |
| Intermediate-risk initial TSH | 0.1-0.5 mU/L (Weak) | Normal reference range if excellent response |
| Long-term suppression | Not explicitly addressed as unnecessary | Explicitly not suggested for low/intermediate with no recurrence |
| Post-lobectomy TSH | 0.5-2 mU/L | Normal reference range; 70-80% can avoid thyroid hormone |
| Cardiovascular/bone risk language | Mentioned as adverse effects | More prominently weighed against benefits |

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

1. **NTCTCSG study (3,238 patients, 2015).** This study found improved overall survival and disease-free survival with moderate suppression (subnormal to normal TSH) but "no further improvement for any stage with TSH levels averaging in the undetectable subnormal range." The 2025 guideline explicitly cites this as evidence against aggressive suppression.

2. **No benefit in low-risk patients.** Multiple retrospective studies showed no recurrence benefit from TSH suppression in low-risk patients after lobectomy. One study of 1,528 lobectomy patients found only 1.4% recurrence over 5.6 years with no difference across TSH strata. Another study of 2,297 lobectomy patients found no impact of mean TSH on recurrence.

3. **Cardiovascular and bone morbidity.** The 2025 guideline cites a study of 771 low-to-intermediate risk patients showing that TSH <=0.4 was associated with increased risk of atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis despite similar recurrence rates. An observational study demonstrated increased cardiovascular mortality when TSH was <0.02 mU/L. The guideline states explicitly: "for patients at low-risk for recurrence, the risks of TSH suppression may exceed any potential benefit."

4. **Difficulty achieving targets.** The 2025 guideline notes real-world data showing only 29% of patients achieved target TSH despite good adherence. About 50% were overtreated and 20% undertreated. This practical problem argues for simpler, less aggressive targets.

5. **Lobectomy population growth.** As more patients undergo lobectomy, the TSH target question is especially relevant. The 2025 guideline explicitly states that if the goal is within normal range, 70-80% of lobectomy patients can avoid thyroid hormone supplementation entirely.

**Rationale type:** New evidence (NTCTCSG, multiple retrospective cohorts) + Outcome re-weighting (cardiovascular/bone morbidity) + Implementation feedback (difficulty achieving targets).

**Confidence in rationale:** High for low-risk patients. Moderate for high-risk patients (evidence is less definitive).

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **Low-risk patients after total thyroidectomy with excellent response:** TSH target relaxes from 0.5-2 to simply "normal range." Many patients overtreated with L-T4 can have doses reduced. Impact: MODERATE.
- **Low-risk patients after lobectomy:** 70-80% can potentially avoid thyroid hormone entirely. Impact: HIGH.
- **Intermediate-risk patients with excellent response:** No longer need ongoing TSH suppression below normal. Impact: MODERATE.
- **High-risk patients:** Still may benefit from subnormal TSH, but the recommendation is now Conditional rather than Strong, and undetectable TSH is no longer the target. Impact: MODERATE.
- **Older adults, patients with atrial fibrillation, postmenopausal women:** Explicit recognition that risks of suppression may outweigh benefits in these populations. Impact: HIGH for these subgroups.

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Endocrinologists:** For low-risk DTC patients with excellent response, target TSH within the normal reference range. Do not suppress. Review existing patients on long-term suppression and consider dose reduction.
2. **Post-lobectomy management:** Check TSH 6-8 weeks after surgery. If normal, do not start thyroid hormone. Only initiate L-T4 if TSH rises above reference range.
3. **High-risk patients:** Individualize. Subnormal TSH may still be reasonable but avoid undetectable levels. Reassess every 1-2 years.
4. **Re-evaluate over time:** The 2025 guideline explicitly recommends re-evaluation. Patients who have been on suppressive therapy for years and have sustained excellent response can have their doses reduced.
5. **Cardiovascular risk assessment:** Before initiating or continuing TSH suppression, assess for atrial fibrillation risk, osteoporosis, and ischemic heart disease.

**Net effect:** Reduces iatrogenic subclinical thyrotoxicosis and its complications (atrial fibrillation, osteoporosis, fracture), eliminates unnecessary thyroid hormone replacement in many lobectomy patients, simplifies management.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Workflow 7: Surveillance and Follow-Up

### Layer 1 -- What Changed

#### 2015 Workflow (R62-67)

- **Tg monitoring:** Every 6-12 months initially, with lengthened intervals (12-24 months) for excellent response (R62B-C). Stimulated Tg at 6-18 months post-ablation to verify excellent response (R63A). No endpoint for monitoring defined.
- **Post-lobectomy Tg:** "Periodic serum Tg measurements on thyroid hormone therapy should be considered." (R64, Strong/Low)
- **Neck ultrasound:** At 6-12 months post-surgery, then "periodically depending on the patient's risk for recurrent disease and Tg status." (R65A, Strong/Moderate). No endpoint defined.
- **Diagnostic WBS:** Not needed for low/intermediate-risk excellent response (R66). Consider for high/intermediate-risk (R67A).
- **"Remission" or "cure":** Not formally defined. No endpoint for surveillance discontinuation.

#### 2025 Workflow (R47-50)

- **Tg after total thyroidectomy:** Measure on thyroxine therapy to monitor response (R47B, Strong/Moderate). Every 6-12 months initially (R47C, GPS).
- **Tg after lobectomy:** "Not recommended routinely" (R47D, Conditional/Very Low). A single postoperative Tg at ~12 weeks is prudent (R30B, GPS), but routine serial monitoring is not recommended.
- **Neck ultrasound de-escalation (R48):**
  - After total thyroidectomy + RAI, sustained excellent response for 5-8 years: routine US can be discontinued.
  - After total thyroidectomy alone, sustained excellent response for 5-8 years: routine US can be discontinued.
  - After lobectomy: US every 1-3 years for 5-8 years, then can stop if no abnormalities.
- **Tg de-escalation (R48):**
  - After total thyroidectomy + RAI, sustained excellent response for 10-15 years: routine biochemical monitoring can be discontinued. Patient has achieved "complete remission."
  - After total thyroidectomy alone, sustained excellent response for 10-15 years: same.
  - After lobectomy: additional Tg testing not recommended routinely after a normal initial postoperative value.
- **"Complete remission" concept:** Formally introduced for the first time. Defined as sustained excellent response for 10-15 years.
- **Diagnostic WBS:** Not needed after lobectomy (R49, GPS). Not needed for low/low-intermediate risk excellent response (R50B, Conditional/Low). May be considered for intermediate-high/high risk with clinical suspicion (R50C, Conditional/Low).

#### Summary of the Shift

| Monitoring Modality | 2015 | 2025 |
|--------------------|------|------|
| Tg after total thyroidectomy | Indefinite, every 6-24 months | Can stop at 10-15 years (excellent response) |
| Tg after lobectomy | Periodic, indefinite (Strong) | Single postop value; routine serial monitoring NOT recommended (Conditional) |
| Neck US after total thyroidectomy | Periodic, indefinite | Can stop at 5-8 years (excellent response) |
| Neck US after lobectomy | Periodic, indefinite | Every 1-3 years for 5-8 years, then stop |
| Formal endpoint/remission concept | Not defined | "Complete remission" at 10-15 years |
| Stimulated Tg testing | For verification at 6-18 months | Not required if basal Tg undetectable on sensitive assay |

### Layer 2 -- Why It Changed

1. **Ultra-low recurrence rates after sustained excellent response.** The 2025 guideline cites multiple cohorts (>1000 patients, >10 years follow-up) showing <1.4% recurrence after 5-8 years of sustained excellent response. After 10 years, recurrence is exceptionally rare.

2. **False-positive US exceeds true recurrence rate.** In low-risk patients with excellent biochemical response, the false-positive rate of continued neck US may exceed the true recurrence rate. The 2025 guideline cites data showing that in patients with PTC found to be at low risk with excellent response, "there is a high incidence of false positive findings that may engender additional fruitless testing."

3. **Tg unreliable after lobectomy.** The 2025 guideline commissioned a systematic review that found "serum Tg measurement following partial thyroidectomy was not accurate for diagnosing DTC recurrence or metastasis." Two specific studies are cited: one of 208 patients where Tg did not differ between those with and without recurrence, and another of 167 patients where Tg rose in only 3 of 18 who recurred.

4. **Financial and psychological burden.** The 2025 guideline explicitly addresses the burden of lifelong surveillance: "Lifelong biochemical assessments and neck ultrasounds are associated with financial and psychological burden that may not be justified in many patients in view of the extremely low risk of recurrence long after treatment."

5. **Growing survivor population.** With over 800,000 thyroid cancer survivors in the US and rising, the resource implications of lifelong monitoring are substantial. The 2025 guideline positions de-escalation as both patient-centered and system-sustainable.

**Rationale type:** New evidence (long-term cohort data on low recurrence rates, systematic review on Tg after lobectomy) + De-escalation philosophy + Patient-centered (reducing burden) + Implementation feedback (false positives, resource use).

**Confidence in rationale:** High for total thyroidectomy + RAI patients (extensive data). Moderate for lobectomy patients (data more limited; the guideline acknowledges "a call for more research for this growing population").

### Layer 3 -- Clinical Impact

**Who is most affected:**

- **All low-risk DTC survivors with sustained excellent response >5 years:** Hundreds of thousands of patients currently in indefinite surveillance programs. Impact: HIGH.
- **Post-lobectomy patients:** A rapidly growing population who will no longer need routine serial Tg monitoring. Impact: HIGH.
- **Patients with cancer anxiety ("scanxiety"):** The formal introduction of "complete remission" at 10-15 years provides psychological closure. Impact: HIGH (quality of life).
- **Healthcare systems:** Reduced imaging and laboratory costs, fewer unnecessary FNA biopsies triggered by false-positive US, fewer clinic visits. Impact: HIGH (system-level).

**What to do differently Monday morning:**

1. **Review existing surveillance schedules.** Identify low-risk patients with excellent response who have been followed >5 years. Discontinue routine US. Continue Tg every 1-2 years until 10-15 years, then discuss stopping.
2. **Post-lobectomy patients:** Obtain a single Tg at ~12 weeks postop. If not markedly elevated, do not plan routine serial Tg. Follow with US every 1-3 years for 5-8 years.
3. **Introduce "complete remission" into clinical vocabulary.** At the 10-15 year mark for patients with sustained excellent response, explicitly tell the patient: "You have achieved complete remission. You no longer need routine thyroid cancer monitoring."
4. **Do NOT stop monitoring for:** Patients with indeterminate or incomplete response, patients with history of structural recurrence, intermediate-high or high-risk patients (monitor longer), or patients with rising TgAb.
5. **Document response category.** At each visit, formally document whether the patient has excellent, indeterminate, biochemically incomplete, or structurally incomplete response. This drives the de-escalation timeline.

**Net effect:** Massive reduction in healthcare utilization and patient burden for the largest group of DTC survivors, psychological benefit of formal "complete remission" designation, maintained safety through risk-stratified approach.

**Impact level:** HIGH

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. De-Escalation as a Central Philosophy

The 2025 guideline is fundamentally a de-escalation document. Across all seven workflows, the direction is consistent: less surgery, less RAI, less TSH suppression, less surveillance -- for appropriately selected patients. This is not driven by a single study but by a decade of converging evidence that the most common DTCs (small, low-risk PTCs) were being overtreated by a system designed for higher-risk disease.

### 2. Shift from "One-Size-Fits-All" to "Risk-Adapted"

The 2015 guideline was already risk-adapted, but the 2025 guideline pushes further by:
- Splitting intermediate risk into two tiers
- Creating a formal active surveillance pathway
- Introducing "complete remission" endpoints
- Tying every treatment decision to the 4-tier risk category plus dynamic response assessment

### 3. Evidence Grade Upgrades

Several key changes are backed by stronger evidence than was available in 2015:
- RAI in low-risk: Weak/Low (2015) to Strong/High (2025) -- driven by ESTIMABL2 RCT
- pCND recommendation: Weak/Low (2015) to Strong/Moderate (2025) -- driven by multiple meta-analyses
- These upgrades mean these recommendations are less discretionary and more directive.

### 4. Patient-Centered Framework

The 2025 guideline includes a patient advocate on the task force (a first), embeds shared decision-making in multiple recommendations, and introduces the DATA framework (Diagnosis, Assessment, Treatment, Assessment) that explicitly structures the patient journey.

### 5. Growing Lobectomy Population Creates New Questions

With lobectomy becoming the default for most T1-T2 tumors, the 2025 guideline must address a growing population for whom the traditional post-thyroidectomy surveillance paradigm (Tg, stimulated Tg, RAI scans) does not apply. The recommendation against routine Tg monitoring after lobectomy is an acknowledgment that the old tools do not work in this new context. This is an area where the guideline calls for more research.

## Cross-Validation Notes

The following corrections, nuances, and additional perspectives were identified during cross-validation by the methods-evidence-appraiser and the surgery-rai-risk-stratification-clinical agent.

### 1. Active Surveillance Existed in 2015 R12 -- Frame as Elevated, Not New

Active surveillance was NOT absent from the 2015 guideline. The 2015 R12 states: "an active surveillance management approach can be considered as an alternative to immediate surgery in (A) patients with very low risk tumors (e.g., papillary microcarcinomas without clinically evident metastases or local invasion), (B) patients at high surgical risk because of comorbid conditions, (C) patients expected to have a relatively short remaining life span, or (D) patients with concurrent medical or surgical issues."

The correct characterization is: Active surveillance was endorsed as "can be considered" in 2015, but it appeared in the nodule management section (R12), not as a DTC management recommendation; it was framed primarily for patients with surgical contraindications rather than as a universal option; and it lacked the standardized monitoring protocols, conversion triggers, and percutaneous ablation alternatives that the 2025 guideline (R11-14) provides. The 2025 change should be described as "elevated and expanded with protocol standardization," not as "newly added."

### 2. Compounding Effects: Lobectomy + No RAI + No TSH Suppression = Transformative

The cross-validation identified that treating each workflow change independently underestimates the combined impact. A patient diagnosed with a 1.5 cm PTC under the 2015 guideline might have received: total thyroidectomy, RAI, lifelong levothyroxine at suppressive doses, and indefinite Tg + US surveillance. Under the 2025 guideline, the same patient may receive: lobectomy only, no RAI, no thyroid hormone replacement (if TSH remains normal), and surveillance that can be discontinued after 5-8 years. This represents a transformative change in the patient experience that is greater than the sum of its individual workflow changes. The proportion of newly diagnosed DTC patients receiving RAI could decrease by 40-60% under full 2025 guideline implementation when combining lobectomy expansion (which makes patients ineligible for RAI) with the strengthened recommendation against RAI in low-risk patients.

### 3. Training Paradox for Central Neck Dissection (CND)

The recommendation against prophylactic CND for T1-T2 cN0 PTC (R19A, Strong/Moderate) creates a training paradox that neither the guideline nor the primary analysis addresses. If surgeons no longer perform routine prophylactic CND, they will have dramatically fewer opportunities to learn and practice central neck dissection. When they encounter a patient who DOES need therapeutic CND (cN1a), they will have less operative experience. The guideline itself hints at this: "the benefits of pCND should be weighed against the risks, especially when a surgeon's experience is limited." This creates a self-reinforcing cycle -- the guideline discourages routine pCND partly because complication rates are higher with less experienced surgeons, but discouraging routine pCND ensures that surgeons remain less experienced. Implications include: changes to surgical residency/fellowship training curricula, potential concentration of CND procedures at high-volume centers, and credentialing considerations.

### 4. Implementation Barriers for the 4-Tier Risk System

The transition from 3-tier to 4-tier risk stratification requires substantial institutional infrastructure changes that the impact analysis should not underestimate:

- Updating electronic medical record templates and order sets
- Retraining tumor board participants on new tier definitions
- Revising pathology synoptic reports to include the features that drive the new tier boundaries (node size >2mm, node count, extranodal extension, vascular invasion count)
- Developing institutional protocols that map the 4 tiers to treatment pathways (especially for the RAI decision in low-intermediate vs intermediate-high)
- Re-stratifying existing patients currently in follow-up under the old 3-tier system

The 2025 guideline acknowledges that the cutoffs between low-intermediate and intermediate-high are not precisely defined (Figure 2 footnote: "No clear cutoffs for LNs between low-intermediate and high-intermediate risk groups"). This ambiguity will create inter-institutional variability that may undermine the purpose of a standardized risk system, at least in the near term.

### 5. Low-Risk TSH Change Should Be Rated HIGH Impact (Combined Effect)

The impact rating for the low-risk TSH change (currently MODERATE in the summary table) should be HIGH when the compounding effect is considered. The combined effect of (a) more lobectomies and (b) no TSH suppression for low-risk disease means that the majority of newly diagnosed low-risk DTC patients may need neither total thyroidectomy, nor RAI, nor lifelong thyroid hormone. The 2025 guideline explicitly states that "70-80% of patients who undergo lobectomy can avoid thyroid hormone supplementation" if the TSH goal is within the normal range. This is transformative for patient quality of life and represents a fundamental change in the DTC treatment paradigm.

### 6. Tg After Lobectomy: Growing Importance of the Reversal

The impact rating for the Tg-after-lobectomy reversal (currently MODERATE) deserves reconsideration as HIGH for forward-looking implementation. Given that lobectomy will become the dominant procedure for T1 disease and the proportion of DTC patients managed with lobectomy is expected to increase substantially, the population affected by this recommendation will grow rapidly. The shift from "Periodic serum Tg measurements should be considered" (Strong, Low) to "not recommended routinely" (Conditional, Very low) is a genuine reversal. As the lobectomy population grows, the absolute number of patients affected grows proportionally. Additionally, the loss of Tg as a surveillance tool after lobectomy creates a gap in the monitoring paradigm that is not yet filled -- making this an area of active clinical uncertainty.
