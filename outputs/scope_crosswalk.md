## 0. Executive Scope Summary

| Attribute | 2015 Guideline | 2025 Guideline |
|---|---|---|
| Full title | 2015 ATA Management Guidelines for Adult Patients with Thyroid Nodules and Differentiated Thyroid Cancer | 2025 ATA Management Guidelines for Adult Patients with Differentiated Thyroid Cancer |
| Scope | Thyroid nodules AND DTC | DTC only (including NIFTP and FUMP) |
| Total recommendations | 101 (R1-R101) | 84 (R1-R84) |
| Nodule recommendations | R1-R31 (31 recommendations) | None -- excluded by design |
| DTC recommendations | R32-R101 (70 recommendations) | R1-R84 (84 recommendations) |
| Grading system | ACP Guideline Grading System (Strong/Weak, High/Moderate/Low quality) + custom diagnostic test system | Modified GRADE (Strong/Conditional, High/Moderate/Low/Very Low certainty) + Good Practice Statements (GPS) |
| Patient population | Adults (>18-20 y) with thyroid nodules or DTC | Adults (>18 y) with DTC, NIFTP, or FUMP |
| Staging system referenced | AJCC 7th edition (with 8th edition preview) | AJCC 8th edition |
| WHO classification | 4th edition (2004) | 5th edition (2022/2025 update) |
| Starting point | Incidentally discovered or palpable thyroid nodule | Established or near-certain diagnosis of thyroid cancer |
| Key new framework | ATA initial risk stratification system (3 tiers: low, intermediate, high) | DATA framework (Diagnosis, Assessment, Treatment, Assessment); 4-tier risk (low, low-intermediate, intermediate-high, high) |

### Stated Reason for Scope Split

The 2025 guideline **explicitly states** in its Methods section (p. 845):

> "Due to the expansion of knowledge concerning the management of thyroid nodules and DTC, a decision was made to separate the topics into two sets of updated guidelines."

The Aim and Target Audience section (p. 845) further states:

> "Compared with prior guidelines, this document applies only to patients with DTC, including individuals diagnosed with noninvasive follicular tumors with papillary-like nuclear features (NIFTP) and follicular tumors with uncertain malignant potential (FUMP), extremely low-risk lesions with diagnosis possible only after surgical excision."

The Introduction (p. 844) also clarifies that the 2025 DTC guidelines "begin with a certain or near-certain diagnosis of thyroid cancer" rather than with a thyroid nodule, marking a philosophical shift in the starting point of clinical care.

## 2. Scope Shift Analysis

### 2.1 Thyroid Nodule Content (2015 R1-R31): Excluded by Design

**What changed:** 31 of 101 recommendations in 2015 (R1-R31, approximately 30% of the guideline) addressed thyroid nodule evaluation, FNA cytology, molecular testing of indeterminate nodules, benign nodule management, and nodules in pregnancy. None of these have a direct counterpart in 2025.

**Why it cannot be directly compared:** The 2025 guideline begins its clinical pathway at the point of cancer diagnosis, not at the point of nodule discovery. These are fundamentally different starting points for clinical decision-making.

**Does 2025 explain this?** Yes. The Methods section explicitly states the decision to separate topics was due to "expansion of knowledge." The Aim section confirms the document "applies only to patients with DTC." The Introduction further clarifies the starting point as "a certain or near-certain diagnosis of thyroid cancer on preoperative FNA testing (Bethesda VI cytology and/or molecular results with high certainty of malignancy) as reviewed in the thyroid nodule guidelines, or after initial surgery."

**What the scope change means for interpretation:**
- The absence of nodule evaluation content in 2025 does NOT mean the ATA has changed its position on thyroid nodule management.
- A separate ATA thyroid nodule guideline is anticipated/under development.
- Any analyst comparing "total number of recommendations" between 2015 and 2025 must exclude the nodule recommendations (R1-R31) from 2015 to avoid inflating apparent "removed" recommendations.
- The 2015 nodule recommendations remain the most recent ATA guidance on nodule management until the separate nodule guideline is published.

**Philosophical shift:** The separation represents a recognition that nodule evaluation and DTC management are distinct clinical workflows with different evidence bases, different specialist involvement, and different patient populations. A patient with a thyroid nodule is not yet a cancer patient; the 2025 guideline respects this distinction.

### 2.2 Active Surveillance (2025 R11-R14): New Formal Section

**What changed:** 2015 mentioned active surveillance only in the Future Research section [D3], citing Japanese data on papillary microcarcinoma surveillance. 2025 creates a full formal section with 4 recommendations covering eligibility criteria, monitoring protocols, Tg/TgAb monitoring during surveillance, and criteria for intervention.

**Why:** Significant post-2015 evidence from Japanese and Korean prospective studies validated active surveillance for small, low-risk PTC. The 2025 guideline also introduces percutaneous ablation as an alternative to surgery for selected small tumors (R11B), reflecting newer ablation technologies.

**Does 2025 explain this?** Yes. The introduction states that "prior guidelines introduced criteria to enhance initial decision-making" and that new evidence has accumulated to support these approaches.

### 2.3 Genetic Counseling and Germline Testing (2025 R2-R5): Major Expansion

**What changed:** 2015 had only R1 (no recommendation, insufficient evidence for screening familial DTC). 2025 creates 4 new recommendations on germline genetic testing (R2), FNMTC testing (R3), family screening (R4), and somatic-to-germline testing (R5).

**Why:** Advances in understanding hereditary thyroid cancer syndromes, availability of multigene panel testing, and inclusion of a cancer genetics specialist on the 2025 task force (Pamela L. Brock) all contributed.

**Does 2025 explain this?** The addition of a cancer genetics expert to the task force is noted in the author listing.

### 2.4 Risk Stratification System: 3-Tier to 4-Tier

**What changed:** 2015 ATA risk system had 3 tiers (low, intermediate, high risk of recurrence). 2025 introduces a 4-tier system (low <10%, low-intermediate 10-15%, intermediate-high 16-30%, high >30%) with separate criteria tables for PTC, FTC/IEFVPTC, and OTC.

**Why:** The 2015 "intermediate" category was heterogeneous, containing patients with very different recurrence risks. Splitting it provides better discrimination. Additionally, WHO 5th edition classification of OTC as a separate entity required OTC-specific risk criteria.

**Does 2025 explain this?** The risk stratification section (R28, Table 6, Fig. 2) provides the new framework with detailed justification.

### 2.5 RAIR DTC Systemic Therapy: Massive Expansion

**What changed:** 2015 had approximately 6 recommendations on systemic therapy (R93, R96-R100). 2025 has approximately 14 recommendations (R61-R75) covering mutation-specific targeted therapy, immunotherapy, redifferentiation, and detailed MKI management.

**Why:** Multiple new FDA-approved targeted agents (larotrectinib, entrectinib, selpercatinib, pralsetinib, dabrafenib+trametinib) have become available since 2015. The era of precision oncology in thyroid cancer began largely after 2015.

**Does 2025 explain this?** Yes. The introduction notes "major advances in understanding the molecular causes of thyroid cancer development and progression that have created newly approved treatment options for subsets of patients."

### 2.6 Survivorship and Patient-Reported Outcomes: New Section

**What changed:** 2015 had no formal survivorship recommendations. 2025 adds R82-R84 covering long-term treatment side effects, financial toxicity, and psychosocial concerns.

**Why:** 2015 listed survivorship as a future research priority [D9]. The 2025 task force included a patient advocate (Gary Bloom, ThyCa) as a full voting member for the first time, leading to formal attention to patient-centered outcomes.

**Does 2025 explain this?** Yes. The Introduction states: "we provide specific sections regarding survivorship concerns from patient advocates and point to areas where patient-reported outcomes research is needed."

### 2.7 Evidence Grading Methodology Change

**What changed:** 2015 used the ACP Guideline Grading System (Strong/Weak with High/Moderate/Low quality evidence) plus a custom diagnostic test grading system. 2025 uses modified GRADE (Strong/Conditional with High/Moderate/Low/Very Low certainty) plus Good Practice Statements (GPS).

**Why:** GRADE is now the international standard for clinical guideline development. The 2025 change aligns with best practices. The introduction of GPS provides a mechanism for common-sense recommendations where direct evidence is lacking but benefits are obvious.

**Does 2025 explain this?** Yes. The Methods section details the GRADE approach and GPS criteria.

**Impact on comparison:** Recommendation strength labels are NOT directly mappable between guidelines. "Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence" (2015) is NOT the same framework as "Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence" (2025), even though they may sound similar. This is a comparison trap (see Section 4).

### 2.8 DATA Framework: New Conceptual Overlay

**What changed:** 2015 organized recommendations by clinical workflow (nodule evaluation -> surgery -> RAI -> follow-up). 2025 introduces the DATA framework (Diagnosis, Assessment, Treatment, Assessment) as a cyclical, patient-journey-centered model.

**Why:** The DATA framework reflects a shift from a linear treatment algorithm to an iterative, personalized management approach. It emphasizes repeated reassessment and shared decision-making.

**Impact on comparison:** The structural organization of recommendations differs between guidelines. A recommendation that appears in the "initial management" section of 2015 may appear in a different structural context in 2025 (e.g., RAI side effects moved from long-term management to the RAI administration section).

## 4. Comparison Traps List

The following are specific traps that could create false conclusions when comparing the 2015 and 2025 guidelines. Downstream agents MUST be aware of these.

### Trap 1: Counting "Removed" Recommendations from Nodule Section
**Risk:** Concluding that 31 recommendations were "removed" when in fact they are out of scope.
**Reality:** 2015 R1-R31 (thyroid nodule recommendations) were not removed from DTC guidance; they were separated into a forthcoming standalone thyroid nodule guideline.
**Rule:** When counting recommendation changes, compare only 2015 R32-R101 (70 DTC recs) against 2025 R1-R84 (84 DTC recs).

### Trap 2: "Weak" (2015) vs "Conditional" (2025) Appears as a Downgrade
**Risk:** Interpreting a change from "Strong recommendation" in 2015 to "Conditional recommendation" in 2025 as a downgrade, when it may simply reflect the different grading methodology.
**Reality:** The ACP system (2015) and GRADE system (2025) use different terminology and thresholds. "Conditional" in GRADE is the closest analog to "Weak" in ACP, but they are not identical.
**Rule:** Always note the grading system change when comparing recommendation strength. True downgrades require examination of the stated rationale.

### Trap 3: "Low-quality evidence" (2015) vs "Low certainty evidence" (2025) Appears Unchanged
**Risk:** Assuming evidence quality is unchanged because both say "Low."
**Reality:** The 2015 ACP system and the 2025 GRADE system define "Low" differently. GRADE also introduces "Very Low," which has no ACP equivalent.
**Rule:** Do not equate evidence quality labels across grading systems without checking the specific criteria.

### Trap 4: Good Practice Statements (GPS) Look Like Downgrades
**Risk:** Interpreting a GPS in 2025 as weaker than a "Strong recommendation" in 2015.
**Reality:** GPS in the 2025 framework is EQUIVALENT to a strong recommendation in terms of clinical applicability ("applicable to all or nearly all persons or situations; not following a GPS would be considered outside of usual clinical practice"). It is used when direct evidence is lacking but benefits are obvious and consensus is unanimous.
**Rule:** Treat GPS as functionally equivalent to strong recommendations for clinical comparison purposes.

### Trap 5: Relocated RAI Side Effect Recommendations Appear "New"
**Risk:** Concluding that RAI side effect recommendations (2025 R39-R43) are new when they are actually relocated from the long-term management section of 2015 (R83-R90).
**Reality:** 2015 discussed RAI side effects in [C32-C35] (R83-R90). 2025 moves this content to be adjacent to RAI administration recommendations (R39-R43), which is a structural reorganization, not new content.
**Rule:** Check the recommendation numbering map before concluding content is new.

### Trap 6: "Intermediate Risk" (2015) Maps to Two Categories (2025)
**Risk:** Directly comparing outcomes or thresholds for 2015 "intermediate risk" patients with 2025 patients.
**Reality:** The 2015 intermediate-risk category was split into "low-intermediate" (10-15%) and "intermediate-high" (16-30%) in 2025. Any recommendation that referenced "intermediate risk" in 2015 now has TWO potentially different recommendations in 2025.
**Rule:** For any 2015 recommendation mentioning "intermediate risk," check BOTH 2025 low-intermediate and intermediate-high categories.

### Trap 7: OTC (Oncocytic/Hurthle Cell) Recommendations Appear "New"
**Risk:** Concluding that OTC-specific recommendations are entirely new.
**Reality:** 2015 treated oncocytic (Hurthle cell) carcinoma as a subtype of FTC. 2025 follows WHO 5th edition in classifying OTC as a separate DTC entity with its own risk stratification criteria. Some OTC-specific language is genuinely new, but some is reclassification of existing guidance.
**Rule:** When encountering OTC-specific recommendations in 2025, check whether the content was previously covered under FTC in 2015.

### Trap 8: Surgery Thresholds Appear Dramatically Different
**Risk:** Concluding that surgery recommendations changed dramatically because 2015 used size cutoffs (1 cm, 4 cm) and 2025 uses clinical staging (T1-T4, N, M).
**Reality:** While the FRAMING changed significantly, many of the actual surgical decisions map similarly (e.g., 2015 ">4 cm or gross ETE or cN1 -> total thyroidectomy" is similar to 2025 "cT3-4 or cN1 or cM1 -> total thyroidectomy"). However, there ARE genuine changes: 2025 favors lobectomy more broadly for cT2N0M0 (2-4 cm tumors) compared to 2015.
**Rule:** Compare actual surgical decisions for specific clinical scenarios, not just the categorical labels.

### Trap 9: Active Surveillance Appears as a "New" Concept
**Risk:** Stating that active surveillance was "introduced" in 2025.
**Reality:** The 2015 guideline acknowledged active surveillance in the discussion text of [D3] (Future Research) based on Japanese data. The concept was present but NOT formalized into recommendations. 2025 formalizes it into R11-R14.
**Rule:** Describe this as "elevation from future research discussion to formal recommendations" rather than "newly introduced."

### Trap 10: Recommendations That Look "Unchanged" but Are in a Different Structural Context
**Risk:** Assuming that because the wording of a recommendation is similar, the clinical implications are identical.
**Reality:** A recommendation for TSH suppression that appears in the context of 3-tier risk stratification (2015) means something different when it appears in the context of 4-tier risk stratification (2025), because the "intermediate" patients it previously applied to have been split into two groups with potentially different TSH targets. Similarly, RAI recommendations that are worded similarly but are now in the context of a 4-tier system may apply to different patient populations.
**Rule:** Always check the risk stratification context when comparing apparently identical recommendations.

### Trap 11: 2015 R1 (Familial DTC Screening) Appears "Replaced" by 2025 R2-R5
**Risk:** Mapping 2015 R1 directly to 2025 R2 and concluding the recommendation was strengthened.
**Reality:** 2015 R1 asked "should we screen family members?" (answer: insufficient evidence). 2025 R2-R5 ask different questions: "which patients should get germline testing?", "should FNMTC families get tested?", "should family members be screened?", and "when should somatic findings trigger germline testing?" These are related but distinct clinical questions reflecting the maturation of precision medicine.
**Rule:** Note that this is an EXPANSION of the clinical question, not just a recommendation change.

### Trap 12: Counting "84 recommendations in 2025 vs 101 in 2015" as a Reduction
**Risk:** Concluding that the field simplified or that recommendations were removed.
**Reality:** 2015 had 70 DTC recommendations (R32-R101) and 31 nodule recommendations. 2025 has 84 DTC recommendations. This represents a NET INCREASE of 14 DTC recommendations (+20%). The total number appears lower only because nodule recommendations were separated.
**Rule:** Compare DTC-to-DTC recommendation counts only.

### Trap 13: DHGTC (Differentiated High-Grade Thyroid Carcinoma) Is a New Entity
**Risk:** Failing to recognize that some patients previously classified as "poorly differentiated" or "aggressive DTC variant" are now classified as DHGTC.
**Reality:** WHO 5th edition introduced DHGTC as a formal category. Some 2015 recommendations for "poorly differentiated" or "aggressive variants" now apply to DHGTC patients in the 2025 framework. DHGTC has specific risk stratification implications in the 2025 4-tier system.
**Rule:** When comparing high-risk categories, note the new DHGTC classification and its implications.

### Trap 14: 2025 "Consolidated" Recommendations May Obscure Changes
**Risk:** When 2015 had multiple separate recommendations (e.g., R62-R64 on serum Tg) and 2025 consolidates them into one (R47), individual sub-recommendations may have changed direction without being obvious.
**Rule:** For consolidated recommendations, compare each sub-component individually.

## 6. Key Structural Differences Affecting All Downstream Analysis

1. **Starting point shift:** 2015 begins at thyroid nodule; 2025 begins at cancer diagnosis. This changes the universe of patients to whom recommendations apply.

2. **Grading system change:** ACP (2015) to GRADE (2025). All recommendation strength comparisons must account for this methodological difference.

3. **Risk stratification granularity:** 3-tier (2015) to 4-tier (2025). Every risk-stratified recommendation must be re-analyzed in the context of the new tier definitions.

4. **WHO classification update:** 4th edition (2015) to 5th edition (2025). OTC is now a separate entity; DHGTC is new; NIFTP is formalized. All pathology-related recommendations are affected.

5. **AJCC staging update:** 7th edition (2015, with 8th edition preview) to 8th edition (2025). Age cutoff changed from 45 to 55 years; microscopic extrathyroidal extension removed from T3.

6. **Precision oncology expansion:** 2015 had generic kinase inhibitor recommendations; 2025 has mutation-specific treatment algorithms (NTRK, RET, ALK, BRAF).

7. **Patient-centered additions:** 2025 adds survivorship (R82), financial toxicity (R83), and psychosocial concerns (R84) as formal recommendations, reflecting inclusion of patient advocate on task force.

8. **Consolidation pattern:** 2025 tends to consolidate multiple related 2015 recommendations into fewer, more comprehensive recommendations. This makes point-by-point comparison challenging but reflects a more integrated clinical approach.
