# 1. ACTIVE SURVEILLANCE COHORT STUDIES FOR LOW-RISK THYROID MICROCARCINOMA

---

## 1A. Ito et al. (Kuma Hospital) -- Landmark 2003 Original Observation Trial

- **PMID**: 12804106
- **Citation**: Ito Y, Uruno T, Nakano K, et al. Thyroid. 2003;13(4):381-387.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/105072503321669875](https://doi.org/10.1089/105072503321669875)
- **Key finding**: First report of observation trial for PTMC at Kuma Hospital (1993-2001); 162 patients observed -- >70% of tumors did not change or decreased in size; enlargement >10mm in only 10.2%; LN metastasis appeared in only 1.2% during follow-up.
- **Supports recommendation**: Active surveillance as a safe first-line management option for low-risk PTMC (new in ATA 2015, strengthened in 2025).
- **Full text available**: No (journal article, not in PMC)
- **Data source**: Abstract

---

## 1B. Ito et al. (Kuma Hospital) -- 2010 Expanded Observation Trial (340 patients)

- **PMID**: 20020290
- **Citation**: Ito Y, Miyauchi A, Inoue H, et al. World J Surg. 2010;34(1):28-35.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00268-009-0303-0](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00268-009-0303-0)
- **Key finding**: 340 patients under observation (avg 74 months): 5-year enlargement rate 6.4%, 10-year 15.9%; novel nodal metastasis 1.4% at 5 years, 3.4% at 10 years. None of 109 patients who later underwent surgery showed recurrence. No factors predicted progression.
- **Supports recommendation**: Confirms safety of AS; progression rates remain low even at 10 years; delayed surgery does not compromise outcomes.
- **Full text available**: No (Springer, not open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract

---

## 1C. Miyauchi & Ito (Kuma Hospital) -- 30-Year Experience (5,646 patients, 2023)

- **PMID**: 37166389
- **Citation**: Miyauchi A, Ito Y, Fujishima M, et al. Thyroid. 2023;33(7):817-825.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2023.0076](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2023.0076)
- **Key finding**: 30-year data from Kuma Hospital: 3,222 patients AS vs 2,424 immediate surgery. 10-year and 20-year tumor enlargement rates were 4.7% and 6.6%; LN metastasis 1.0% and 1.6%. None died of thyroid carcinoma. AS required significantly fewer surgeries overall.
- **Supports recommendation**: Definitive evidence that AS is a viable long-term management strategy; no mortality difference between AS and immediate surgery over 20+ years.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC7354707 -- PMC available)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text available via PMC

## 1D. Ito & Miyauchi (Kuma Hospital) -- AS as First-Line Management Review (2019)

- **PMID**: 30230964
- **Citation**: Ito Y, Miyauchi A. Annu Rev Med. 2019;70:369-379.
- **DOI**: [10.1146/annurev-med-051517-125510](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-med-051517-125510)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive review of both Japanese hospitals' AS data since mid-1990s. Most PMCs grew very slowly or did not grow; none showed distant metastasis or died of thyroid carcinoma during AS. AS is safer and less costly than immediate surgery.
- **Supports recommendation**: AS should be first-line for low-risk PMC; incorporated into guidelines in Japan (2010) and US (ATA 2015).
- **Full text available**: No (Annual Reviews, subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 1E. Sugitani et al. (Cancer Institute Hospital, Tokyo) -- 2010 Prospective Trial

- **PMID**: 20066418
- **Citation**: Sugitani I, Toda K, Yamada K, et al. World J Surg. 2010;34(6):1222-1231.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00268-009-0359-x](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00268-009-0359-x)
- **Key finding**: Proposed three types of PTMC requiring different treatment. As of 2008, 230/244 candidates accepted AS. After mean 5 years, 7% had tumor increase, 90% unchanged, 3% decreased. No distant metastasis. 1% developed apparent LN metastasis. Type I (asymptomatic) PMC is harmless and suitable for observation.
- **Supports recommendation**: Second major Japanese center confirming safety of AS; provides classification framework for PTMC management.
- **Full text available**: No (Springer, not open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 1F. Sugitani et al. -- Multifocality During AS (571 patients, Cancer Institute Hospital, 2021)

- **PMID**: 34100116
- **Citation**: Nagaoka R, Ebina A, Toda K, ..., Sugitani I. World J Surg. 2021;45(9):2769-2776.
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00268-021-06185-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00268-021-06185-2)
- **Key finding**: 571 patients under AS (mean 7.6 years). 10-year progression rate 13.1%. Multifocality was NOT a significant risk factor for progression (OR 1.45, p=0.22). Even patients with multiple PTMCs are good candidates for AS.
- **Supports recommendation**: Expands AS eligibility criteria; multifocality alone should not exclude patients from AS.
- **Full text available**: No (Springer)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 1H. Leboulleux, Tuttle, Pacini, Schlumberger -- Western Perspective on AS (Lancet D&E, 2016)

- **PMID**: 27550849
- **Citation**: Leboulleux S, Tuttle RM, Pacini F, Schlumberger M. Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol. 2016;4(11):933-942.
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S2213-8587(16)30180-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587(16)30180-2)
- **Key finding**: Key Western perspective arguing for shift from surgery to AS in PTMC. Low-risk PTMC has 1-5% recurrence rate; surgery carries 1-3% risk of permanent complications. AS with curative intent should be considered in properly selected patients.
- **Supports recommendation**: Western adoption of AS paradigm; Tuttle (MSKCC) co-authored, providing critical validation for non-Japanese populations.
- **Full text available**: No (Lancet, subscription)

## 1J. Molinaro, Tuttle, Elisei et al. -- Italian AS Experience (JCEM, 2020)

- **PMID**: 31652318
- **Citation**: Molinaro E, Campopiano MC, ..., Tuttle RM, Elisei R. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2020;105(3):e172-180.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/clinem/dgz113](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgz113)
- **Key finding**: First Italian prospective AS study (93 patients enrolled of 185 eligible, 50.3% acceptance rate). Only 3% showed clinical progression during median 19 months follow-up. All operated patients had excellent response despite delayed surgery.
- **Supports recommendation**: Demonstrates AS feasibility in Western/European context; Tuttle co-authored, extending MSKCC influence internationally.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC8105780)

## 1K. Lee et al. -- Korean Multicenter Prospective AS Cohort (KoMPASS Interim, 2022)

- **PMID**: 36205563
- **Citation**: Lee EK, Moon JH, Hwangbo Y, et al. Thyroid. 2022;32(11):1328-1336.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2021.0614](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2021.0614)
- **Key finding**: Korean multicenter prospective study: 755 patients chose AS, 422 surgery. 5-year progression rate 14.2% (slightly higher than Japanese data). Risk factors: age <30 (OR 2.86), male sex (OR 2.48), tumor >=6mm (OR 1.89). No distant metastases during AS.
- **Supports recommendation**: First large Korean prospective AS data; validates AS safety while identifying population-specific risk factors.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC9700369)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 1L. Jeon et al. -- Korean Delayed Surgery Outcomes (Asan/Kwon cohort, 2017)

- **PMID**: 28432268
- **Citation**: Jeon MJ, Kim WG, Kwon H, et al. Eur J Endocrinol. 2017;177(1):25-31.
- **DOI**: [10.1530/EJE-17-0160](https://doi.org/10.1530/EJE-17-0160)
- **Key finding**: 2,863 Korean PTMC patients: delayed surgery (>12 months) was NOT associated with higher structural recurrence/persistent disease compared to immediate surgery (p=0.34). Disease-free survival did not differ by delay period.
- **Supports recommendation**: Delayed surgery in PTMC is safe; supports AS approach by showing no harm from watchful waiting before surgery.
- **Full text available**: No (Bioscientifica)
- **Data source**: Abstract

# 2. TSH SUPPRESSION -- CARDIOVASCULAR AND BONE MORBIDITY

---

## 2A. Cooper et al. -- NTCTCSG Registry: TSH Suppression and Disease Progression (1998)

- **PMID**: 9777742
- **Citation**: Cooper DS, Specker B, Ho M, et al. Thyroid. 1998;8(9):737-744.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.1998.8.737](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.1998.8.737)
- **Key finding**: LANDMARK NTCTCSG study (617 papillary + 66 follicular patients). TSH score category (undetectable vs subnormal vs normal) did NOT independently predict disease progression in low-risk patients. Greater degrees of TSH suppression are NOT required for low-risk patients.
- **Supports recommendation**: De-escalation of TSH suppression in low-risk patients; move from undetectable to subnormal/low-normal TSH targets.
- **Full text available**: No (journal article)
- **Data source**: Abstract

---

## 2B. Carhill et al. -- NTCTCSG Registry 1987-2012 Update (4,941 patients)

- **PMID**: 26171797
- **Citation**: Carhill AA, Litofsky DR, Ross DS, ..., Sherman SI. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2015;100(9):3270-3279.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/JC.2015-1346](https://doi.org/10.1210/JC.2015-1346)
- **Key finding**: Updated NTCTCSG analysis (4,941 patients, median 6-year FU). MODERATE TSH suppression (subnormal-normal) was associated with improved OS and DFS across all stages. NO additional survival benefit from AGGRESSIVE suppression (undetectable-subnormal), even in patients who developed distant metastatic disease during follow-up.
- **Supports recommendation**: Definitive evidence against aggressive TSH suppression; moderate suppression sufficient. Directly supports ATA 2025 de-escalation of TSH targets.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC5393522)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

---

## 2C. Klein Hesselink et al. -- Increased AF Risk After DTC Treatment (JCEM, 2015)

- **PMID**: 26480284
- **Citation**: Klein Hesselink EN, Lefrandt JD, Schuurmans EP, et al. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2015;100(12):4563-4569.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/jc.2015-2782](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2015-2782)
- **Key finding**: 518 DTC patients vs 1,563 matched controls: DTC patients had 2.47-fold increased AF risk (95% CI 1.55-3.95). AF incidence 6.2/1000 person-years (vs 2.7 controls). TSH level itself was NOT associated with AF; higher cumulative RAI dose slightly increased AF risk.
- **Supports recommendation**: DTC patients have increased AF risk; supports less aggressive TSH suppression to reduce cardiovascular morbidity.
- **Full text available**: No (JCEM, subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 2D. Yang et al. -- Meta-Analysis: TSH Suppression and Cardiovascular Events (2022)

- **PMID**: 36619576
- **Citation**: Yang X, Guo N, Gao X, et al. Front Endocrinol. 2022;13:991876.
- **DOI**: [10.3389/fendo.2022.991876](https://doi.org/10.3389/fendo.2022.991876)
- **Key finding**: Meta-analysis of 14 studies: TSH suppression therapy increases AF risk (RR 1.52, 95% CI 1.28-1.81). Also associated with higher heart rate and blood pressure. No significant difference in ejection fraction.
- **Supports recommendation**: Quantifies AF risk from TSH suppression; supports risk-benefit reassessment and de-escalation of TSH targets.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC9814721)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

---

## 2E. Yu et al. -- Systematic Review: Cardiovascular Outcomes of Long-Term TSH Suppression (2023)

- **PMID**: 37295414
- **Citation**: Yu J, Kaur R, Ayeni FE, et al. Horm Metab Res. 2023;55(6):379-387.
- **DOI**: [10.1055/a-2084-3408](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-2084-3408)
- **Key finding**: 195,879 DTC patients (median 8.6-year FU). Increased risk of AF (HR 1.58), stroke (HR 1.14), and all-cause mortality (HR 2.04). No difference in heart failure, IHD, or CV mortality. TSH suppression must be titrated to balance cancer recurrence risk and cardiovascular morbidity.
- **Supports recommendation**: Large-scale evidence for cardiovascular harm from TSH suppression; directly supports de-escalation in low-risk patients.
- **Full text available**: No (Thieme, subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 2F. Biondi & Cooper -- Risk-Benefit of TSH Suppression Review (2010)

- **PMID**: 20151821
- **Citation**: Biondi B, Cooper DS. Thyroid. 2010;20(2):135-146.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2009.0311](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2009.0311)
- **Key finding**: Proposed 9-category risk-stratified approach balancing DTC recurrence risk against adverse effects of subclinical hyperthyroidism (AF, osteoporosis, increased LV mass). More aggressive suppression for high-risk disease; less aggressive for low-risk patients with cardiovascular/skeletal risk factors.
- **Supports recommendation**: Framework for individualized TSH targets based on combined cancer and treatment-toxicity risk assessment.
- **Full text available**: No (Thyroid journal)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 2G. Ku et al. -- Meta-Analysis: TSH Suppression and Bone Mineral Density (JCEM, 2021)

- **PMID**: 34302730
- **Citation**: Ku EJ, Yoo WS, Lee EK, et al. J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2021;106(12):3655-3667.
- **DOI**: [10.1210/clinem/dgab539](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab539)
- **Key finding**: Meta-analysis (17 studies, 739 patients, 1085 controls). Postmenopausal women on TSH suppression had SIGNIFICANTLY decreased lumbar spine BMD (-0.03; p<0.05). Premenopausal women showed no decrease. Men showed no association.
- **Supports recommendation**: Evidence that TSH suppression harms bone health in postmenopausal women; supports de-escalation of TSH suppression in this population.
- **Full text available**: No (JCEM, subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

---

## 2H. Kwak et al. -- Meta-Analysis: TSH Suppression and BMD in Postmenopausal Women (BMJ Open, 2021)

- **PMID**: 33986046
- **Citation**: Kwak D, Ha J, Won Y, et al. BMJ Open. 2021;11(5):e043007.
- **DOI**: [10.1136/bmjopen-2020-043007](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjopen-2020-043007)
- **Key finding**: 16 studies (426 patients, 701 controls). Stringent TSH suppression (TSH <0.10) was associated with significantly lower lumbar spine BMD in postmenopausal women (SMD -0.55, 95% CI -0.99 to -0.10). This effect was consistent in studies with >10 years FU. Moderate suppression (TSH 0.10-0.49) showed NO significant difference.
- **Supports recommendation**: Strong evidence that aggressive (not moderate) TSH suppression harms bone; supports moderate over aggressive suppression targets.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC8126273)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 2I. Brancatella & Marcocci -- TSH Suppressive Therapy and Bone Review (2020)

- **PMID**: 32567550
- **Citation**: Brancatella A, Marcocci C. Endocr Connect. 2020;9(7):R158-R172.
- **DOI**: [10.1530/EC-20-0167](https://doi.org/10.1530/EC-20-0167)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive review: chronic TSH suppression is safe in premenopausal women and men, but in postmenopausal women it is associated with decreased BMD, deteriorated bone architecture, and possibly increased fracture risk. Guidelines should value both cancer benefit and treatment-related skeletal risks.
- **Supports recommendation**: Provides nuanced age/sex-specific guidance for TSH suppression and bone health monitoring.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC7424360)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 2J. Parker et al. -- Systematic Review: Long-Term DTC Treatment Morbidity (2017)

- **PMID**: 28553154
- **Citation**: Parker WA, Edafe O, Balasubramanian SP. Pragmat Obs Res. 2017;8:57-67.
- **DOI**: [10.2147/POR.S130510](https://doi.org/10.2147/POR.S130510)
- **Key finding**: Systematic review (66 papers, 4,517 patients): 9/26 studies showed reduced BMD with TSH suppression; 13/23 showed increased bone turnover markers. 4/5 echo studies showed increased LV mass. One study showed higher AF prevalence. 6 studies showed impaired QoL. Effects were more marked in postmenopausal women.
- **Supports recommendation**: Comprehensive evidence base for long-term treatment morbidity supporting de-escalation.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC5440065)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

# 3. SURVEILLANCE ENDPOINTS -- LONG-TERM FOLLOW-UP DATA

## 3A. Seejore et al. -- 756 DTC Patients with Excellent Response: Evidence for Discharge (2021)

- **PMID**: 34185343
- **Citation**: Seejore K, Mulla O, Gerrard GE, et al. Clin Endocrinol. 2021;96(3):395-401.
- **DOI**: [10.1111/cen.14549](https://doi.org/10.1111/cen.14549)
- **Key finding**: 756 DTC patients with excellent response after total thyroidectomy + RAI; median FU 11.2 years. Radiological recurrence in only 15/756 (2.0%); 85% of recurrences (13/15) identifiable within 5 years. All recurrences preceded by raised Tg or TgAb. ATA low/intermediate-risk PTC patients with excellent response at 5 years can reasonably be discharged.
- **Supports recommendation**: Supports shortened follow-up (5 years) for excellent responders; supports stopping routine surveillance/Tg monitoring beyond 5 years in low-risk PTC.
- **Full text available**: No (Wiley, subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 3B. Valerio et al. -- DRS Integrated with ATA Risk (522 excellent responders, 2023)

- **PMID**: 37760625
- **Citation**: Valerio L, Dalmiglio C, Maino F, et al. Cancers. 2023;15(18):4656.
- **DOI**: [10.3390/cancers15184656](https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers15184656)
- **Key finding**: 522 PTC patients with initial excellent response followed median 8 years. Recurrence rate in low-risk patients was only 1.2% (vs 6.9% intermediate-risk). Integration of DRS with ATA initial risk improves prognostic performance (AUC 0.89 vs 0.69 for ATA risk alone).
- **Supports recommendation**: Confirms very low (<1.4%) recurrence rate in low-risk excellent responders; supports early discharge from intensive follow-up.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10526505)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 3C. Yoon et al. -- False-Positive US Rates Exceed Recurrence in PTMC (Medicine, 2016)

- **PMID**: 26735548
- **Citation**: Yoon JH, Lee HS, Kim EK, et al. Medicine. 2016;95(1):e2435.
- **DOI**: [10.1097/MD.0000000000002435](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000002435)
- **Key finding**: 375 PTMC patients after total thyroidectomy + RAI: only 4 recurrences (1.1%) found over follow-up; only 1 had a meaningful metastatic LN (>8mm) found at 7.6 years with biochemical abnormalities. Cumulative false-positive rate was 8.3% by the 8th US and 8.1% by 8-9 years. SHORTER follow-up intervals independently predicted MORE false-positives (HR 0.60).
- **Supports recommendation**: False-positive US rates EXCEED recurrence rates in long-term PTMC follow-up; supports lengthening US intervals and reducing routine imaging in low-risk excellent responders.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC4706268)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

## 3D. Geron et al. -- Long-Term Outcome After Hemithyroidectomy for PTC (2019)

- **PMID**: 30591680
- **Citation**: Geron Y, Benbassat C, Shteinshneider M, et al. Cancers. 2019;11(1):26.
- **DOI**: [10.3390/cancers11010026](https://doi.org/10.3390/cancers11010026)
- **Key finding**: 109 PTC patients after hemithyroidectomy (84% female, 82% microcarcinoma, 95% ATA low-risk). After 8.6-year FU: 3.7% persistent disease at 1 year; 7.5% recurrence in those with initial excellent response. All 109 patients disease-free at last visit. No difference vs total thyroidectomy.
- **Supports recommendation**: Supports lobectomy for low-risk PTC; validates less intensive surgery and reduced need for lifelong Tg monitoring with TSH suppression.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6356549)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

# 4. COMPETING RISK ANALYSES -- NON-THYROID CAUSES OF DEATH DOMINATE

---

## 4A. Tran et al. -- All-Cause and Cause-Specific Mortality in Low-Risk DTC (SEER, 2024)

- **PMID**: 38149602
- **Citation**: Tran TV, Schonfeld SJ, Pasqual E, Haymart MR, Morton LM, Kitahara CM. Thyroid. 2024;34(2):215-224.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2023.0449](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2023.0449)
- **Key finding**: SEER analysis of 51,854 low-risk DTC patients (median FU 8.8 years). Thyroid cancer accounted for only 4.3% of deaths (148/3,467). Most common causes: other malignancies (29.7%) and CVD (26.3%). 20-year thyroid cancer-specific mortality was only 0.6%. Overall mortality was LOWER than expected (SMR 0.69).
- **Supports recommendation**: Definitive competing risk data showing non-thyroid causes dominate mortality in low-risk DTC; directly supports de-escalation of treatment and surveillance intensity.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10884550)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

---

## 4B. Ito et al. -- 30-Year Kuma Hospital Data (Competing Risk Context)

- **PMID**: 37166389
- **Citation**: Miyauchi A, Ito Y, et al. Thyroid. 2023;33(7):817-825.
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2023.0076](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2023.0076)
- **Key finding**: Among 5,646 PTMC patients (AS and IS combined), NONE died of thyroid carcinoma over up to 30 years. Distant metastatic recurrence observed in only 2 patients (both alive). This implicitly demonstrates that competing risks completely dominate in PTMC.
- **Supports recommendation**: Zero disease-specific mortality over 30 years proves that thyroid cancer death risk is negligible compared to other causes in low-risk PTMC patients.
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10354707)
- **Data source**: Abstract / Full text via PMC

---

## 4C. Ito et al. -- Active Surveillance Review (Endocrine-Related Cancer, 2025)

- **PMID**: 41196684
- **Citation**: Ito Y, Miyauchi A, Fujishima M. Endocr Relat Cancer. 2025;32(11).
- **DOI**: [10.1530/ERC-25-0287](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-25-0287)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive 2025 review: NO cases of thyroid carcinoma-related death reported among AS patients worldwide. Young age predicts tumor growth. Patients who undergo conversion surgery after AS have outcomes that do NOT differ from immediate surgery. AS offers better physical QoL.
- **Supports recommendation**: Most current evidence synthesis supporting AS as "excellent initial management strategy" for PTMC.
- **Full text available**: No (Bioscientifica)
- **Data source**: Abstract
