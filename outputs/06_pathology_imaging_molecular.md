## About This Document

This document analyzes changes in diagnostic frameworks -- pathology classification, imaging criteria, and molecular marker integration -- between the 2015 and 2025 ATA DTC guidelines. It addresses the "upstream" reclassification effects that drive many of the apparent treatment changes documented in `05_clinical_implementation.md`. The critical finding is that many of the most impactful treatment changes are not truly "treatment changes" -- they are driven by upstream changes in how patients are classified and risk-stratified. It should be read alongside `04_evidence_methodology.md` for evidence context and `03_comparison_matrix.md` for the full recommendation-level comparison.

## 0. Scope and Methodology Notes

- The 2015 guideline references the **WHO 4th edition** (2004) for thyroid tumor classification and **AJCC 7th edition** for staging.
- The 2025 guideline references the **WHO 5th edition** (2022) for thyroid tumor classification and **AJCC 8th edition** for staging.
- The 2015 guideline uses a **3-tier** risk stratification system (low, intermediate, high).
- The 2025 guideline uses a **4-tier** risk stratification system (low, low-intermediate, intermediate-high, high) with **separate columns for PTC, FTC/IEFVPTC, and OTC**.
- The 2015 guideline covers thyroid nodules AND DTC; the 2025 guideline covers DTC only (starting from a certain or near-certain diagnosis). Nodule-level molecular testing (e.g., for indeterminate FNA) is excluded from the 2025 scope.
- Molecular testing for preoperative nodule classification (ThyroSeq, Afirma, etc.) is within the 2015 scope but outside the 2025 scope. This represents a **scope difference, not a recommendation change**.

## 2. Impact on Decision Thresholds

### 2A. Where Diagnostic Findings Now Change Clinical Decisions Differently

#### 2A.1. Microscopic ETE No Longer Triggers T3 Staging or Automatic Escalation

**2015 Threshold:** Microscopic ETE --> T3 staging --> intermediate risk --> total thyroidectomy generally recommended; RAI often indicated.

**2025 Threshold:** Microscopic ETE --> no T stage change (remains T1 or T2 based on size); low-intermediate risk (10-15%) --> lobectomy may be sufficient for small unifocal tumors; RAI not routinely recommended for low-intermediate risk if excellent response.

**WHY the threshold shifted:** (1) Poor inter-pathologist reproducibility (kappa 0.14) made microscopic ETE an unreliable criterion for escalating treatment; (2) recurrence data (3-9%) positioned microscopic ETE closer to low risk than intermediate risk; (3) AJCC 8th edition removed microscopic ETE from T3 definition to avoid inappropriate upstaging.

**Patient impact:** A 40-year-old with a 1.5cm PTC with microscopic ETE and no LN metastases would have been T3/intermediate risk (2015) --> total thyroidectomy + likely RAI. Now T1b/low-intermediate risk (2025) --> lobectomy may be sufficient; RAI not routinely indicated.

#### 2A.2. The 2mm Nodal Metastasis Threshold

**2015 Threshold:** <=5 micrometastases (<0.2cm) = low risk. Everything else with LN involvement = intermediate risk.

**2025 Threshold:** <=5 LNs all <=2mm = LOW risk. pN1a >2mm OR >5 LNs = LOW-INTERMEDIATE. cN1b <3cm = INTERMEDIATE-HIGH. cN1 >=3cm = HIGH.

**WHY:** The 2015 "intermediate" category was too heterogeneous (4-32% recurrence range within a single tier). Post-2015 data showed 2mm and 5-node thresholds meaningfully separate recurrence trajectories. The 4-tier system resolves this heterogeneity.

**Patient impact:** A patient with 6 small (<2mm) central compartment micrometastases discovered incidentally: 2015 = intermediate risk (>5 LN involved); 2025 = still borderline between low and low-intermediate. The 2025 system provides more nuance; patients with just barely >5 micrometastases are not automatically escalated to the same tier as patients with macroscopic N1b disease.

#### 2A.3. Vascular Invasion in FTC: From Low to Low-Intermediate

**2015 Threshold:** FTC with <4 foci of vascular invasion = low risk (2-3% recurrence).

**2025 Threshold:** FTC with <4 vessels of vascular invasion = LOW-INTERMEDIATE risk (10-15% recurrence).

**WHY:** Accumulating evidence showed any vascular invasion in FTC carries meaningful recurrence risk (Lee et al. HR 29.06, Ito et al. HR 2.5 for <4 vessels). The 2015 "low risk" designation was overly reassuring.

**Patient impact:** An FTC patient with 2 foci of vascular invasion: 2015 = low risk, no RAI needed, minimal surveillance. 2025 = low-intermediate risk, RAI may be considered, closer surveillance warranted. This affects completion thyroidectomy decisions after lobectomy.

#### 2A.4. Comprehensive Genomic Testing Now Mandatory Before Systemic Therapy for RAIR DTC

**2015 Threshold:** RAIR DTC --> start sorafenib or lenvatinib empirically; no molecular testing required.

**2025 Threshold:** RAIR DTC --> comprehensive genomic testing (R61, Strong/Moderate) --> treatment decision based on molecular findings: NTRK fusion --> larotrectinib; RET fusion --> selpercatinib; BRAF V600E --> consider BRAF-directed therapy or lenvatinib; no actionable target --> lenvatinib.

**WHY:** FDA approvals of selective inhibitors (larotrectinib, selpercatinib, pralsetinib, dabrafenib+trametinib) created mutation-specific therapeutic options with better response rates and tolerability than empiric MKI for ~15-25% of RAIR patients.

**Patient impact:** A patient with progressive RAIR PTC harboring an NTRK3 fusion: 2015 = would start lenvatinib or sorafenib (ORR ~12-65% but significant toxicity). 2025 = starts larotrectinib (ORR 86% in DTC, well tolerated). This represents a fundamental change in the treatment paradigm.

#### 2A.5. NIFTP: From Cancer to Non-Cancer

**2015 Threshold:** Noninvasive EFVPTC = cancer (low risk PTC) --> surveillance for recurrence, potential RAI, cancer registry reporting, psychosocial burden of cancer diagnosis.

**2025 Threshold:** NIFTP = not cancer (R1, GPS) --> no completion thyroidectomy, no RAI, no cancer diagnosis.

**WHY:** Multicenter validation (n=363, follow-up to 11.8 years) showed near-zero recurrence; WHO 2017 reclassification; ATA 2017 task force statement.

**Patient impact:** ~2-10% of follicular cell-derived neoplasms reclassified; these patients avoid cancer diagnosis entirely with substantial psychological, insurance, and treatment-burden implications.

#### 2A.6. Positive Anterior vs. Posterior Margin

**2015 Threshold:** Positive margin = intermediate or high risk (no distinction by location).

**2025 Threshold:** Positive anterior margin = LOW risk; positive posterior margin = LOW-INTERMEDIATE risk.

**WHY:** Lang et al. (638 pts) showed 3.6% recurrence for anterior vs 11.6% for posterior margin (HR 22.95); anterior positive margins in thyroidectomy specimens are often artifacts of dissection near strap muscles without clinical significance.

**Patient impact:** A patient with a positive anterior margin only: 2015 = may trigger completion thyroidectomy and/or RAI. 2025 = low risk, no additional treatment needed.

## 3. The "Upstream Shift" Analysis

### 3A. Which Treatment Changes Are Actually Reclassification Changes in Disguise?

**Key Finding:** Many of the most impactful treatment changes in the 2025 guideline are not truly "treatment changes" -- they are driven by upstream changes in how patients are classified and risk-stratified. The treatment algorithm is the same; the patient moves to a different entry point in the algorithm.

#### Reclassification Change 1: AJCC 8th Edition Downstaging (20-30% of patients)

- **Mechanism:** Age cutoff 45-->55; microscopic ETE removed from T3; Level VII reclassified as N1a.
- **Cascade:** Patient is downstaged --> lower ATA risk tier --> less likely to receive total thyroidectomy, RAI, TSH suppression, and intensive surveillance.
- **Example:** A 50-year-old with a 2cm PTC and microscopic ETE and N1a disease:
  - 2015: Age >=45, T3 (microscopic ETE), N1a = Stage III. ATA intermediate risk. Total thyroidectomy + RAI + TSH suppression <0.1.
  - 2025: Age <55, T1b (microscopic ETE irrelevant to T), N1a = Stage I. ATA low-intermediate risk. Lobectomy may be sufficient; RAI may or may not be considered; TSH within normal range.
- **The surgery recommendation didn't change; the patient's classification changed.**

#### Reclassification Change 2: 3-Tier to 4-Tier Risk Split

- **Mechanism:** The 2015 "intermediate" category (3-32% recurrence range) is split into low-intermediate (10-15%) and intermediate-high (16-30%).
- **Cascade:**
  - Patients in the lower half of the former intermediate category (microscopic ETE, <=5 small LN, minor vascular invasion in FTC) are now LOW-INTERMEDIATE --> de-escalated treatment.
  - Patients in the upper half (aggressive histology, vascular invasion in PTC, bilateral multifocality >1cm, cN1b <3cm) are now INTERMEDIATE-HIGH --> treatment unchanged or slightly escalated.
- **This is the most consequential reclassification.** It affects the majority of treatment de-escalation recommendations:
  - RAI: "May be considered" for both low-intermediate and intermediate-high, but the threshold for recommending it is lower for intermediate-high.
  - Completion thyroidectomy: Less compelling for low-intermediate.
  - Surveillance intensity: Can be reduced earlier for low-intermediate.

#### Reclassification Change 3: NIFTP From Cancer to Non-Cancer

- **Mechanism:** ~2-10% of follicular cell-derived neoplasms are no longer called cancer.
- **Cascade:** Patient exits the cancer management pathway entirely. No completion thyroidectomy, no RAI, no cancer-specific surveillance, no cancer diagnosis.
- **Incidence impact:** Contributes to the observed decline in thyroid cancer incidence since 2015.

#### Reclassification Change 4: OTC as Distinct Entity

- **Mechanism:** OTC separated from FTC with its own risk stratification column.
- **Cascade:** OTC patients now assessed differently:
  - RAI is less compelling because OTC is often non-RAI-avid.
  - Completion thyroidectomy to facilitate RAI is less compelling (R16B).
  - Risk stratification uses OTC-specific criteria (widely invasive vs minimally invasive vs encapsulated angioinvasive).
- **The treatment recommendation for RAI didn't change; the expected response changed because the entity was reclassified.**

#### Reclassification Change 5: Microscopic ETE Demotion

- **Mechanism:** Microscopic ETE removed from T3 definition and positioned as low-intermediate risk.
- **Cascade:** Patients with small tumors + microscopic ETE are no longer "T3/intermediate risk"; they may be "T1 or T2/low-intermediate risk."
- **This drives the apparent "de-escalation" in lobectomy recommendations.** The lobectomy threshold didn't actually change in the algorithm; the patients who now qualify for lobectomy were reclassified downward.

## 4. Uncertainty and Limitations

### 4A. Where the Guideline Is Cautious About Molecular Data

1. **BRAF V600E alone is still insufficient for risk stratification.** Despite thousands of patients studied, isolated BRAF V600E has a PPV of only 25% for recurrence and is found in 46-90% of PTCs. The guideline explicitly states it is "not routinely recommended for initial postoperative risk stratification" (R28B). The rationale is clear: BRAF is too common to be discriminating, and its prognostic impact is confounded by association with other adverse features.

2. **Preoperative molecular testing for confirmed DTC is "not recommended routinely" (R10).** The guideline acknowledges that retrospective studies show molecular risk categories correlate with outcomes, but notes critical limitations: (a) studies included cytologically indeterminate nodules not addressed in DTC guidelines; (b) patients were clinically selected (not consecutive); (c) molecular high-risk profiles are uncommon in cT1-2N0M0 tumors; (d) no prospective studies with cost-benefit analysis exist.

3. **Molecular testing for active surveillance is explicitly stated to have "insufficient data" (p. 859).** Despite intuitive appeal, conflicting retrospective results prevent any recommendation.

4. **Postoperative molecular profiling is acknowledged as potentially useful but "not routinely recommended" (R28B).** The guideline notes that molecular risk categories are similar in predictive ability to the histopathologic ATA risk system and "additional studies are needed to determine if routine postoperative genomic analysis improves accuracy."

5. **BRAF and RAS mutations were "neither predictive nor prognostic" in the SELECT (lenvatinib) trial (ref 1197).** This limits the ability to use these markers to select patients for MKI therapy.

6. **Circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) testing is acknowledged but not validated for RAIR DTC.** The guideline notes it "may be limited by relatively sparse tumor cell shedding" and "technical challenges in detecting kinase fusions" (p. 913).

7. **Ki-67 proliferation index is discussed but explicitly stated to need "additional studies" before becoming an essential pathological assessment step (p. 852).**

### 4B. Where the Guideline Acknowledges Insufficient Evidence

1. **Lymph node ratio (LNR):** Extensively discussed but NOT formally incorporated into risk tiers due to methodological concerns: "largely dependent on the absolute number of metastatic and total lymph nodes studied, for which small alterations in either may substantially alter the LNR" (p. 878). Different surgical procedures (prophylactic vs therapeutic dissection) confound interpretation.

2. **Optimal FNA-Tg threshold:** No consensus exists. Studies cite thresholds ranging from 1 to 40 ng/mL. The guideline states "further studies are needed to determine an optimal FNA-Tg threshold" (R7).

3. **Age as independent predictor of recurrence:** Data are conflicting. Several studies support age >=55 as predictor (refs 649-653), but others do not (refs 655,656). The guideline states "additional studies are needed to determine the overall impact of patient age at diagnosis as an independent predictor of long-term outcomes" and does NOT include age in risk of recurrence prediction.

4. **Sex as predictor:** Male sex is associated with advanced stage DTC but "was not an independent prognostic factor for disease-specific survival" or "DTC recurrence" after multivariable analysis (p. 881). NOT included in risk of recurrence.

5. **BMI/obesity:** Associated with adverse pathological features but "not currently part of the ATA Risk of Recurrence predictors" and has "different impacts depending on the cluster or cohort of patients" (p. 881).

6. **Microscopically positive margins:** Conflicting data. Meta-analysis of 7696 pts found no association with local recurrence. But NCDB studies show association with increased death risk. The guideline notes "more studies need to be done in this area, with special attention to the difference between anterior and posterior microscopically positive margins" (p. 879).

7. **Strap muscle invasion:** Variable recurrence rates (5-25.9%) with significant heterogeneity. The guideline notes "further studies are needed to confirm these findings" (p. 879).

8. **Optimal monitoring of NIFTP:** "The optimal approach to postoperative monitoring of these tumors is uncertain" (R1). No comparative data on surveillance strategies exist.

## 5. Summary of Practice-Changing Upstream Shifts

### For the Pathologist
1. Report per WHO 5th edition: classify OTC separately from FTC; diagnose NIFTP and DHGTC as distinct entities; report IEFVPTC as separate category.
2. Report number of invaded vessels (not just "present/absent") for all DTC types.
3. Distinguish anterior from posterior positive margins.
4. Report extranodal extension as a distinct finding.
5. Report LN metastasis size (specifically whether >2mm) and total LN count.

### For the Radiologist/Sonographer
1. Preoperative US should explicitly assess for gross ETE (now relevant for T3b/T4 distinction).
2. FNA-Tg washout is now a formal recommendation for suspicious LN (with caveat about intact thyroid).
3. SPECT/CT with WBS is now a formal recommendation when available.
4. Surveillance US can cease at 5-8 years for low/intermediate risk patients with excellent response.

### For the Molecular Pathologist/Geneticist
1. Comprehensive genomic testing (NGS) is mandatory before systemic therapy for RAIR DTC.
2. RNA-based assays preferred for fusion detection (NTRK, RET, ALK).
3. Germline testing criteria are now structured (Cowden/PHTS, DICER1, FAP/APC).
4. Somatic-to-germline conversion should prompt genetic counseling referral.
5. Routine preoperative and postoperative molecular testing are NOT recommended for most DTC patients.

### For the Treating Clinician
1. The 4-tier risk system replaces the 3-tier system; patients formerly classified as "intermediate" must be re-evaluated as low-intermediate or intermediate-high.
2. AJCC 8th edition staging (age 55 cutoff, no microscopic ETE in T3) substantially downstages many patients.
3. Treatment decisions cascade from upstream classification: the biggest "treatment changes" are actually classification changes.
4. Molecular data, when available, can refine risk but should not override histopathologic assessment.
5. For RAIR DTC, molecular testing before systemic therapy is now the standard of care.

## Cross-Validation Notes

The following notes were identified during cross-validation by the methods-evidence-appraiser and the surgery-rai-risk-stratification-clinical agent.

### 1. Upstream Reclassification Effects Are the Hidden Driver of Treatment De-escalation

The "Upstream Shift" analysis in Section 3 identifies one of the most important findings in this entire project: many of the most impactful treatment changes in the 2025 guideline are not true treatment algorithm changes -- they are reclassification changes that move patients to different entry points in the treatment algorithm. This distinction is clinically critical because it means:

- **The treatment for a given risk category has not necessarily changed.** What changed is which patients qualify for each risk category.
- **Clinicians who focus only on treatment recommendations will miss the real mechanism of change.** The pathologist's reclassification (WHO 5th edition, AJCC 8th edition) and the risk stratification split (3-tier to 4-tier) do more to change patient management than most treatment-level recommendation changes.
- **The 20-30% of patients downstaged by AJCC 8th edition changes** represent the single largest population affected by the 2015-to-2025 evolution. This downstaging occurs before any treatment decision is made.

### 2. The Molecular Profiling Gap

The 2025 guideline presents a tension that deserves explicit highlighting: it presents extensive molecular taxonomy and prognostic data (BRAF-like vs RAS-like, TERT/TP53 co-mutations, genomic landscape table) but concludes that routine molecular testing is "not recommended" for initial surgical extent (R10) and "not recommended routinely" for postoperative risk stratification (R28B). This creates a gap between what is known about molecular biology and what is actionable in clinical practice. The practical consequence is that patients whose tumors happen to have been molecularly profiled (e.g., from preoperative FNA molecular testing for indeterminate nodules) receive more precise risk estimates than patients who were not profiled. This creates an inequality in risk precision that is not addressed by the guideline. As molecular testing becomes cheaper and more accessible, this gap will likely close in future guideline iterations, but for now it represents an acknowledged limitation.

### 3. Additional Notes

- The FTC vascular invasion upgrade from LOW to LOW-INTERMEDIATE risk (Section 1A, row for FTC vascular invasion) is one area where the 2025 guideline actually escalates risk assessment compared to 2015. This is a notable exception to the general de-escalation trend and has practical implications: FTC patients with 1-3 foci of vascular invasion who were previously classified as low-risk and observed may now warrant consideration for completion thyroidectomy and RAI.
- The NIFTP diagnostic criteria tightening (complete absence of papillae, not <1%) deserves emphasis because misclassification has real consequences. A tumor with even <1% papillae that is incorrectly called NIFTP could harbor lymph node metastases. Pathologists should be aware that the 2025 criteria are stricter than the original 2016 proposal.
