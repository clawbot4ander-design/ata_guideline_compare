## Methodology Notes

- **Grading system change:** 2015 uses ACP system (Strong/Weak, High/Moderate/Low quality). 2025 uses modified GRADE (Strong/Conditional, High/Moderate/Low/Very Low certainty) plus Good Practice Statements (GPS). "Weak" (2015) approximately maps to "Conditional" (2025), but this is a methodology change, not always a substantive strength change.
- **Staging system change:** 2015 references AJCC 7th edition; 2025 uses AJCC 8th edition. The age cutoff changed from 45 to 55 years, and microscopic ETE was removed from T3 definition.
- **Risk stratification change:** 2015 uses 3-tier ATA risk (low, intermediate, high). 2025 uses 4-tier ATA risk (low, low-intermediate, intermediate-high, high).
- **Recommendations in the 2015 nodule section (R1-R31) are excluded** from this comparison per the scope crosswalk.

## Priority Topic 1: Extent of Surgery (Lobectomy vs Total Thyroidectomy)

### [1.1] Lobectomy Threshold for Small Tumors (<=2 cm)

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 35C:**
> "If surgery is chosen for patients with thyroid cancer <1 cm without extrathyroidal extension and cN0, the initial surgical procedure should be a thyroid lobectomy unless there are clear indications to remove the contralateral lobe. Thyroid lobectomy alone is sufficient treatment for small, unifocal, intrathyroidal carcinomas in the absence of prior head and neck radiation, familial thyroid carcinoma, or clinically detectable cervical nodal metastases."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [Section B7, p. 32]

**2025 Recommendation 15A:**
> "When resection is performed for patients with thyroid cancer <=2 cm without gross extra-thyroidal extension (cT1) and without metastases (cN0M0), the initial surgical procedure should be a thyroid lobectomy unless there are bilateral cancers or other indications to remove the contralateral lobe."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)* [Section: Initial Management of DTC, p. 862]

**Change Type:** Broadened indication
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Unchanged (Strong/Strong)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Unchanged (Moderate/Moderate certainty)

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline provides extensive rationale. The discussion following R15 states: "Since the 2015 ATA DTC guidelines, additional retrospective studies have reinforced the lack of a disease-specific survival difference between total thyroidectomy and lobectomy in patients with 1-4 cm intrathyroidal PTC without clinically evident lymph node metastases." The guideline cites Adam et al. (2014, NCDB) demonstrating that the survival advantage for total thyroidectomy in 1-4 cm PTC "disappeared when further adjustment was made for additional variables related to complexity and severity of illness." It also cites multiple SEER analyses (Barney et al., Mendelsohn et al.) showing no survival difference, and single-center studies (Matsuzu et al., with 97% cause-specific survival at median 17 years of follow-up after lobectomy).

The 2025 guideline also explicitly notes that lower lobectomy complication rates (even for high-volume surgeons: 7.6% vs 14.5% for total thyroidectomy) favor lobectomy when oncologic outcomes are equivalent.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **Reinterpretation of existing data + new data**: Post-2015 analyses of NCDB and SEER databases demonstrating no survival difference after adjusting for confounders
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Avoiding unnecessary surgical morbidity (hypoparathyroidism, bilateral RLN injury)
- **Outcome re-weighting**: Emphasis shifted from recurrence reduction to survival equivalence and morbidity avoidance
- **Implementation feedback**: Recognition that the prior 1 cm cutoff was arbitrary; lobectomy patients with 1-2 cm tumors had equally excellent outcomes

**Rationale Confidence:** High (guideline provides explicit rationale with citations)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients with unilateral, intrathyroidal DTC between 1 and 2 cm (cT1bN0M0) -- this is a large proportion of newly diagnosed DTC patients.

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Surgeons should now offer lobectomy as the **default** for tumors up to 2 cm (cT1), not just <1 cm
- Previously, 1-2 cm tumors fell into the "either lobectomy or total thyroidectomy" zone (R35B); now lobectomy is the preferred initial procedure
- Fewer patients require lifelong thyroid hormone replacement
- Fewer patients exposed to surgical morbidity of bilateral surgery

**Impact Level:** HIGH (affects the majority of newly diagnosed DTC patients, as most present with T1 disease)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment, reduces morbidity (hypoparathyroidism, RLN injury), reduces cost, reduces need for lifelong thyroid hormone replacement

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R35C, section B7, p. 32] vs [2025 R15A, p. 862]

### [1.2] Lobectomy for Tumors 2-4 cm (cT2)

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 35B:**
> "For patients with thyroid cancer >1 cm and <4 cm without extrathyroidal extension, and without clinical evidence of any lymph node metastases (cN0), the initial surgical procedure can be either a bilateral procedure (near-total or total thyroidectomy) or a unilateral procedure (lobectomy). Thyroid lobectomy alone may be sufficient initial treatment for low-risk papillary and follicular carcinomas; however, the treatment team may choose total thyroidectomy to enable RAI therapy or to enhance follow-up based upon disease features and/or patient preferences."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [Section B7, p. 31-32]

**2025 Recommendation 15B:**
> "For patients with low risk, unilateral thyroid cancer >2 and <=4 cm (cT2N0M0), thyroid lobectomy may be the preferred initial surgical treatment due to significantly lower risk and side effects. However, the patient and treatment team may adopt total thyroidectomy to enable RAI administration and/or enhance follow-up based on disease features, suspicious contralateral nodularity, and/or patient preferences. When thyroid lobectomy is offered as initial treatment, counsel the patient about the possibility of conversion to total thyroidectomy or need for subsequent completion thyroidectomy if higher-risk factors emerge intraoperatively or postoperatively."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low-moderate certainty evidence)* [Section: Initial Management, p. 862]

**Change Type:** Stronger recommendation (lobectomy now "preferred" vs previously neutral "either")
**Recommendation Strength Change:** 2015 Strong (either option) --> 2025 Conditional (lobectomy preferred)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Moderate --> Low-moderate certainty

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline explicitly notes that lobectomy is "preferred" for T2N0M0 tumors "due to significantly lower risk and side effects." The discussion text references the same population-level data showing no survival difference, combined with the consistently lower complication rates for lobectomy versus total thyroidectomy. The guideline also introduces mandatory counseling about potential conversion to total thyroidectomy.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Direct statement that lobectomy is preferred due to lower morbidity
- **Reinterpretation of existing data**: The same NCDB/SEER analyses that 2015 cited as allowing "either" are now interpreted as favoring lobectomy
- **Patient-centered**: New emphasis on shared decision-making and informed consent about conversion possibility
- **New evidence**: Additional post-2015 studies confirming equivalence

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients with unilateral, intrathyroidal DTC 2-4 cm without lymph node metastases

**What Changes in Practice:**
- The framing shifts: lobectomy is now the "preferred" option rather than an equivalent alternative
- Surgeons should present lobectomy as the recommended approach, with total thyroidectomy as an option if the patient/team prefer it for RAI or surveillance reasons
- New requirement to counsel patients about possible need for completion thyroidectomy

**Impact Level:** HIGH

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment, reduces surgical morbidity

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R35B, section B7, p. 31-32] vs [2025 R15B, p. 862]

### [1.3] Total Thyroidectomy for Advanced Disease

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 35A:**
> "For patients with thyroid cancer >4 cm, or with gross extrathyroidal extension (clinical T4), or clinically apparent metastatic disease to nodes (clinical N1) or distant sites (clinical M1), the initial surgical procedure should include a near-total or total thyroidectomy and gross removal of all primary tumor unless there are contraindications to this procedure."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [Section B7, p. 31]

**2025 Recommendation 15C:**
> "For patients with thyroid cancer >4 cm (cT3a), cancer of any size with gross extra-thyroidal extension (cT3b or cT4), or clinically apparent metastatic disease to lymph nodes (cN1) or distant sites (cM1), the initial surgical procedure should include a total thyroidectomy with gross removal of all primary tumor and node dissection unless there are contraindications to this procedure."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)* [p. 862]

**Change Type:** Wording clarification
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Unchanged
**Evidence Quality Change:** Unchanged

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The change reflects updated AJCC 8th edition T staging terminology (cT3a = >4 cm intrathyroidal; cT3b = gross ETE into strap muscles; cT4 = gross ETE beyond strap muscles). Also adds explicit mention of "node dissection" alongside total thyroidectomy.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **Methodology change**: AJCC 8th edition staging nomenclature update
- **Wording clarification**: Addition of "node dissection" makes the surgical scope more explicit

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** Minimal impact -- patients with advanced disease continue to receive total thyroidectomy

**What Changes in Practice:** Updated staging terminology used in communication; explicit inclusion of node dissection in the recommendation statement

**Impact Level:** Low (wording update aligns with existing practice)

**Net Effect:** Reduces ambiguity

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R35A, p. 31] vs [2025 R15C, p. 862]

## Priority Topic 2: Active Surveillance for Low-Risk Disease

### [2.1] Active Surveillance as Formal Management Option

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015:** No formal recommendation for active surveillance. The 2015 guideline mentions active surveillance only in the "Directions for Future Research" section [D3, p. 89], stating: "An active surveillance management approach for very low risk papillary thyroid cancers (e.g., papillary microcarcinomas confined to the thyroid without evidence of metastases) has been proposed as an alternative to immediate surgery."

**2025 Recommendation 11A:**
> "Active surveillance may be offered as an appropriate management option for some patients with cT1aN0M0 PTCs. Shared clinical decision-making between the patient and clinical team regarding risks and benefits of this approach is essential."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)* [p. 859]

**2025 Recommendation 11B:**
> "Ultrasound-guided percutaneous ablation may be considered as an alternative to active surveillance or resection for cT1aN0M0 PTC in selected patients."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)* [p. 859]

**Change Type:** Newly added (from research direction to formal recommendation)
**Recommendation Strength Change:** N/A (new recommendation)
**Evidence Quality Change:** N/A

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline cites a systematic review conducted by a subgroup of the task force, which found "low-certainty evidence that in adults with small, low-risk DTC, active surveillance and immediate resection are associated with similar, low risk of all-cause or cancer-specific mortality, distant metastasis, and recurrence after thyroidectomy." The guideline specifically references a large Japanese cohort (N=1235, mean follow-up 75 months) showing that older patients (>60 years) are significantly less likely to experience tumor progression. The guideline also references prospective data from Korean and US centers supporting the safety of this approach.

For percutaneous ablation, the guideline cites meta-analyses showing 57.6-91% complete tumor disappearance rates for RFA with low complication rates (0.7% major complications).

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Multiple prospective cohort studies from Japan, Korea, and US published since 2015 providing 5-10+ year follow-up data
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Avoiding surgical complications for indolent cancers
- **Patient-centered**: Shared decision-making explicitly mandated
- **New technology**: Percutaneous ablation (RFA, MWA, LA) data matured since 2015

**Rationale Confidence:** High (guideline provides extensive rationale with citations to systematic review)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients with papillary thyroid microcarcinoma (<=1 cm, cT1aN0M0), especially older patients (>60 years) and those with surgical risk factors

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Clinicians now have guideline-level support for offering active surveillance rather than immediate surgery
- Requires establishing an active surveillance protocol (US every 6 months for 1-2 years, then annually)
- Percutaneous ablation (RFA) enters the treatment algorithm as a third option alongside surgery and surveillance
- Multidisciplinary shared decision-making becomes mandatory

**Impact Level:** HIGH (affects a large and growing population of incidentally detected microcarcinomas)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment dramatically for low-risk disease

**Citation Anchors:** [2015: Future Research section D3, p. 89] vs [2025 R11-R14, pp. 859-861]

### [2.2] Active Surveillance Monitoring Protocols

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015:** No recommendation (topic was in future research)

**2025 Recommendation 12:**
> "For patients undergoing active surveillance, neck ultrasound should be used to monitor disease progression."
> *(Good Practice Statement)* [p. 861]

**2025 Recommendation 13:**
> "For patients undergoing active surveillance, routine measurement of serum Tg and/or TgAb levels is not recommended."
> *(Good Practice Statement)* [p. 861]

**2025 Recommendation 14:**
> "In patients undergoing active surveillance, surgical resection is indicated if there is evidence of new biopsy-proven lymph node metastases, growth of the primary tumor by >=3 mm, distant metastases, evidence of extrathyroidal extension, posterior growth, when there is patient anxiety, inability to follow-up, and/or expressed preference for surgery."
> *(Good Practice Statement)* [p. 861]

**Change Type:** Newly added
**Recommendation Strength Change:** N/A
**Evidence Quality Change:** N/A (GPS)

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
R12: Based on prior studies addressing active surveillance, neck ultrasound every 6 months for 1-2 years then annually is the monitoring modality of choice. R13: "Data are lacking on the role of serum Tg levels when the entire thyroid is intact" -- since the intact thyroid produces Tg, serial Tg levels are unlikely to be meaningful. R14: The 3 mm growth threshold is derived from the Japanese active surveillance literature.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Japanese and Korean AS protocols
- **Implementation feedback**: Practical guidance based on AS program experience

**Rationale Confidence:** Moderate (GPS designation reflects limited data but practical consensus)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** All patients entering active surveillance programs

**What Changes in Practice:** Establishes a standardized AS protocol: US every 6 months x 1-2 years, then annually; no Tg monitoring; clear triggers for conversion to surgery

**Impact Level:** Moderate (procedural guidance for a newly formalized approach)

**Net Effect:** Reduces uncertainty for clinicians implementing AS programs

**Citation Anchors:** [2025 R12-R14, p. 861]

## Priority Topic 3: Completion Thyroidectomy

### [3.1] Completion Thyroidectomy Indications

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 38A:**
> "Completion thyroidectomy should be offered to patients for whom a bilateral thyroidectomy would have been recommended had the diagnosis been available before the initial surgery. Therapeutic central lymph node dissection should be included if the lymph nodes are clinically involved. Thyroid lobectomy alone may be sufficient treatment for low-risk papillary and follicular carcinomas."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [Section B9, p. 35]

**2025 Recommendation 16A:**
> "Completion thyroidectomy for cancer following initial lobectomy may be considered to address persistent primary malignancy, facilitate RAI administration, and/or enhance follow-up based upon higher estimated risk of recurrence identified postoperatively, accounting for recurrent laryngeal nerve function."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low-moderate certainty evidence)* [p. 864]

**Change Type:** Weaker recommendation / Narrowed indication
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Strong --> Conditional
**Evidence Quality Change:** Moderate --> Low-moderate

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline provides extensive guidance on when completion thyroidectomy IS and IS NOT warranted. Key changes in reasoning include: (1) "most cytologically indeterminate cases that prove to be malignant on final histology are low-risk cancers adequately treated with lobectomy alone"; (2) microscopic/minimal ETE "no longer promotes tumors to T3 in the AJCC 8th edition staging system and may not require completion thyroidectomy, particularly for T1 and small T2 unifocal tumors"; (3) the guideline introduces detailed criteria including lymph node ratio (>0.3), number of positive nodes (>3-5), and extranodal extension as factors that favor completion thyroidectomy.

The discussion explicitly states: "Postoperative estimated risk of recurrence should be balanced with potential risks of nerve injury and hypoparathyroidism from additional surgery."

**Evidence Driver:**
- **Reinterpretation of existing data**: Recognition that most incidental cancers found after lobectomy are low-risk
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Balancing recurrence risk against surgical morbidity
- **Methodology change**: AJCC 8th edition removing microscopic ETE from T3 definition
- **New evidence**: Data on LNR and extranodal extension as better predictors than simple pN1a status
- **Patient-centered**: RLN function status must be assessed before proceeding

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients diagnosed with DTC after lobectomy for indeterminate cytology -- a common clinical scenario

**What Changes in Practice:**
- The default shifts from "offer completion thyroidectomy" to "consider whether it is necessary"
- Microscopic ETE alone is no longer an automatic trigger for completion thyroidectomy
- RLN function must be confirmed before proceeding
- Decision-making must incorporate specific pathological features (LNR, number of positive nodes, vascular invasion extent)
- Patient preference is explicitly incorporated

**Impact Level:** HIGH (directly changes a very common clinical decision point)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment, reduces secondary surgical morbidity

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R38A, section B9, p. 35] vs [2025 R16A, p. 864-866]

## Priority Topic 4: Central and Lateral Neck Dissection

### [4.1] Prophylactic Central Neck Dissection

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 36B:**
> "Prophylactic central-compartment neck dissection (ipsilateral or bilateral) should be considered in patients with papillary thyroid carcinoma with clinically uninvolved central neck nodes (cN0) who have advanced primary tumors (T3 or T4) or clinically involved lateral neck nodes (cN1b), or if the information will be used to plan further steps in therapy."
> *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)* [Section B8, p. 33-34]

**2015 Recommendation 36C:**
> "Thyroidectomy without prophylactic central neck dissection is appropriate for small (T1 or T2), noninvasive, clinically node-negative papillary thyroid carcinoma."
> *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)* [p. 34]

**2025 Recommendation 19A:**
> "Prophylactic central-compartment lymph node dissection should not be performed for most small, noninvasive, clinically node-negative PTC (cT1-T2, cN0) and for most FTCs."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)* [p. 866-867]

**2025 Recommendation 19B:**
> "Prophylactic central-compartment neck dissection may be considered in patients with PTC and clinically uninvolved lymph nodes (cN0) who have advanced primary tumors (T3 or T4) or for whom the information will be used to plan further steps in therapy, but this approach should be weighed against the risks as they evolve during thyroidectomy."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)* [p. 867]

**Change Type:** Stronger recommendation (against prophylactic CND for small tumors)
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Weak --> Strong (for avoiding pCND in T1-T2 cN0)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Low --> Moderate certainty

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline cites a meta-analysis by Chen et al. (23 studies, 18,376 patients) showing that while pCLND reduced locoregional recurrence (OR 0.65), it significantly increased transient RLN injury (OR 2.03), transient hypocalcemia (OR 2.23), and permanent hypocalcemia (OR 2.22). The guideline also cites Hughes et al. (14 studies) finding that "observation of the central neck is safe and should be recommended for all patients with PTC thought before and during surgery to be free of central compartment metastasis." Wang et al. meta-analysis concluded "it would be necessary to treat 31 patients with pCLND to prevent a single recurrence."

The 2025 guideline explicitly removes the 2015 mention of cN1b lateral disease as a standalone indication for prophylactic CND, instead folding it into the general T3/T4 consideration.

Also notable: 2025 adds the qualifier "this approach should be weighed against the risks as they evolve during thyroidectomy" -- reflecting real-world surgical decision-making.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Multiple meta-analyses published since 2015 quantifying NNT and morbidity
- **Outcome re-weighting**: Morbidity of pCND (hypocalcemia, RLN injury) weighed more heavily against marginal recurrence benefit
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Explicit number-needed-to-treat analysis (31 pCND to prevent 1 recurrence)

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients with T1-T2 cN0 PTC undergoing total thyroidectomy (a large population)

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Surgeons should NOT routinely perform prophylactic CND for T1-T2 cN0 PTC -- this is now a strong recommendation
- For T3-T4 tumors, pCND remains an option but with explicit risk-benefit weighing
- Intraoperative decision-making (evolving findings) is recognized as important
- Fewer patients will be upstaged to pN1a (avoiding cascade of RAI and more intensive follow-up)

**Impact Level:** HIGH (affects surgical planning for most PTC patients)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment, reduces surgical morbidity, reduces unnecessary upstaging

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R36B-C, section B8, p. 33-34] vs [2025 R19A-B, pp. 866-867]

### [4.2] Therapeutic Neck Dissection

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 36A:**
> "Therapeutic central-compartment (level VI) neck dissection for patients with clinically involved central nodes should accompany total thyroidectomy to provide clearance of disease from the central neck."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [p. 33]

**2015 Recommendation 37:**
> "Therapeutic lateral neck compartmental lymph node dissection should be performed for patients with biopsy-proven metastatic lateral cervical lymphadenopathy."
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [p. 34]

**2025 Recommendation 20:**
> "A. Therapeutic central-compartment (Level VI and upper Level VII) neck dissection for patients with clinically involved central nodes (cN1a) should accompany thyroidectomy to clear disease from the central neck. *(Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)*
> B. Therapeutic CLND with dissection of the ipsilateral central compartment lymph nodes is recommended to accompany lateral-compartment neck dissection and thyroidectomy for patients with clinically involved lateral neck lymph nodes (cN1b). *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)*
> C. Therapeutic lateral neck compartmental lymph node dissection, typically including Levels IIa, III, IV and Vb, should be performed as part of initial surgical therapy for patients with biopsy-proven or clinically obvious metastatic lateral compartment cervical lymphadenopathy. *(Strong recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)*"
> [p. 867-868]

**Change Type:** Wording clarification with added specificity
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Unchanged for core recommendations
**Evidence Quality Change:** Unchanged

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline adds more anatomical specificity (Level VII included with central neck, specific lateral neck levels listed). It also adds new guidance on ipsilateral CLND accompanying lateral neck dissection. The addition of lymph node ratio (LNR) data and lymph node yield (LNY) data provides evidence-based metrics for evaluating surgical adequacy.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: LNR and LNY studies providing quantitative metrics
- **Implementation feedback**: More precise anatomical specifications based on surgical experience

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** Patients with clinically positive neck nodes

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Adds Level VII to central neck dissection specification
- Specifies typical levels for lateral neck dissection (IIa, III, IV, Vb)
- Adds ipsilateral CLND when performing lateral neck dissection
- Introduces LNR and LNY as quality metrics for surgical completeness

**Impact Level:** Moderate (refines existing practice rather than changing direction)

**Net Effect:** Reduces variability in surgical technique, improves quality metrics

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R36A, R37, pp. 33-34] vs [2025 R20, pp. 867-868]

## Priority Topic 5: Postoperative Risk Stratification

### [5.1] ATA Risk Stratification System: 3-Tier to 4-Tier

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 48:**
> The 2015 ATA initial risk stratification system uses a **three-tiered** system: Low, Intermediate, and High risk of structural disease recurrence.
> *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)* [Section B19, p. 41; Table 12]

**2025 Recommendation 28A:**
> "The 2025 ATA Risk Stratification System, which evaluates the histopathologic features of the tumor and number of cervical lymph nodes in combination with the AJCC staging system, postoperative imaging, and serum Tg and TgAb testing (if appropriate), is recommended to determine the risk of structural disease persistence/recurrence (locoregionally and/or distantly) and/or survival in patients with DTC."
> *(Strong Recommendation, Moderate certainty evidence)* [p. 874; Table 8]

The 2025 system uses a **four-tiered** system: Low, Low-Intermediate, Intermediate-High, and High risk.

**Change Type:** Replaced framework
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Unchanged (Strong/Strong)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Unchanged (Moderate/Moderate)

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline explicitly states: "The 2015 ATA Risk of Recurrence stratification system has been reported to be a better predictor of structural persistence/recurrence than AJCC/UICC TNM staging." It cites multivariable analyses showing 1.5% recurrence in low-risk, 5.4% in intermediate-risk, and 25% in high-risk patients. The guideline splits the intermediate category because "not all criteria within the intermediate category predict the same risk of recurrence." This is driven by studies showing that patients with microscopic nodal disease (now low-intermediate) have much lower recurrence rates than those with extensive nodal disease or aggressive histology (now intermediate-high).

Key changes in the 2025 system include:
- Microscopic ETE removed from risk factors (per AJCC 8th edition)
- Subdivision of intermediate risk recognizes heterogeneity within this category
- Molecular profiling data incorporated as optional modifier (R28B)
- High-grade follicular cell-derived non-ATC added as a recognized entity

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Post-2015 validation studies of the 2015 ATA risk system showing heterogeneity within the intermediate category
- **Methodology change**: AJCC 8th edition changes (removing microscopic ETE, changing age cutoff)
- **Reinterpretation**: Recognition that microscopic N1a disease has very different outcomes than macroscopic nodal disease

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients previously classified as "ATA intermediate risk" -- they will now be split into two groups with different management implications

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Patients with microscopic N1a disease, small-volume nodal involvement, or intrathyroidal tumors with aggressive histology variants are now "low-intermediate" -- may receive less aggressive RAI and follow-up
- Patients with macroscopic N1a, lateral neck disease, or multiple high-risk features are "intermediate-high" -- continue to receive aggressive management
- Clinicians need to learn and implement the new 4-tier system
- Electronic medical records and tumor boards need updating

**Impact Level:** HIGH (fundamentally changes how the largest heterogeneous group is managed)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment of lower-risk "intermediate" patients; improves precision of risk-adapted therapy

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R48, Table 12, pp. 41-44] vs [2025 R28, Table 8, pp. 874-881]

## Priority Topic 6: RAI Indications

### [6.1] RAI for Low-Risk DTC

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 51 (partial, Table 14):**
> For ATA low-risk DTC: "RAI remnant ablation is not routinely recommended after thyroidectomy for ATA low-risk DTC patients... RAI adjuvant therapy is not routinely recommended after thyroidectomy for ATA low-risk DTC patients." [Table 14, p. 55-56]
> *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence for most sub-recommendations)*

**2025 Recommendation 32A:**
> "Remnant ablation is not recommended routinely after total thyroidectomy for patients with ATA low-risk DTC."
> *(Strong recommendation, High certainty evidence)* [p. 884-885]

**Change Type:** Stronger recommendation
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Weak --> Strong
**Evidence Quality Change:** Low --> High certainty

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline cites the ESTIMABL2 Phase III trial (776 patients with low-risk DTC, randomized to follow-up vs RAI), which "demonstrated noninferiority of a follow-up strategy when compared with systematic adjuvant postoperative administration of RAI in patients with low-risk DTC." Additionally, the HiLo trial (438 patients, median 6.5 years follow-up) showed similar recurrence rates between low-dose (1.1 GBq) and high-dose (3.7 GBq) RAI. Multiple retrospective studies (NTCTCSG, multi-institutional cohort of 1298 patients) found "no benefit of RAI therapy with respect to overall or disease-free survival" in ATA low-risk DTC.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: ESTIMABL2 (Phase III RCT, published post-2015) -- landmark trial
- **New evidence**: HiLo trial (RCT, published post-2015)
- **Reinterpretation**: Accumulation of retrospective data confirming no benefit
- **Methodology change**: Upgraded from Weak/Low to Strong/High based on RCT data now available

**Rationale Confidence:** High (RCT data directly supports this change)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
All patients with ATA low-risk DTC who have undergone total thyroidectomy (a large majority of DTC patients)

**What Changes in Practice:**
- RAI is now **strongly** not recommended for low-risk DTC -- this is a much clearer signal than the 2015 "not routinely recommended"
- If RAI is still used in select cases, dose should be low (1.1-1.85 GBq / 30-50 mCi)
- Nuclear medicine departments may see reduced RAI referrals for low-risk patients
- Patients avoid radiation exposure, isolation requirements, and RAI side effects

**Impact Level:** HIGH

**Net Effect:** Dramatically reduces overtreatment with RAI; avoids side effects (salivary gland damage, secondary malignancy risk)

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R51/Table 14, pp. 55-56] vs [2025 R32A, p. 884-885; Table 10]

### [6.2] RAI for Intermediate-Risk DTC

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 51 (Table 14):**
> For ATA intermediate-risk DTC: "RAI adjuvant therapy is recommended in ATA intermediate-risk level DTC patients... preferentially for those patients with higher risk features."
> *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)* [Table 14]

**2025 Recommendation 32B:**
> "RAI adjuvant therapy may be considered after total thyroidectomy in patients with ATA low-intermediate and intermediate-high risk of recurrent DTC."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)* [p. 884]

**Change Type:** Wording clarification / Framework adaptation
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Weak --> Conditional (approximately equivalent)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Low --> Low certainty (approximately equivalent)

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline notes: "We have divided this category into low-intermediate and high-intermediate, as not all criteria within the intermediate category predict the same risk of recurrence." For intermediate-risk patients, the guideline cites the NCDB analysis (21,870 patients) showing RAI was "associated with a 29% reduction in the risk of death" (HR 0.71). However, it also cites conflicting data from Lamartina et al.'s systematic review where "11 nonrandomized studies suggested benefit, whereas 13 studies did not."

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Large NCDB analysis showing survival benefit for intermediate-risk patients
- **Replaced framework**: 4-tier risk system means the old "intermediate" is now split, allowing more nuanced RAI decisions
- **Reinterpretation**: Low-intermediate patients may not benefit as much as high-intermediate patients

**Rationale Confidence:** Moderate (conflicting data acknowledged)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Patients now classified as "low-intermediate" risk (e.g., microscopic N1a, small-volume nodal disease) may now appropriately avoid RAI

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Clinicians must now consider whether the patient is low-intermediate (consider) vs intermediate-high (stronger consideration for RAI)
- RAI dose range: 1.1-3.7 GBq (30-100 mCi) for the intermediate group

**Impact Level:** Moderate (affects treatment decisions for intermediate-risk subgroup)

**Net Effect:** More nuanced treatment; potential reduction of RAI in low-intermediate patients

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R51/Table 14] vs [2025 R32B, p. 884-886; Table 10]

### [6.3] rhTSH Preparation for RAI

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 54:**
> "Preparation with rhTSH (Thyrogen) can be used as an alternative to thyroid hormone withdrawal for remnant ablation or adjuvant therapy in patients who have undergone near-total or total thyroidectomy."
> *(Strong recommendation, High-quality evidence)* [p. 59]

**2025 Recommendation 34A:**
> "In patients with DTC in whom RAI remnant ablation or adjuvant therapy is planned, preparation with rhTSH stimulation is preferred over thyroid hormone withdrawal."
> *(Strong recommendation, High certainty evidence)* [p. 887]

**Change Type:** Stronger recommendation (from "alternative" to "preferred")
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Unchanged (Strong/Strong)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Unchanged (High/High)

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline cites meta-analyses of 1535 patients across seven clinical trials showing no difference in remnant ablation success between rhTSH and thyroid hormone withdrawal. Critically, "QoL was superior on the day of remnant ablation in the rhTSH group." An additional open-label study (291 patients) confirmed no difference in ablation success rates. The shift from "alternative" to "preferred" reflects the QoL advantage.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Additional RCTs and meta-analyses confirming equivalence
- **Outcome re-weighting**: QoL now weighted as a decisive factor when efficacy is equal
- **Patient-centered**: Avoiding hypothyroid symptoms during preparation

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** All DTC patients receiving RAI for remnant ablation or adjuvant therapy

**What Changes in Practice:**
- rhTSH should be the default preparation method, not just "an option"
- Thyroid hormone withdrawal reserved for patients with distant metastases or special circumstances

**Impact Level:** Moderate (changes process but not indication)

**Net Effect:** Reduces morbidity (hypothyroid symptoms), improves patient experience

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R54, p. 59] vs [2025 R34A, p. 887]

## Priority Topic 7: TSH Suppression Targets

### [7.1] TSH Suppression by Risk Category

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 59:**
> "(A) For high-risk thyroid cancer patients, initial TSH suppression to below 0.1 mU/L is recommended. *(Strong recommendation, Moderate-quality evidence)*
> (B) For intermediate-risk thyroid cancer patients, initial TSH suppression to 0.1-0.5 mU/L is recommended. *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)*
> (C) For low-risk patients who have undergone remnant ablation and have undetectable serum Tg levels, TSH may be maintained at the lower end of the reference range (0.5-2 mU/L). *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)*
> (E) For low-risk patients who have undergone lobectomy, TSH may be maintained in the mid to lower reference range (0.5-2 mU/L). *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)*"
> [Section B44, p. 64-65]

**2025 Recommendation 45:**
> "Individualization of decisions to initiate TSH suppression to below the reference range is recommended based on potential benefits and risks; recognizing that patients with high-risk disease may be more likely to benefit from a TSH in the subnormal range than those with low-risk disease."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)* [p. 895-896; Table 9]

**2025 Recommendation 46:**
> "A. Long-term TSH suppression is not suggested for patients with low- or intermediate-risk disease who have no evidence of biochemical or structural recurrence. *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)*
> B. Risks versus benefits of TSH suppression and TSH goals should be re-evaluated over time. *(Good Practice Statement)*"
> [p. 896-897]

**Change Type:** Weaker recommendation / Broadened de-escalation
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Strong (for high-risk) --> Conditional; specific TSH targets become less prescriptive
**Evidence Quality Change:** Moderate/Low --> Low certainty

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline provides extensive new data challenging TSH suppression benefits:
1. The most recent NTCTCSG study (2015, 3238 patients) "found improved overall survival and disease-free survival across all stages for mean TSH scores in the moderate suppression range (subnormal to normal TSH) and no further improvement for any stage with TSH levels averaging in the undetectable subnormal range."
2. A cohort study of 867 intermediate and high-risk patients "found that TSH suppression showed no association with progression-free survival at 5 years."
3. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis "found that progression-free survival, disease-free survival, and relapse-free survival were not significantly different between TSH suppression and non-suppression groups."
4. For lobectomy patients, a study of 1528 patients "found that during 5.6 years of follow-up, only 1.4% experienced recurrence. There was no difference in recurrences among patients with TSH levels ranging across <0.5, 0.5-1.9, 2.0-4.4, or >4.5 mIU/L."

The guideline explicitly weighs risks: "patients with a TSH <=0.4 mIU/L were at increased risk of atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis" without recurrence benefit in low-/intermediate-risk patients.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: NTCTCSG 2015 study, multiple post-2015 cohort studies
- **Outcome re-weighting**: Cardiovascular and bone risks of TSH suppression now weighed more heavily
- **De-escalation philosophy**: No benefit of aggressive suppression in low-risk disease
- **New evidence**: 2024 meta-analysis finding no benefit of TSH suppression in intermediate-/high-risk DTC

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
- Low-risk DTC patients previously receiving TSH suppression to 0.5-2 mU/L -- can now maintain normal TSH
- Intermediate-risk patients previously suppressed to 0.1-0.5 mU/L -- may be able to maintain normal TSH after achieving excellent response
- Post-lobectomy patients -- most can avoid thyroid hormone supplementation if TSH is in normal range

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Less aggressive TSH suppression for most DTC patients
- Explicit recommendation against long-term TSH suppression for low- and intermediate-risk patients without evidence of recurrence
- Periodic reassessment of TSH goals required
- About 70-80% of lobectomy patients may avoid thyroid hormone supplementation

**Impact Level:** HIGH (affects virtually all DTC patients receiving long-term thyroid hormone therapy)

**Net Effect:** Reduces overtreatment, reduces cardiovascular and bone morbidity from iatrogenic subclinical hyperthyroidism

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R59, section B44, pp. 64-65] vs [2025 R45-R46, pp. 895-897; Table 9]

## Priority Topic 8: Surveillance Protocols

### [8.1] De-Escalation and Discontinuation of Monitoring

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 62-65:** Recommend ongoing Tg monitoring every 6-12 months, periodic US, and continued surveillance. No recommendation for discontinuing monitoring. [Sections C5-C10, pp. 66-70]

**2025 Recommendation 48:**
> "1. For patients with low-risk DTC treated with total thyroidectomy and RAI and a sustained excellent response 5-8 years after initial therapy, routine ultrasound can be discontinued, and patients can be followed subsequently with biochemical markers alone every 1-2 years. *(Conditional recommendation, Low certainty evidence)*
> 2. Patients with low-risk DTC treated with total thyroidectomy and RAI and sustained excellent response for 10-15 years do not require continued routine biochemical monitoring for thyroid cancer and should be considered to have achieved a complete remission. *(Good Practice Statement)*
> 3-4. [Similar recommendations for total thyroidectomy without RAI]
> 5. For patients with low-risk DTC treated with lobectomy, if initial ultrasound is negative, subsequent ultrasounds should be performed every 1-3 years for 5-8 years after initial therapy."
> [pp. 899-901; Table 11]

**Change Type:** Newly added (de-escalation and complete remission concept)
**Recommendation Strength Change:** N/A (new)
**Evidence Quality Change:** N/A

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline introduces the concept of "complete remission" for thyroid cancer for the first time. Key evidence cited includes:
1. A retrospective analysis of 1020 patients (median follow-up 10.4 years): only 13 (1.4%) with sustained excellent response developed structural recurrence, all within 8 years.
2. Multiple studies showing that in low-risk patients with excellent biochemical response, "ultrasound of patients at low risk for recurrence who have had excellent biochemical responses has not been found to identify clinically significant disease, but it can increase the number of interventions undertaken based on false positive findings."
3. A SEER review of 22,000 patients showing "more intense ultrasound imaging and surveillance were associated with increased intervention but with no impact on patient survival."
4. Cost analyses showing "the cost to detect recurrence in low-risk patients is up to seven times higher than that performed for intermediate- and high-risk patients."

The guideline states: "Lifelong biochemical assessments and neck ultrasounds are associated with financial and psychological burden that may not be justified in many patients in view of the extremely low risk of recurrence long after treatment."

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Multiple post-2015 cohort studies with >5-10 year follow-up demonstrating very low recurrence after sustained excellent response
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Reducing unnecessary monitoring burden
- **Patient-centered**: Financial and psychological burden of lifelong monitoring
- **Implementation feedback**: False positive findings from continued US causing unnecessary interventions

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Low-risk DTC patients with sustained excellent response -- the majority of all DTC survivors

**What Changes in Practice:**
- US can be stopped after 5-8 years of sustained excellent response
- Biochemical monitoring (Tg) can be stopped after 10-15 years
- "Complete remission" is a new concept -- patients can be told they are in remission
- Significant reduction in follow-up visits, imaging, and blood tests
- After lobectomy: routine Tg monitoring is not recommended

**Impact Level:** HIGH (affects hundreds of thousands of DTC survivors)

**Net Effect:** Dramatically reduces overmonitoring, reduces cost, reduces psychological burden, reduces false positive interventions

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R62-65, sections C5-C10, pp. 66-70] vs [2025 R48, pp. 899-901; Table 11]

### [8.2] Tg Monitoring After Lobectomy

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 64:**
> "Periodic serum Tg measurements on thyroid hormone therapy should be considered during follow-up of patients with DTC who have undergone less than total thyroidectomy..."
> *(Strong recommendation, Low-quality evidence)* [Section C8, p. 69]

**2025 Recommendation 47D:**
> "Measurement of serum Tg on thyroid hormone in patients after lobectomy during initial follow-up is not recommended routinely."
> *(Conditional recommendation, Very low certainty evidence)* [p. 897]

**2025 Recommendation 48.6:**
> "For patients with low-risk DTC treated with lobectomy, if postoperative Tg is not markedly elevated, additional Tg testing is not recommended routinely."
> *(Good Practice Statement)* [p. 899-900]

**Change Type:** Reversed recommendation
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Strong (consider Tg) --> Conditional (not recommended)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Low --> Very low

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline states that "serum Tg has not been shown to predict recurrence in patients with DTC after thyroid lobectomy" and that "there is no clear role for measuring serum Tg levels preoperatively or postoperatively after lobectomy for thyroid cancer monitoring." With the intact contralateral lobe producing Tg, distinguishing residual/recurrent cancer from normal thyroid tissue is unreliable.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **Reinterpretation of existing data**: Recognition that Tg from intact thyroid tissue confounds monitoring
- **Implementation feedback**: In practice, Tg after lobectomy is not useful for disease detection
- **De-escalation philosophy**: Avoiding tests that generate ambiguous results

**Rationale Confidence:** Moderate (limited evidence, but logical clinical reasoning)

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:**
Growing population of patients undergoing lobectomy for low-risk DTC

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Do NOT routinely order serial Tg levels after lobectomy
- One postoperative Tg may be obtained as baseline, but serial monitoring is discouraged
- Imaging (US) becomes the primary monitoring modality after lobectomy

**Impact Level:** Moderate

**Net Effect:** Reduces unnecessary testing, reduces patient anxiety from ambiguous Tg values

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R64, p. 69] vs [2025 R47D, R48.6, pp. 897-900]

## Priority Topic 10: Shared Decision-Making

### [10.1] Formalization of Shared Decision-Making

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015:** Shared decision-making mentioned in the Aim section and sporadically in recommendation text, but not as a structural principle embedded in individual recommendations.

**2025:** Shared decision-making is explicitly embedded in multiple recommendations:
- R11: "Shared clinical decision-making between the patient and clinical team regarding risks and benefits of this approach is essential."

#### Layer 2: Rationale

The 2025 Introduction states: "Shared decision-making between patients and their treating clinicians is paramount in determining the goals of initial therapy for patients with DTC." This reflects the broader trend in oncology toward patient-centered care and the recognition that many DTC treatment decisions involve trade-offs (lobectomy vs total thyroidectomy, surveillance vs surgery) where patient values are decisive.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **Patient-centered**: Explicit prioritization of patient preferences
- **Consensus shift**: Evolution of medical culture toward shared decision-making
- **Implementation feedback**: Recognition that patients have diverse values regarding treatment intensity

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** All DTC patients, especially those with low- and intermediate-risk disease where multiple management options exist

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Documentation of shared decision-making discussions becomes expected
- Patient preferences must be explored before treatment decisions
- Clinical teams must present multiple options (e.g., lobectomy vs total thyroidectomy, surveillance vs surgery) with balanced risk/benefit information

**Impact Level:** Moderate (cultural/procedural rather than clinical, but affects all patients)

**Net Effect:** Improves patient autonomy, reduces regret

## Priority Topic 11: Genetic Testing and Molecular Profiling

### [11.1] Germline Genetic Testing

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 1:**
> "Screening people with familial follicular cell-derived DTC may lead to an earlier diagnosis of thyroid cancer, but the panel cannot recommend for or against US screening since there is no evidence that this would lead to reduced morbidity or mortality."
> *(No recommendation, Insufficient evidence)* [Section A2, p. 10]

**2025 Recommendation 2-5:**
> R2: "Which patients with DTC should be offered germline genetic testing?" -- Formal criteria provided (patients with syndromic features, criteria-specific tumor types).
> R3: "Should patients with non-syndromic FNMTC receive genetic testing?" -- Conditional recommendation.

**Change Type:** Replaced framework (from no recommendation to detailed testing criteria)
**Recommendation Strength Change:** No recommendation --> Conditional/GPS recommendations
**Evidence Quality Change:** Insufficient --> Low-Very Low

#### Layer 2: Rationale

Advances in understanding of cancer predisposition syndromes (PTEN hamartoma, FAP/APC, Werner, Carney complex, DICER1) and the maturation of somatic tumor testing (NGS panels) have created scenarios where incidental germline findings are detected. The ESMO Precision Medicine Workgroup recommends focused germline testing if somatic mutations are identified. The 2025 guideline creates a systematic framework for when to offer germline testing.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Somatic-to-germline conversion studies, ESMO Precision Medicine guidelines
- **New technology**: Widespread availability of tumor NGS panels

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** DTC patients with syndromic features, familial clustering, or actionable somatic mutations

**What Changes in Practice:**
- Formal criteria for offering germline genetic testing
- Genetic counseling referral pathways established
- Screening recommendations for family members of FNMTC patients

**Impact Level:** Moderate (affects a minority of DTC patients but with high individual impact)

**Net Effect:** Improves detection of hereditary cancer syndromes, enables cascade testing

### [11.2] Preoperative Somatic Molecular Testing

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 52:**
> (Discussed the role of BRAF status in RAI decision-making) "The routine use of molecular markers to guide the extent of initial thyroidectomy in patients with DTC is not currently supported by the evidence."
> [Section B38, p. 58]

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**

## Priority Topic 12: Intraoperative Nerve Monitoring

### [12.1] Intraoperative Neural Monitoring (IONM) for RLN

#### Layer 1: Observation

**2015 Recommendation 42B:**
> "Intraoperative neural stimulation (with or without monitoring) may be considered to facilitate nerve identification and confirm neural function."
> *(Weak recommendation, Low-quality evidence)* [Section B13, p. 36]

**2025 Recommendation 23:**
> "How should the recurrent laryngeal nerves be assessed intraoperatively?" -- Extensive discussion elevating IONM role with evidence from multiple RCTs. The recommendation supports IONM use to facilitate nerve identification and, critically, to prevent bilateral RLN paralysis by deferring contralateral resection when loss of signal occurs.
> [pp. 869-872]

**Change Type:** Stronger recommendation
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Weak --> Stronger emphasis (with GPS and evidence-based support)
**Evidence Quality Change:** Low --> Supported by multiple RCTs

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**
The 2025 guideline provides extensive new RCT evidence:
1. NPV of NIM for confirming intact RLN function: 99-99.9%, specificity 99%
2. One team "eliminated bilateral RLN paralysis completely (0% vs. 2.7%, p=0.03)" after adopting staged approach
3. A dexamethasone protocol showed "significantly greater rate of intraoperative return of signal (18% vs. 88%, p<0.001)"
4. For EBSLN: "significantly greater rate of EBSLN identification with nerve stimulation compared to visual identification alone (96% vs. 77%)"
5. Multiple RCTs showing lower voice impairment scores with NIM-supported stimulation

The International Neural Monitoring Study Group (INMSG) guidelines are cited as supporting staged procedures when loss of signal occurs.

**Evidence Driver:**
- **New evidence**: Multiple RCTs published post-2015 for both RLN and EBSLN protection
- **Implementation feedback**: NIM systems available in ~99% of North American thyroid surgery institutions
- **Patient-centered**: Voice preservation and avoidance of bilateral RLN paralysis

**Rationale Confidence:** High

#### Layer 3: Clinical Impact

**Who Is Most Affected:** All patients undergoing thyroidectomy, especially total thyroidectomy

**What Changes in Practice:**
- IONM use is more strongly supported, especially for total thyroidectomy, cancer surgery, and reoperation
- Staged procedures (deferring contralateral resection) when loss of signal occurs
- EBSLN identification with NIM now supported by RCTs
- Estimated NNT/monitor: 30-70 to prevent one RLN injury

**Impact Level:** Moderate

**Net Effect:** Reduces RLN injury rates, virtually eliminates bilateral RLN paralysis with staged approach

**Citation Anchors:** [2015 R42B, p. 36] vs [2025 R23, pp. 869-872]

## Priority Topic 13: RAI-Refractory DTC and Targeted Therapy

#### Layer 1: Observation

> Discussed kinase inhibitor therapy (sorafenib and lenvatinib) as first-line options for progressive, RAI-refractory DTC.

**2025 Recommendations 61-72:**

**Change Type:** Replaced framework (expanded from 2 drugs to mutation-specific precision oncology)
**Recommendation Strength Change:** Various
**Evidence Quality Change:** Various

#### Layer 2: Rationale

**Guideline's Own Explanation:**

**Impact Level:** HIGH (for the affected population, though this is a smaller subgroup)

## Summary of Key Themes Across All Changes

1. **De-escalation is the dominant theme**: Lobectomy threshold expanded, RAI restricted, TSH suppression relaxed, monitoring de-escalated, prophylactic CND discouraged.

2. **Evidence quality has improved**: Several recommendations upgraded from Weak/Low to Strong/High based on RCT data (particularly for RAI in low-risk disease).

3. **Patient-centered care is formalized**: Shared decision-making, patient advocate on task force, explicit consideration of QoL and morbidity.

4. **Precision medicine emerges**: 4-tier risk stratification, mutation-specific targeted therapy, molecular profiling for risk refinement.

5. **"Complete remission" concept introduced**: For the first time, DTC patients can be told they are in remission and monitoring can be discontinued.

6. **Methodology has changed**: ACP grading to modified GRADE, AJCC 7th to 8th edition, WHO 4th to 5th edition. These systemic changes affect all comparisons.
