# 1. ESTIMABL2 RCT (The Pivotal Trial)

## 1a. ESTIMABL2 — Primary Results (3-Year)
- **PMID**: 35263518
- **Citation**: Leboulleux S et al., N Engl J Med, 2022; 386(10):923-932
- **DOI**: [10.1056/NEJMoa2111953](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2111953)
- **Key finding**: In 730 patients with low-risk DTC (pT1a/pT1b, N0/Nx), thyroidectomy without postoperative RAI was noninferior to thyroidectomy with RAI ablation (1.1 GBq after rhTSH) regarding functional, structural, and biologic events at 3 years.
- **Supports recommendation**: Omission of RAI in low-risk DTC (the single most important new evidence driving the 2025 guideline change on RAI)
- **Full text available**: No (NEJM subscription required; no PMC listing found)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 1b. ESTIMABL2 — 5-Year Follow-Up
- **PMID**: 39586309
- **Citation**: Leboulleux S et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol, 2025; 13(1):38-46
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S2213-8587(24)00276-6](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587(24)00276-6)
- **Key finding**: At 5 years, event-free survival was 94.8% in the RAI group vs 93.2% in the no-RAI group; the unadjusted difference of -1.6% did not exceed the non-inferiority margin of -5%, confirming durability of the primary result.
- **Supports recommendation**: Sustained evidence that RAI omission is safe in low-risk DTC at 5 years
- **Full text available**: No (Lancet subscription required; no PMC listing found)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 1c. ESTIMABL1 — Original Low-Dose vs High-Dose RAI Trial (Companion Trial)
- **PMID**: 22551127
- **Citation**: Schlumberger M, Catargi B, Borget I et al., N Engl J Med, 2012; 366(18):1663-73
- **DOI**: [10.1056/NEJMoa1108586](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1108586)
- **Key finding**: In 752 patients with low-risk DTC, low-dose RAI (1.1 GBq) was equivalent to high-dose RAI (3.7 GBq) for thyroid remnant ablation, and rhTSH preparation was equivalent to thyroid hormone withdrawal. Ablation was complete in 92% overall.
- **Supports recommendation**: Low-dose RAI is sufficient when RAI is used; the precursor trial that established the framework for ESTIMABL2
- **Full text available**: No (NEJM subscription required)
- **Data source**: Abstract

# 2. HiLo RCT

## 2a. HiLo — Original NEJM Publication
- **PMID**: 22551128
- **Citation**: Mallick U, Harmer C, Yap B et al., N Engl J Med, 2012; 366(18):1674-85
- **DOI**: [10.1056/NEJMoa1109589](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1109589)
- **Key finding**: In 438 patients with DTC (T1-T3, N0/N1, M0), low-dose RAI (1.1 GBq) was noninferior to high-dose RAI (3.7 GBq) for ablation success at 6-9 months (85.0% vs 88.9%). Low-dose had fewer adverse events (21% vs 33%, p=0.007) and shorter hospitalizations.
- **Supports recommendation**: Low-dose RAI is effective and safer than high-dose; supports de-escalation of RAI dosing
- **Full text available**: No (NEJM subscription required)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 2b. HiLo — Long-Term Follow-Up (6.5 Years)
- **PMID**: 30501974
- **Citation**: Dehbi HM, Mallick U et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol, 2019; 7(1):44-51
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S2213-8587(18)30306-1](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587(18)30306-1)
- **Key finding**: At median 6.5-year follow-up of 434 patients, recurrence rates were similar between low-dose (1.1 GBq) and high-dose (3.7 GBq) RAI groups: 21 confirmed recurrences total (11 low-dose, 10 high-dose); 7-year cumulative recurrence 5.9% vs 7.3%. Confirms long-term safety of low-dose RAI.
- **Supports recommendation**: Long-term evidence supporting low-dose RAI when ablation is indicated; further supports de-escalation strategy
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC6299255)
- **Data source**: Abstract / PMC full text available

## 2c. HiLo — Trial Protocol
- **PMID**: 18474322
- **Citation**: Mallick U et al., Clin Oncol, 2008; 20(5):325-329
- **DOI**: [10.1016/j.clon.2008.03.010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clon.2008.03.010)
- **Key finding**: Protocol paper describing the HiLo trial design: multicentre randomised factorial trial of high- vs low-dose RAI with/without rhTSH for remnant ablation in DTC.
- **Supports recommendation**: Background/design reference
- **Full text available**: No
- **Data source**: Protocol paper (no abstract available)

# 3. IoN Trial (Iodine or Not)

*Note: The user asked about "IoTA" trial -- the actual trial name is "IoN" (Iodine or Not), the UK successor to HiLo. No trial called "IoTA" was found in thyroid cancer literature.*

## 3a. IoN Trial — Primary Results
- **PMID**: 40543520
- **Citation**: Mallick U, Newbold K, Beasley M et al., Lancet, 2025; 406(10498):52-62
- **DOI**: [10.1016/S0140-6736(25)00629-4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(25)00629-4)
- **Key finding**: In 504 patients with low-risk DTC (pT1-pT3a, N0/Nx/N1a) at 33 UK centres, 5-year recurrence-free rates were 97.9% (no ablation) vs 96.3% (1.1 GBq ablation); absolute risk difference 0.5 percentage points (95% CI -2.2 to 3.2), meeting non-inferiority criterion. RAI ablation can be safely avoided in patients with pT1, pT2, N0/Nx tumours without adverse features.
- **Supports recommendation**: Independent confirmation of ESTIMABL2 findings; together these two RCTs form the evidence base for omitting RAI in low-risk DTC in the 2025 guidelines
- **Full text available**: No (Lancet subscription required; no PMC listing found)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 3b. IoN Trial — Design/Protocol Paper
- **PMID**: (referenced in Clin Oncol 2012 — see web reference)
- **Citation**: Described in: Clin Oncol (R Coll Radiol), 2012 — "Iodine or Not (IoN) for Low-risk Differentiated Thyroid Cancer: The Next UK National Cancer Research Network Randomised Trial following HiLo"
- **DOI**: See [Clinical Oncology article](https://www.clinicaloncologyonline.net/article/S0936-6555(12)00002-7/fulltext)
- **Key finding**: Protocol describing the rationale and design of the IoN trial as the next step after HiLo: comparing RAI ablation vs no ablation (rather than high vs low dose)
- **Supports recommendation**: Background reference
- **Full text available**: Via journal
- **Data source**: Protocol

# 4. RAI Side Effects Data

## 4a. Secondary Malignancy — Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (Sawka/Yu)
- **PMID**: 30370820
- **Citation**: Yu CY, Saeed O, Goldberg AS et al., Thyroid, 2018; 28(12):1536-1543 (published online 2018)
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2018.0244](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2018.0244)
- **Key finding**: Pooled risk ratio for any subsequent malignant neoplasm (SMN) after RAI was 1.16 (95% CI 0.97-1.39) from 6 studies with >= 11,241 thyroid cancer patients. Secondary analysis showed increased leukemia risk in RAI-treated patients. Updated the evidence base on RAI-associated second cancer risk.
- **Supports recommendation**: Strengthens the harm side of the RAI risk-benefit calculus, supporting more conservative RAI use
- **Full text available**: No (Thyroid journal subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 4b. Secondary Malignancy — Linear Dose-Response (Kim et al., Korean Cohort)
- **PMID**: 36821433
- **Citation**: Kim KJ, Kim KJ, Choi J et al., J Natl Cancer Inst, 2023; 115(6):695-702
- **DOI**: [10.1093/jnci/djad040](https://doi.org/10.1093/jnci/djad040)
- **Key finding**: In 217,777 Korean thyroid cancer patients (100,448 received RAI), SPM risk increased linearly in a dose-dependent manner (HR 1.08, 95% CI 1.04-1.13 after adjustment). Risk became significant from 3.7 GBq cumulative dose. Myeloid leukemia, salivary gland, lung, uterine, and prostate cancers were most significantly elevated.
- **Supports recommendation**: Dose-dependent second malignancy risk supports minimizing RAI exposure (both omission in low-risk and low-dose when used)
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC10248848)
- **Data source**: Abstract / PMC full text available

## 4c. Secondary Malignancy — Critical Review (Reinecke/Mueller et al.)
- **PMID**: 35320386
- **Citation**: Reinecke MJ, Ahlers G, Burchert A et al., Eur J Nucl Med Mol Imaging, 2022; 49(9):3247-3256
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00259-022-05762-4](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00259-022-05762-4)
- **Key finding**: Critical review of 10 eligible studies found relative effects (RR/HR/OR) for SPM after RAI ranged from 1.14-1.84 (mostly non-significant); for secondary hematologic malignancies, effects ranged 1.30-2.50 (2/3 significant). Quality of evidence was "very low" to "low" per GRADE. An excess risk for SPM cannot be excluded but is expected to be small.
- **Supports recommendation**: Contextualizes the secondary malignancy risk; supports cautious interpretation but does not dismiss the concern
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC9250458)
- **Data source**: Abstract / PMC full text available

## 4d. Secondary Malignancy — Breast Cancer Risk Meta-Analysis
- **PMID**: 34820683
- **Citation**: (Authors from Eur J Nucl Med Mol Imaging, 2021)
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s00259-021-05625-4](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00259-021-05625-4)
- **Key finding**: Systematic review and meta-analysis investigating the risk of primary breast cancer in patients with DTC undergoing RAI therapy. Addresses the specific concern about breast cancer as a second primary after RAI.
- **Supports recommendation**: Contributes to harm assessment of RAI
- **Full text available**: Check journal access
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 4e. Cancer Risk After RAI for Hyperthyroidism — JAMA Meta-Analysis
- **PMID**: 34533571
- **Citation**: Shim SR, Kitahara CM, Cha ES et al., JAMA Netw Open, 2021; 4(9):e2125072
- **DOI**: [10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.25072](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.25072)
- **Key finding**: In 479,452 participants across 12 studies, overall pooled cancer incidence ratio was 1.02 (95% CI 0.95-1.09) for RAI vs non-RAI. However, a linear dose-response association with solid cancer mortality was observed (per 370 MBq: HR 1.14, p=0.01 for solid cancer; HR 1.35, p=0.03 for breast cancer).
- **Supports recommendation**: While this is for hyperthyroidism (not thyroid cancer), it provides additional dose-response data on RAI-associated cancer risk
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC8449277 - JAMA Network Open is open access)
- **Data source**: Abstract / PMC full text available

## 4f. Sialadenitis — Prevalence and Predictors
- **PMID**: 33163074
- **Citation**: Riachy R, Ghazal N, Haidar MB et al., Int J Endocrinol, 2020; 2020:8649794
- **DOI**: [10.1155/2020/8649794](https://doi.org/10.1155/2020/8649794)
- **Key finding**: In 174 DTC patients receiving RAI, early sialadenitis prevalence was 20.1% (95% CI 15-27%). Positive whole-body scan (OR 3.99) and higher iodine exposure in the neck were associated with sialadenitis.
- **Supports recommendation**: Documents significant incidence of salivary gland toxicity from RAI, supporting harm assessment
- **Full text available**: Yes (PMC7604595)
- **Data source**: Abstract / PMC full text available

## 4g. Sialadenitis — Comprehensive Review
- **PMID**: 34143403
- **Citation**: Adramerinas M, Andreadis D, Vahtsevanos K et al., Hormones (Athens), 2021; 20(4):669-678
- **DOI**: [10.1007/s42000-021-00304-3](https://doi.org/10.1007/s42000-021-00304-3)
- **Key finding**: Review of RAI-induced sialadenitis covering mechanism (beta radiation damage to striated duct and stem cells), frequency, prevention, and management. RAI-induced sialadenitis has major impact on quality of life, and given the good prognosis of DTC, minimizing this complication is a priority.
- **Supports recommendation**: Qualitative evidence supporting the harm side of RAI risk-benefit analysis
- **Full text available**: No (subscription required)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 4h. RAI Effects on Female Fertility — Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
- **PMID**: 33012254
- **Citation**: Piek MW, Postma EL, van Leeuwaarde R et al., Thyroid, 2021; 31(4):658-668
- **DOI**: [10.1089/thy.2020.0356](https://doi.org/10.1089/thy.2020.0356)
- **Key finding**: In 22 included studies, menstrual irregularities occurred in 12-31% of patients in the first year after RAI; amenorrhea in 8-16%. RAI-treated women had menopause ~1.5 years earlier (49.5 vs 51 years, p<0.001). AMH levels decreased significantly after RAI. However, pregnancy rates were not decreased.
- **Supports recommendation**: Documents fertility-related concerns from RAI, particularly relevant for younger thyroid cancer patients; supports harm assessment in risk-benefit calculus
- **Full text available**: No (Thyroid journal subscription)
- **Data source**: Abstract

## 4i. Comprehensive Review of RAI Adverse Effects
- **PMID**: 24751702
- **Citation**: Adverse effects of radioactive iodine-131 treatment for differentiated thyroid carcinoma. Nucl Med Commun, 2014; 35(8):808-817 (Published online 2014)
- **DOI**: [10.1097/MNM.0000000000000132](https://doi.org/10.1097/MNM.0000000000000132)
- **Key finding**: Comprehensive review of early complications (GI symptoms, radiation thyroiditis, sialadenitis/xerostomia, bone marrow suppression, gonadal damage, dry eye, nasolacrimal duct obstruction) and late complications (secondary malignancies, pulmonary fibrosis in high-dose) of RAI therapy.
- **Supports recommendation**: Provides the comprehensive side-effect profile that informs RAI risk-benefit discussions
- **Full text available**: No (subscription required)
- **Data source**: Abstract

# Notes

1. **"IoTA" vs "IoN"**: The user asked about an "IoTA trial" -- no trial by that exact name was found. The relevant trial is the **IoN (Iodine or Not)** trial, led by Mallick et al. in the UK. It is possible "IoTA" was a confusion with "ESTIMABL" or a variant name; however, the IoN trial is the correct reference for a UK-based RAI-omission RCT in thyroid cancer.

2. **Combined impact**: The ESTIMABL2 and IoN trials together provide Level 1 evidence from two independent RCTs (France and UK) supporting omission of RAI in low-risk DTC -- this is the strongest new evidence driving the 2025 ATA guideline changes on RAI.

3. **All references retrieved from PubMed** (National Library of Medicine). DOIs link to original publisher sources.
